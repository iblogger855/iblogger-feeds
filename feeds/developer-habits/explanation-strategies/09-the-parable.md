# Strategy 9: The Parable (Narrative Analogy)

**Audience:** Anyone — but especially powerful for explaining cognitive biases, ethical dilemmas, and psychological phenomena.

**Goal:** Use a complete human story — historical, cultural, or moral — to make an abstract concept emotionally real, then explicitly bridge from the story back to the theory.

**Key difference from [Strategy 7 — The Storyteller](07-storyteller-narrative-arc.md):**

| The Storyteller | The Parable |
| :--- | :--- |
| You *build* a narrative around a technical problem | You *find or retell* a human story that embodies the concept |
| Hero → villain → conflict → resolution | Story first — audience sits with "why?" — then theory revealed |
| Technical domain (systems, patterns, bugs) | Human domain (psychology, ethics, philosophy, behavior) |
| Debrief is optional | **Debrief is mandatory** |

---

## The Formula

```
1. Find or craft a story where the characters live the concept — don't name it yet
2. Tell the story completely. Let emotion and empathy do the work.
3. Pause. Ask: "Why do you think this happened?"
4. Let the audience grapple with the question before you answer
5. Bridge: "In psychology / philosophy / economics, this is called X because..."
6. Generalize: show where the concept appears in everyday life
7. DEBRIEFING IS MANDATORY — never leave the audience inside the story alone
```

**Why the brain responds:** When we hear a story, the brain releases Dopamine (curiosity — "what happens next?") and Oxytocin (empathy — "I feel for this character"). By the time you name the theory, the audience has already *experienced* it emotionally. The theory lands as recognition, not information.

### The Bridge (Theory Debrief)

> *"Why did the general refuse? Why did the king burn the mountain?"*

នៅក្នុងទ្រឹស្តីចិត្តវិទ្យាមួយហៅថា **Projection Effect** — ការយកគំនិតខ្លួនឯងទៅដាក់លើអ្នកដទៃ។

The king assumed that what *he* valued — rank, wealth, public recognition — was what *everyone* would want. He could not conceive that the general valued something entirely different: peace, time with his mother, the dignity of being left alone.

So he used the only tool that made sense in his own value system: the threat of death. It never occurred to him it wouldn't work.

**The principle:**
> Don't impose your standard of "good" on others. Don't assume that what you want is what they want. True respect means allowing people to live by their own values — not forcing them to walk your path.

This appears in:
- **Management:** Promoting a quiet engineer into a leadership role they never wanted
- **Parenting:** Pushing a child toward a career that satisfies the parent's ambition
- **Product design:** Building features the team finds impressive but users never asked for
- **Relationships:** Expressing love in a love language the other person doesn't speak

## When to Use It

Explaining cognitive biases, ethical dilemmas, philosophical concepts, and human behavioral patterns to any audience — from executives to students to engineers encountering soft-skills topics for the first time.

**Signature move:** Tell the story completely. Then pause. Ask: *"Why do you think this happened?"* Only after the audience grapples with the question do you reveal the concept name.

---

## Warning: Always Debrief

Without the debrief, the audience takes the wrong lesson. From the prince and general story, a reader without a debrief might conclude: *"the king was simply evil"* — missing the cognitive bias entirely.

The debrief is what separates a parable from mere entertainment. It is non-negotiable.
