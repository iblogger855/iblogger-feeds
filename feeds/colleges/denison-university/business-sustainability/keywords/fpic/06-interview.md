# FPIC / Free, Prior and Informed Consent — Journalist Interview
# ការទទួលបានការយល់ព្រមជាមុនដោយសេរី — បទសម្ភាសន៍សារព័ត៌មាន

*Author: ichamrong | Date: 2026-06-01*

---

*Interview between Ratana Chan of the Phnom Penh Economic Tribune and Dr. Sokunthea Pich, an anthropologist studying land, business, and Indigenous rights.*

---

**Ratana Chan:** Dr. Sokunthea, FPIC sounds like legal jargon. What does it actually mean for ordinary people?

**Dr. Sokunthea Pich:** It means that before a company or government does something major on land a community lives on and depends on, the community has to genuinely agree — and is allowed to refuse. The acronym unpacks the conditions for that agreement to be real: Free, Prior, and Informed Consent. Each word blocks a way that powerful actors fake permission. Free: no coercion or bribery. Prior: asked before the project starts, with time to deliberate. Informed: given the full, honest truth in their own language. And Consent: a real yes-or-no, with no meaning unless no is also possible.

**Ratana:** Where does this principle come from?

**Dr. Sokunthea Pich:** Its strongest expression is the UN Declaration on the Rights of Indigenous Peoples, adopted in 2007. But the deeper roots are anthropological — decades of documenting how development projects dispossessed Indigenous communities who held land by custom rather than by deed. The key insight is that customary rights are real rights. A community that has governed a forest for centuries has a legitimate claim, even with no title in a registry. FPIC takes that insight and makes it a procedural requirement.

**Ratana:** Many laws require companies to "consult" communities. Isn't that the same thing?

**Dr. Sokunthea Pich:** No, and the difference is everything. Consultation means the company listens and then does what it intended. FPIC means the company needs the community's actual agreement and must respect a refusal. The whole battleground of land conflicts sits in that gap. Companies love "consultation" because it's a box to tick; communities need "consent" because only a real no protects them. When you hear a project boasting it "consulted stakeholders," always ask: could the community have said no, and would that no have stopped the project?

**Ratana:** Why should a business invest in genuine FPIC rather than just getting its government permit?

**Dr. Sokunthea Pich:** Because the permit is the cheap part and the consent is the durable part. A project can hold every legal license and still be destroyed by the community it ignored — protests, lawsuits, road blockades, international media, and crucially, financiers pulling out. Lenders increasingly require FPIC through the IFC Performance Standards and the Equator Principles. We call the community's genuine acceptance the "social license to operate." Without it, the legal license is a paper roof in a storm. Real FPIC is risk management, not charity.

**Ratana:** How does this play out in Cambodia specifically?

**Dr. Sokunthea Pich:** Most sharply in the northeast — Ratanakiri and Mondulkiri — where Indigenous Bunong, Tampuon, and other groups hold land communally, including spirit forests and burial grounds central to their identity. When economic land concessions for rubber and agribusiness overlapped these lands, communities that were never truly consulted lost forests their entire way of life depended on. Cambodia does have a mechanism for communal land titling for Indigenous communities, and international buyers increasingly demand FPIC up the supply chain. The challenge is making consent real on the ground, not a thumbprint extracted at a rushed meeting.

**Ratana:** Is there a tension between FPIC and Cambodia's development goals?

**Dr. Sokunthea Pich:** It's framed as a tension, but I'd call it a misunderstanding. Development that bulldozes consent breeds conflict, which is itself expensive and slow. Projects built on genuine consent are more stable, attract better financing, and last longer. FPIC doesn't block development; it filters out the projects that would have blown up anyway and strengthens the ones worth doing. The countries and companies that learn this build more, not less.

**Ratana:** Final question. One thing for a young Cambodian entering business or policy to remember?

**Dr. Sokunthea Pich:** That a community is not an obstacle to clear — it's a rights-holder to ask. Come to ask, not to take. The projects that ask honestly are the ones still standing a decade later.

**Ratana:** Thank you, Dr. Sokunthea.
