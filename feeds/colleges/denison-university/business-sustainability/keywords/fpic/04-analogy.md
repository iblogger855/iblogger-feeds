# FPIC / Free, Prior and Informed Consent — Analogy Bridge
# ការទទួលបានការយល់ព្រមជាមុនដោយសេរី — ស្ពានប្រៀបធៀប

*Author: ichamrong | Date: 2026-06-01*

---

## Analogy 1: A Guest Entering Your Home / ភ្ញៀវចូលផ្ទះអ្នក

**The Story:**
A respectful guest knocks, waits, explains why they've come, and enters only when you open the door. A trespasser climbs through the window while you're out and is rearranging your furniture by the time you return — waving a note from a stranger who claimed your house was theirs to lend. The difference isn't whether someone enters; it's whether *you* agreed. FPIC is the knock at the door for projects on a community's land: the developer must wait for the community to truly open it, not climb in through a permit issued by someone far away.

**The Mapping:**

| Analogy Element | FPIC Element | ខ្មែរ |
|---|---|---|
| Knocking and waiting | Seeking prior consent | ស្វែងរកការយល់ព្រមជាមុន |
| You choose to open | Community's right to say yes/no | សិទ្ធិសហគមន៍បាទ/ទេ |
| Note from a stranger | Concession granted by the state | សម្បទានពីរដ្ឋ |
| Climbing the window | Proceeding without consent | បន្តដោយគ្មានការយល់ព្រម |

**Where the Analogy Breaks Down:**
A home has one clear owner; a community decides collectively through its own institutions, so "opening the door" is a shared, deliberative act, not one person's choice.

## Analogy 2: Signing a Contract You Can't Read / ការចុះកិច្ចសន្យាដែលអ្នកអានមិនបាន

**The Story:**
Someone hands you a thick contract in a language you don't speak, points to a line, and says "sign here, it's good for you" — while a large man stands behind you and the deadline is "now." Even if you sign, no one would call that a fair agreement: you weren't informed (you couldn't read it), you weren't free (the man, the rush), and it wasn't truly prior (no time to think). FPIC is the demand that the contract be explained fully, in your language, with time, and with no one looming — and that you may decline.

**The Mapping:**

| Analogy Element | FPIC Element | ខ្មែរ |
|---|---|---|
| Contract in your language, explained | Informed | មានព័ត៌មាន |
| No looming man, no rush | Free | សេរី |
| Time to read before signing | Prior | ជាមុន |
| You may decline | Consent (right to refuse) | ការយល់ព្រម (សិទ្ធិបដិសេធ) |

**Where the Analogy Breaks Down:**
A contract binds individuals; FPIC protects a whole people's collective heritage — forests, sacred sites, a way of life — which can't simply be signed away by one signature.

---

## Analogy 3: Asking Before Borrowing a Neighbour's Ox / សុំមុនពេលខ្ចីគោអ្នកជិតខាង

**The Story:**
In a village, you don't just take a neighbour's ox because the commune office gave you a slip of paper. You go to the neighbour, before you need it, explain what you'll do with it and for how long, and accept that they might say no — the ox is theirs, and their livelihood depends on it. Custom, not paperwork, governs this. FPIC formalises that village courtesy into a right: ask the rightful users first, honestly, and respect their refusal — because the land, like the ox, is their living.

**The Mapping:**

| Analogy Element | FPIC Element | ខ្មែរ |
|---|---|---|
| The neighbour's ownership by custom | Customary land rights | សិទ្ធិដីតាមទំនៀម |
| Asking before you need it | Prior consent | ការយល់ព្រមជាមុន |
| Explaining honestly | Informed | មានព័ត៌មាន |
| They may refuse | Right to say no | សិទ្ធិបដិសេធ |

**Where the Analogy Breaks Down:**
Borrowing an ox is temporary and reversible; clearing a forest or flooding a valley is permanent, which is why FPIC for land projects carries far higher stakes than a neighbourly loan.

---

## Core Insight Across All Three Analogies / ការយល់ដឹងស្នូល

All three show the same principle: **a piece of paper from an outside authority is not the same as the genuine agreement of the people whose lives and livelihoods are at stake**. FPIC insists on the knock, the honest explanation, the time to decide, and the real right to refuse — converting people from obstacles to be cleared into rights-holders to be asked.
