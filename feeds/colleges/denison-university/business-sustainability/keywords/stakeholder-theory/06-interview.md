# Stakeholder Theory — Journalist Interview
# ទ្រឹស្ដីភាគីពាក់ព័ន្ធ — បទសម្ភាសន៍សារព័ត៌មាន

*Author: ichamrong | Date: 2026-05-29*

---

*Interview between Ratana Chan of the Phnom Penh Economic Tribune and Dr. Virak Lim, corporate governance specialist and visiting lecturer at the Royal University of Law and Economics.*

---

**Ratana Chan:** Dr. Virak, stakeholder theory sounds academic. Is it actually useful for how businesses in Cambodia operate?

**Dr. Virak Lim:** It is more useful in Cambodia than almost anywhere else, because of the specific structure of Cambodian economic development. Many large investments — in mining, agribusiness, garments, tourism — affect communities that have no formal legal standing to stop or modify a project. Stakeholder theory gives those communities a framework for asserting claims, and gives businesses a framework for understanding why ignoring those communities is dangerous.

**Ratana:** The theory is attributed to R. Edward Freeman. What is his core argument?

**Dr. Virak:** Freeman's core argument is that the traditional model of the firm — maximize returns to shareholders — is both descriptively wrong and strategically dangerous. It is descriptively wrong because firms actually depend on employees, suppliers, communities, and governments, not just shareholders, to create value. It is strategically dangerous because ignoring those groups accumulates what he calls "hidden liabilities" — costs that are not on the balance sheet but that eventually materialize as conflicts, regulatory sanctions, or reputational collapse.

**Ratana:** Can you give me a Cambodian example where hidden liabilities materialized?

**Dr. Virak:** The Boeung Kak Lake case is the clearest. A developer acquired rights to fill the lake in 2007. The communities living on the lakeshore — around 4,000 families — were not treated as stakeholders in any meaningful sense. The consultations, if they happened at all, were perfunctory. Families were displaced, often with inadequate compensation. What followed was a decade of protests, international human rights attention, World Bank sanctions against Cambodia over related projects, and reputational damage that affected the country's broader investment environment. The "cost" of those 4,000 families had not been accounted for in anyone's project appraisal.

**Ratana:** But those families had no equity in the project. Why should their interests have mattered to the developer?

**Dr. Virak:** Because they had power — the power to organize, to attract NGO support, to generate international advocacy, and to impose reputational costs that eventually affected the developer's other business interests. In Freeman's framework, a stakeholder is anyone who can affect or is affected by the organization. Equity ownership is not the criterion. Power, legitimacy, and urgency are.

**Ratana:** What about the Seila Program? I understand it applied something like stakeholder mapping at the commune level.

**Dr. Virak:** The Seila Program is actually a beautiful Cambodian example of stakeholder theory applied to governance rather than business. Before designing local service delivery interventions, the program systematically mapped who had claims on commune decisions — village chiefs, elected councils, district authorities, women's groups, ethnic minorities. It then designed consultation mechanisms proportional to those claims. The program is credited with reducing local conflict and improving implementation success. That is the practical payoff of stakeholder thinking.

**Ratana:** Is there a tension between stakeholder theory and Cambodian corporate culture, which tends toward hierarchical decision-making?

**Dr. Virak:** Yes, and it is worth being honest about this. Hierarchical culture means that community consultation can easily become a performance — a meeting that looks like engagement but where decisions are already made. The test is whether the consultation actually changes anything. If a company meets with a community and then does exactly what it planned anyway, that is not stakeholder management. That is stakeholder theater.

**Ratana:** How do you distinguish stakeholder management from stakeholder theater in practice?

**Dr. Virak:** Look at three things. First, did community input change the project design, timeline, or compensation structure in any meaningful way? Second, is there a mechanism for ongoing dialogue — not just a one-time consultation? Third, are there grievance mechanisms that actually work — where community complaints can be escalated and resolved without community members facing retaliation? In Cambodia, all three of these are often absent, even in projects with elaborate consultation documentation.

**Ratana:** What about garment factories, which are a major employer in Cambodia? How should they apply stakeholder theory?

**Dr. Virak:** Garment factories have a very dense stakeholder map. Workers, their families, local health systems, the communities around industrial zones, the brands that commission the garments, international labor rights organizations, and the Cambodian government are all stakeholders with different levels of power and different claims. The factories that do this well are the ones that have genuinely integrated worker voice mechanisms — not just a union checkbox, but actual structures where workers' safety and wage concerns reach management before they become strikes or international press stories.

**Ratana:** Final question: is stakeholder theory optimistic about human nature, or is it really just enlightened self-interest?

**Dr. Virak:** Freeman himself is explicit that stakeholder theory does not require altruism. It is compatible with firms pursuing profit — but profit correctly understood as something that can only be sustained over the long run if the firm maintains the cooperation of all the groups it depends on. Whether that is "enlightened self-interest" or genuine ethics, the operational implication is the same: map your stakeholders, understand their claims, manage the relationships proactively. The communities along the Mekong, the workers in Kampong Speu, the fishing families in Stung Treng — they were always stakeholders. The question is whether businesses will recognize that before or after the crisis.

**Ratana:** Thank you, Dr. Virak.
