# Stakeholder Theory — Feynman Explanation
# ទ្រឹស្ដីភាគីពាក់ព័ន្ធ — ការពន្យល់បែប Feynman

*Author: ichamrong | Date: 2026-05-29*

---

Imagine your family runs a small rice noodle shop (ហាងខ្ចីគីតូ) near Phsar Kandal in Phnom Penh. You wake up at four in the morning to make fresh noodles. By seven, the shop is full.

Now, who does your shop depend on to keep working?

Let me count with you:

- **Your suppliers** — the man who delivers rice flour from Kampong Speu every morning. If he stops coming, you have no noodles.
- **Your workers** — your niece who takes orders, your cousin who washes dishes. If they quit, you cannot serve customers.
- **Your customers** — the office workers, the tuk-tuk drivers, the students. No customers, no income.
- **Your neighbors** — the shop next door, the building owner, the people on your street. If you pour cooking oil into the drain and the neighbors complain, the municipality closes you.
- **The municipality itself** — they issue your license. They can revoke it.
- **The bank** — you borrowed money to buy the gas stoves. They need you to repay.

Now here is the question: **who matters to your shop?**

If you say "only my profit matters," you will ignore the supplier, pay the workers badly, pollute the drain, and never thank the customers. Within six months, your shop is closed.

**Stakeholder theory (ទ្រឹស្ដីភាគីពាក់ព័ន្ធ)** says: every person or group that can affect your business — or that your business can affect — is a *stakeholder*. They have a stake in what you do.

Managing your shop well means paying attention to ALL of them — not equally, but enough that none of them becomes a problem.

The rice flour supplier? Pay on time. He will deliver in bad weather because you are reliable.

The workers? Pay fairly. They come early, stay late, and treat your customers like family.

The neighbors? Keep the drain clean. They will recommend your shop to their visitors.

The customers? Make good broth. They come back every morning and bring their colleagues.

This is not charity. This is strategy. You are building a network of cooperation that makes your shop hard to destroy.

---

**Now scale this up:**

Think about a garment factory in Kampong Speu. It employs 3,000 workers. Its stakeholders are:

- Workers who need fair wages and safe conditions
- Local communities who use the same river for drinking and bathing
- The government that granted the operating license
- International brands that buy the garments
- Investors who funded the machinery
- Environmental inspectors who test water quality

If the factory manager only thinks about the brands and the investors, and ignores workers, the community, and the environment — what happens?

Workers strike. The community files a complaint. An NGO publishes photographs. The brand gets bad press in Europe. The investor's ESG fund calls an emergency meeting.

What started as "we only care about profit" ends as a very expensive problem.

**Stakeholder theory says:** all of those groups were stakeholders from the beginning. Managing them was never optional. The only question was whether you managed them proactively — or reactively, when they became crises.

---

**The simple version:**

Your business does not exist in a bubble. It is surrounded by a web of people and groups who all affect it — and are affected by it. Ignore any thread in that web, and the whole thing shakes.
