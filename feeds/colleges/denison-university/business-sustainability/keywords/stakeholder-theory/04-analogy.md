# Stakeholder Theory — Analogy Bridge
# ទ្រឹស្ដីភាគីពាក់ព័ន្ធ — ស្ពានប្រៀបធៀប

*Author: ichamrong | Date: 2026-05-29*

## Analogy 1: The Village Water Festival Boat Race / ការប្រណាំងទូកបុណ្យអ៊ំទូក

**The Story:**
A traditional racing boat in Cambodia requires 40–60 paddlers rowing in perfect synchrony. The captain (lok nao) at the stern steers. The drum-beater sets the rhythm. But the boat also depends on the boat-builders who maintain the hull, the villagers who cheer and lend moral support, the monks who bless the craft at dawn, and the commune chief who arranges the landing dock.

If the captain focuses only on the paddlers and ignores the boat-builders, the hull cracks mid-race. If the drum-beater sets a rhythm without understanding the paddlers' fatigue, the boat loses pace. If the commune chief is not consulted about docking arrangements, there is chaos at the finish.

**The Mapping:**

| Analogy Element | Stakeholder Theory Element | ខ្មែរ |
|---|---|---|
| Captain | CEO / Management | ប្រធាន |
| Paddlers | Employees | អ្នកចោះ |
| Boat-builders | Suppliers | អ្នកផ្គត់ផ្គង់ |
| Villagers cheering | Community / local society | សហគមន៍ |
| Monks who bless | Regulatory/legitimacy authorities | អង្គភាពស្របច្បាប់ |
| Commune chief | Government | អាជ្ញាធរ |

**Where the Analogy Breaks Down:**
In a boat race, all stakeholders share the immediate goal (win the race) and the timescale is minutes. In business, stakeholders have conflicting interests (workers want wages up; shareholders want costs down) and the timescale is years — making coordination more complex and trade-offs unavoidable.

## Analogy 2: The Mekong Fishery as a Shared Resource System / ការនេសាទទន្លេមេគង្គជាប្រព័ន្ធធនធានរួម

**The Story:**
The Mekong's fishery sustains millions of people across Cambodia, Laos, Thailand, and Vietnam. No single actor owns the river. But many actors depend on it: commercial fishers, subsistence villagers, dam operators, irrigated farmers, downstream delta rice growers. Each has a stake. Each has different levels of power.

When dam operators in Laos made unilateral decisions about water release without consulting downstream fishing communities in Cambodia's Stung Treng Province, the fish migration patterns disrupted and local communities' food security collapsed. The dam operators had *legal* authority. The fishing communities had *stakeholder* claims. The conflict between the two is a textbook stakeholder management failure.

**The Mapping:**

| Analogy Element | Stakeholder Theory Element | ខ្មែរ |
|---|---|---|
| River fishery | Firm's value-creating activity | ការបង្កើតតម្លៃ |
| Dam operators | Firm management | ការគ្រប់គ្រង |
| Downstream fishing communities | Affected non-equity stakeholders | ភាគីពាក់ព័ន្ធ |
| Fish migration disruption | Negative externality = hidden liability | ការចំណាយលាក់ |
| Conflict and advocacy campaigns | Stakeholder backlash | ការតស៊ូ |

**Where the Analogy Breaks Down:**
River governance involves sovereign states, not just firms — adding geopolitical complexity that stakeholder theory in its original form does not fully address.

## Analogy 3: The Pagoda Committee / គណៈកម្មការវត្ត

**The Story:**
Every Cambodian pagoda is maintained by a *committee* (គណៈកម្មការ): the head monk, elected lay leaders, the family who donated the main building, the village elders, and ordinary worshippers. Major decisions — whether to build a new kitchen, whether to accept a company donation for the parking lot — require consultation with all of these groups.

A head monk who makes all decisions unilaterally might technically have religious authority. But if he ignores the family that donated the main building, they stop contributing. If he ignores the village elders, the community loses trust. If he ignores ordinary worshippers, attendance drops and donations dry up.

He may have authority, but he has no cooperation. And without cooperation, there is no functioning pagoda.

**The Mapping:**

| Analogy Element | Stakeholder Theory Element | ខ្មែរ |
|---|---|---|
| Head monk | CEO | ប្រធានក្រុមហ៊ុន |
| Pagoda committee | Board / governance structure | ក្រុមប្រឹក្សា |
| Donor families | Shareholders/investors | ភាគទុនិក |
| Village elders | Community leaders / civil society | ថ្នាក់ដឹកនាំ |
| Ordinary worshippers | Customers / end users | អ្នកប្រើប្រាស់ |
| Functioning pagoda | Long-run firm value | តម្លៃក្រុមហ៊ុនរយៈពេលវែង |

**Where the Analogy Breaks Down:**
Pagoda governance is based on shared religious values and voluntary contribution — there is no profit motive and no competitive market. Business stakeholder management must function even when interests are sharply adversarial.

---

## Core Insight Across All Three Analogies / ការយល់ដឹងស្នូល

All three analogies illustrate the same principle: **authority without cooperation cannot produce results**. Firms, like boat crews, river systems, and pagoda committees, are embedded in webs of interdependence. Pretending that only one stakeholder (the shareholder) matters is not just ethically narrow — it is strategically naive.
