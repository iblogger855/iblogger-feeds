## The Factory That Forgot the River / រោងចក្រដែលភ្លេចទន្លេ

The new general manager arrived from Kuala Lumpur in early January, just after the water festival crowds had cleared from the streets of Phnom Penh. His name was Vincent Tan. He had a three-year turnaround mandate and a bonus structure tied entirely to EBITDA improvement.

His factory — a mid-size garment facility in Kampong Speu — was losing money. Labour costs had risen, a major European buyer had trimmed its order, and the machinery needed upgrading. Vincent had fixed two factories before. He knew what to do.

In his first week, he mapped what he called "the three stakeholders who matter": the Singapore parent company, the European buyer (a mid-market retail chain), and the Cambodian government's Ministry of Labour. Everyone else, he told his management team, was background noise.

He cut the canteen subsidy to save costs. He lengthened shifts to meet the order. He deferred maintenance on the wastewater treatment system — it was functional, barely, and the repair would have cost $80,000.

By March, the EBITDA numbers were improving. He sent a confident slide deck to Singapore.

In April, a fish farmer downstream noticed something wrong with the water in the irrigation channel that connected to the factory's drainage outlet. The fish were dying. He brought a sample to the commune chief, a quiet woman named Meas Sokha, who had lived in this commune her entire life and knew every family that drew water from that channel.

Sokha did not go to the newspapers. She first came to the factory, politely, with the fish farmer and two village elders. She asked to meet the manager.

Vincent gave her twenty minutes.

He acknowledged there might be an issue, thanked her for coming, and promised to look into it.

He did not look into it.

Six weeks later, a Cambodian environmental NGO published a water quality report that named the factory. The report included photographs of grey water, laboratory analysis of chemical concentrations, and interviews with eight farming families who had lost crops or fish stock.

The European buyer sent an urgent email: *We require an independent audit and remediation plan within 30 days or we will source elsewhere.*

The Singapore board called an emergency meeting.

The Cambodian Ministry of Environment opened an investigation.

Vincent flew to Singapore to explain himself.

The $80,000 wastewater repair that he had deferred now cost $340,000 — because the equipment had degraded further, the regulatory remediation required a full system upgrade, and the company had to compensate the farming families. The European buyer suspended its order for three months while the audit was completed. The revenue loss during those three months was $1.2 million.

In the post-crisis review, a consultant asked Vincent: "Who did you consider to be your stakeholders?"

He listed: Singapore board, European buyer, Ministry of Labour.

"What about the community downstream? The fish farmers? The commune chief?"

"They don't own any equity in this business."

"No. But they had the power to contact an NGO, who had the power to contact your buyer, who had the power to suspend $1.2 million in orders. How is that not a stakeholder?"

Vincent had no answer.

The commune chief Meas Sokha later told a journalist: "I came to him first. I tried to solve it quietly. I gave him the chance. He did not take it."

The consultant's final report to the Singapore board recommended a stakeholder mapping exercise for all facilities — a structured process for identifying every group that could affect or be affected by operations, regardless of whether they held equity. The methodology had a name that the board's younger members recognized from business school: **stakeholder theory**.

It had been formulated forty years earlier. Vincent had simply never thought it applied to a garment factory in Kampong Speu.
