## The Solar Workshop That Sold Slices of Itself / រោងជាងព្រះអាទិត្យដែលលក់ចំណែករបស់ខ្លួន

Pisey could see the future from the roof of her small workshop in Kampong Speu. From up there, in the white afternoon glare, she could see a hundred farmhouses with no grid power and a sun that poured down free energy eleven hours a day. She knew how to assemble solar kits — panels, a battery, the small clever wiring — that could light those houses. She had built a dozen by hand already.

What she did not have was money. To build solar kits for a hundred farms, then a thousand, she needed far more than her savings, and the bank looked at her calloused hands and her two-room workshop and offered her almost nothing.

Her uncle, who had spent years working at a trading house in Singapore, listened to her despair and asked one strange question: "Why are you trying to *borrow* all of it? Sell pieces of the company instead."

This is the first thing the story teaches: when a venture needs more money than any one person can give or lend, it can divide its ownership into slices and sell them. Each buyer puts in a little and owns a piece of everything that comes after.

So Pisey did a frightening, hopeful thing. She drew up the workshop as a company and sold slices of it — shares — to whoever believed in the idea. Her uncle bought the largest piece. A schoolteacher who'd seen the solar lights bought a small one. Eleven neighbours, a rice trader, a returned migrant with savings — each bought a slice. The money they handed over, all pooled, was suddenly enough to buy panels by the container instead of one at a time.

That pooling of many people's money into the company's hands to build something real — that was the heart of it. The cash went into panels and a proper assembly bench and three trained workers. Within a year the workshop was lighting farmhouses across two districts.

But here is the second thing the story teaches, and Pisey only learned it when the schoolteacher came to her, embarrassed, a year in. His mother was ill; he needed his money back. "I'm sorry," he said, "I know the money is all in panels now. I don't expect—"

"You don't sell your slice back to me," Pisey's uncle explained gently, having stayed on as an adviser. "You sell it to *someone else* who wants to own a piece of a workshop that's clearly thriving. The company keeps every riel it raised; the slice just changes hands."

And it did. A businesswoman from the provincial town, who had watched the solar lights spread and judged the company a winner, bought the teacher's slice — and paid him *more* than he had originally put in, because the workshop was now worth far more than when it started.

This is the third thing the story teaches: because owners can sell their slices to others whenever they need to, people are willing to invest in the first place — and the price those slices fetch becomes a public verdict on how the company is doing. When the businesswoman paid a high price, she was announcing to everyone: *this venture is worth backing.*

That verdict had a power Pisey hadn't expected. With her shares now visibly valuable, she could sell a few *new* slices to fund her biggest dream yet — solar for a thousand farms — and investors, seeing the rising price, lined up to buy.

Years later her son studied finance and came home full of words: *primary market, secondary market, liquidity, price discovery.* Pisey listened from the roof, watching the farmhouse lights blink on across the dark valley, each one a place her pooled-and-traded slices had reached.

"I know all the words now," she said. "But it was simpler than the words. I sold pieces of the sun's work to people who believed in it, and their belief — and the price they'd pay each other for a piece — built every light you can see from up here."
