# Equity Markets — Journalist Interview
# ទីផ្សារភាគហ៊ុន — បទសម្ភាសន៍សារព័ត៌មាន

*Author: ichamrong | Date: 2026-06-01*

---

*Interview between Ratana Chan of the Phnom Penh Economic Tribune and Dr. Sokunthea Pich, financial economist at the Royal University of Agriculture.*

---

**Ratana Chan:** Dr. Sokunthea, stock markets can seem like casinos to ordinary people. What do equity markets actually *do* for an economy?

**Dr. Sokunthea Pich:** They do something profound that's easy to miss behind the daily price drama. An equity market connects two groups who desperately need each other: companies that need large amounts of long-term money to grow, and savers who have money they want to grow but can't each fund a whole enterprise. By dividing company ownership into shares, the market lets a thousand small savers collectively build a factory none of them could afford alone. That's the real economic function — turning scattered savings into productive capital.

**Ratana:** Walk us through how a company actually raises money this way.

**Dr. Sokunthea Pich:** When a company first sells shares to the public — an IPO, an initial public offering — that's the primary market, and the money goes straight to the company to invest. After that, those shares trade among investors on the exchange — the secondary market. Crucially, the company gets nothing from secondary trading; investors are just passing ownership back and forth. People often think the secondary market is pointless for the firm, but it's essential.

**Ratana:** Why essential, if the company gets no money from it?

**Dr. Sokunthea Pich:** Because of liquidity. If you knew you could never sell a share once you bought it, you'd be terrified to buy in the first place — your money would be trapped forever. The secondary market guarantees you can exit whenever you need to. That assurance is exactly what makes you willing to fund the company originally. The two markets sustain each other: the secondary market's liquidity feeds the primary market's fundraising.

**Ratana:** And the share price itself — what is it really telling us?

**Dr. Sokunthea Pich:** It's a verdict. Every trade reflects thousands of investors, each risking their own money, estimating the company's future. The price that emerges is the crowd's collective best guess at what the business is worth — we call it price discovery. And it does real work: a company the market values highly can raise fresh capital cheaply and grow, while a doubted company can't. So prices quietly steer society's savings toward the enterprises people judge most promising. That's the market's deepest job — allocating capital.

**Ratana:** How does that connect to sustainability?

**Dr. Sokunthea Pich:** This is where it gets exciting and contested. Increasingly, large investment funds practise ESG investing — steering money toward companies with strong environmental and governance records, and away from heavy polluters. When enough capital does this, it raises the cost of capital for unsustainable businesses and lowers it for clean ones. Green companies can fund themselves more cheaply through equity; shareholders pressure boards to decarbonize. The equity market becomes a steering wheel for the whole economy's direction.

**Ratana:** Does that steering actually work?

**Dr. Sokunthea Pich:** Only as well as the information in the prices. If a polluting firm remains hugely profitable because its environmental damage isn't priced in, capital still flows to it — the market rewards profit, not virtue, unless the two are aligned. And there's a darker risk: greenwashing, where companies exaggerate their environmental credentials to attract ESG money they don't deserve. The market can be fooled. So sustainable finance depends on honest disclosure and on prices that genuinely reflect environmental risk.

**Ratana:** What about Cambodia specifically?

**Dr. Sokunthea Pich:** Our equity market, the Cambodia Securities Exchange, is young and small — only a handful of listed firms since 2011. That thinness limits liquidity and price discovery; a market needs depth and trust to work well. But the potential is real. A maturing exchange could let Cambodian savers fund Cambodian growth — including sustainable infrastructure — rather than depending entirely on foreign capital. Building that market is itself a long-term development project.

**Ratana:** One sentence for a young entrepreneur?

**Dr. Sokunthea Pich:** An equity market lets you sell slices of your vision to people who believe in it — and the price they pay becomes the verdict that decides whether your idea gets fed the capital to grow.

**Ratana:** Thank you, Dr. Sokunthea.
