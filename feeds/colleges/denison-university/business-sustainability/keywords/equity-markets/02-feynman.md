# Equity Markets — Feynman Explanation
# ទីផ្សារភាគហ៊ុន — ការពន្យល់បែប Feynman

*Author: ichamrong | Date: 2026-06-01*

---

Imagine your cousin has a brilliant idea for a noodle factory, but it'll cost a fortune to build — far more than she has, and the bank won't lend that much to an untested idea. What can she do?

She can sell *slices of ownership* in the factory. "Give me money now," she tells a hundred friends and strangers, "and in return you own a piece of the factory forever. When it earns, you earn a share. When it grows, your piece grows." Each slice is called a **share**, and the whole arrangement — buying and selling these ownership slices — is what an **equity market** (ទីផ្សារភាគហ៊ុន) is.

---

So the first magic trick equity does: it gathers a little money from many people and turns it into the big money one company needs. A thousand savers, each putting in a modest amount, can build a factory none of them could afford alone.

When the company sells *brand-new* slices to raise this money, that's the **primary market**. The cash goes straight to the company to build the factory. This is the whole point — raising capital.

---

But here's a worry. If you buy a slice of the noodle factory, are you stuck owning it forever? What if you need your money back next year for your daughter's wedding?

That's the second magic trick. There's a marketplace — the stock exchange — where you can sell your slice to *someone else* who wants in. This is the **secondary market**. The company doesn't get money here; you're just passing your slice to a new owner. But this matters enormously: because you *can* sell whenever you want, you were willing to buy in the first place. The ability to get out easily is called **liquidity**, and without it, almost nobody would invest.

---

Now the cleverest trick of all. Every time slices change hands, a *price* gets set — what people will pay for a piece of that factory today. And that price is really a verdict. It's thousands of people, betting their own money, all guessing how well the factory will do.

If the noodles are a hit, buyers crowd in and the price climbs. If a rival opens up and sales sag, the price drops. The market is constantly pricing the company's future. Economists call this **price discovery** — the crowd's running estimate of what a business is truly worth.

And that verdict does real work: a factory the crowd loves can sell new slices easily and cheaply to grow more. A factory the crowd doubts can't. So the market quietly steers everyone's savings toward the businesses people believe in most.

---

**The simple version:**

An equity market lets a company sell slices of itself to raise the big money it needs, lets owners share in the profits without running the place, and lets them sell their slice whenever they want. Every trade sets a price, and that price is the crowd's verdict on the company's future — a verdict that decides which businesses get fed more money to grow. Shares of ownership, a place to trade them, and a price that's really a prediction: that's the whole machine.
