# Sanctions: Three Analogies from Cambodian Life
# ទណ្ឌកម្ម៖ ការប្រៀបធៀបបីយ៉ាង

---

## Analogy 1: The Village Market Boycott
## ការប្រៀបធៀបទី ១៖ ការបដិសេធទីផ្សារភូមិ

**The Analogy:** In a village in Takeo province, a merchant is caught selling fake medicine. The village elders can't force him to stop — he's within his rights technically, and they have no courts. So they organize a boycott: they tell all the other stall holders not to sell to him, not to buy from him, and not to let him use the shared storage facility. His business withers.

**The Mapping:**
| Village Boycott | Economic Sanctions |
|----------------|-------------------|
| Village market | International trading system |
| Village elders | Sanctioning countries (US, EU) |
| Merchant selling fake medicine | Target state engaging in prohibited behavior |
| Shared storage facility | US dollar clearing system / SWIFT banking |
| Other stall holders refusing to trade | Third-party compliance with sanctions |
| Merchant withering | Economic pressure on target |
| Merchant finding a buyer outside the village | Target state finding alternative partners (China) |

**Why It Works:** Captures the coercive mechanism perfectly — you can't force behavior, but you can restrict economic access. Also captures the alternative-partner problem: if the merchant can sell to the next village, the boycott fails.

**Where It Breaks Down:** In a village boycott, the merchant is clearly an individual wrongdoer. In international sanctions, the "wrongdoer" is a government — and the people being hurt by the boycott are often the innocent citizens of that country, not the leaders who made the decisions.

## Analogy 2: The Sihanoukville Building Inspector Who Only Inspects Enemies
## ការប្រៀបធៀបទី ២៖ អ្នកត្រួតពិនិត្យអាគារ ដែលត្រួតពិនិត្យតែ "អ្នកប្រឆាំង"

**The Analogy:** Imagine a powerful building inspector in Sihanoukville who has close friends and political connections. He has the power to require any building to pass an inspection before it can operate. In practice, he only applies strict inspections to businesses owned by people he doesn't like politically. His friends' buildings sail through with minimal scrutiny. When he wants a business to close, he schedules an intensive inspection and finds reasons to fail it.

**The Mapping:**
| Building Inspector | Secondary Sanctions |
|-------------------|---------------------|
| Inspector's power to approve/deny | US Treasury's power to grant/deny dollar system access |
| Selective application of inspections | Targeted sanctioning of specific entities/behaviors |
| His friends getting easy approval | US allies getting exemptions or waivers |
| Targeted businesses forced to close | Sanctioned entities cut off from global finance |
| Businesses seeking alternative approvals | Sanctioned countries creating alternative payment systems (CIPS, SPFS) |

**Why It Works:** Captures the *power asymmetry* of sanctions. The US controls the inspection booth of the global financial system (the dollar clearing mechanism). It can selectively apply this power. The targeted party has little recourse — appealing to a neutral authority is nearly impossible.

**Where It Breaks Down:** A building inspector operates within a legal framework with appeals processes. US sanctions are challenged in international law, but the US doesn't accept most international jurisdiction over its own sanctions policy. The analogy understates the impunity of the sanctions authority.

## Analogy 3: The Cambodia-Vietnam Border Rice Trader During the 1979 Embargo
## ការប្រៀបធៀបទី ៣៖ ពាណិជ្ជករអង្ករ ព្រំដែនខ្មែរ-វៀតណាម ក្នុងការហាមឃាត់ ១៩៧៩

**The Analogy:** After 1979, when Western countries refused to recognize the Vietnamese-installed Cambodian government, international aid and trade were largely cut off. A rice trader in Prey Veng who had built connections with international food agencies suddenly found those connections worthless. But traders who had informal cross-border networks with Vietnamese counterparts — outside formal channels — continued operating. The embargo created a thriving informal economy that bypassed official channels.

**The Mapping:**
| Rice Trade Under Embargo | Sanction Evasion |
|--------------------------|-----------------|
| Official international food agency channels | Formal sanctioned banking and trade systems |
| Informal cross-border Vietnamese trade | Informal sanction-evasion networks |
| Traders who adapted to informal channels | States/entities that build alternative financial systems |
| Western embargo | US-led international sanctions |
| Vietnamese economic channel | China as alternative economic partner |
| Embargo failing to stop food trade | Sanctions failing to isolate target when alternatives exist |

**Why It Works:** The 1979 embargo is historically specific to Cambodia and illustrates the unintended consequences of sanctions. The formal trade cutoff hurt legitimate development but didn't stop all trade — it redirected it through informal, often less efficient and less transparent channels.

**Where It Breaks Down:** The rice trade under embargo was driven by basic survival needs — rice is not optional. Sanctions on luxury goods, advanced technology, or financial services may have more bite because the target's demand can't be met through informal channels as easily.

## The Common Thread / ខ្សែប្រស្ហោ

All three analogies point to the same fundamental insight about sanctions:

> Sanctions work by restricting access to a system that the target values. They fail when the target can exit that system — either by finding an alternative partner, building a parallel system, or bearing the cost of exclusion.

ទណ្ឌកម្មដំណើរការ ដោយដាក់កំហិតលើការចូលដំណើរការប្រព័ន្ធ។ ពួកវាបរាជ័យ ពេលគោលដៅរកបានដៃគូ/ប្រព័ន្ធជំនួស — ដូចចិនជាដៃគូ/ហ្វ្រ៉ង-ប្ដូររបស់ Cambodia ។
