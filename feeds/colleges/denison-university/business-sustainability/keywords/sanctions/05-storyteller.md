# The Factory That Made Perfect Jeans
# រោងចក្រដែលដេររ៉ូបខោ Jean ល្អឥតខ្ចោះ

*A story from a garment district in Phnom Penh*

---

Chantha had built her factory from nothing over eleven years.

She had started as a line worker at twenty-two, sleeping in a shared room with seven other women in a building near Olympic Stadium, sending half her wages back to her parents in Kompong Chhnang. She learned everything — the cutting, the stitching, the finishing, the quality checks. She saved. She took a loan from a microfinance institution. She rented a small space in Russey Keo and hired eight women she trusted.

By 2016, her factory — Chanthabori Garments — employed 340 people and held supply agreements with two European brands and one American label. She met their standards: Fair Trade certified, no child labor, no forced overtime, documented wage sheets, proper safety exits. The factory had been inspected three times. It passed every time.

In the summer of 2020, her account manager at the American label called.

"Chantha," he said, "I have to tell you something before you hear it from someone else."

The American company's compliance team had run an automated scan of their Tier 2 suppliers — the companies that supplied Chanthabori with fabric and buttons. One of her fabric suppliers, a Chinese company based in Guangzhou, had been found in a US Treasury database. Not on the SDN list itself — not yet — but on a list of entities "under review for potential connections to Xinjiang cotton supply chains."

The US had been building sanctions architecture around Xinjiang — the region where forced labor of Uyghur Muslims had been documented in cotton production. The Uyghur Forced Labor Prevention Act, not yet signed but clearly coming, would create a *rebuttable presumption* that any goods containing Xinjiang cotton were made with forced labor, placing the burden of proof on importers.

Chanthabori's fabric supplier was Guangzhou-based, not Xinjiang-based. But its own raw cotton inputs — Chantha had never asked about this — came partly from Xinjiang.

"We need you to provide documentation that none of your inputs contain Xinjiang cotton," the account manager said. "If you can't do that within 90 days, we'll have to pause our orders."

Chantha hung up and sat in her office for a long time.

She called her fabric supplier. They were cooperative but uncertain. They had five different cotton sources. Separating the documentation for each was technically possible but would take months and cost significant money. They would try.

She called a compliance consultant in Singapore who worked with Cambodian garment factories. The consultant explained the landscape calmly. The UFLPA had become law. US Customs was now permitted to detain any shipment suspected of containing Xinjiang inputs. The practical burden was enormous — she would need supply chain transparency down to the cotton gin level.

"But I didn't do anything wrong," Chantha said.

"I know," the consultant said. "But the law doesn't operate on what you knew or intended. It operates on what you can prove."

She passed this information to her 340 workers carefully, at a staff meeting, on a Friday afternoon. The orders wouldn't stop immediately. She was working on it. She needed time. She asked for their patience.

What Chantha understood that afternoon — watching the faces of the women who had stayed with her through factory expansions, through COVID wage cuts, through minimum wage negotiations — was that the sanction was not aimed at her. It was aimed at a labor system in a province of China she had never visited, enforced by a law passed in Washington D.C. by legislators who had likely never heard of Chanthabori Garments.

She was not the target. She was the collateral.

By 2022, she had switched to certified non-Xinjiang fabric suppliers — more expensive, longer lead times, more complex documentation. She lost one of her three brand clients. She hired a part-time compliance officer. She passed the next inspection.

Her workforce was down to 290.

---

*What Chantha encountered had a name: secondary sanction effects — the way that sanctions on a distant target (Xinjiang labor practices) create compliance burdens that cascade down global supply chains, reaching factories in Cambodia that had no connection to the original violation, operated with full transparency, and passed every audit. The sanction worked, in a sense: Xinjiang cotton is harder to sell. But the cost was spread across thousands of suppliers who had no say in the original wrong.*
