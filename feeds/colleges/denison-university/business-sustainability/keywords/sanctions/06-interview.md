# Sanctions: A Cambodian Journalist Interviews a Trade Lawyer
# ទណ្ឌកម្ម៖ អ្នកសារព័ត៌មានខ្មែរសំភាសន៍មេធាវីពាណិជ្ជកម្ម

*Journalist: Sopheak Mony, economics reporter, The Phnom Penh Post*
*Expert: Natasha Browning, International Trade and Sanctions Counsel, Singapore-based law firm advising Southeast Asian manufacturers*

---

**Sopheak:** Ms. Browning, Cambodia's garment sector has been navigating trade preference suspensions and sanctions-adjacent compliance pressure for years now. Can you give us a plain-language picture of what "sanctions risk" actually looks like for a Cambodian factory?

**Natasha Browning:** It operates on multiple levels simultaneously. There's the direct level — if Cambodia itself were comprehensively sanctioned, Cambodian businesses couldn't access US dollars, couldn't use SWIFT banking, couldn't transact with US-affiliated companies. Cambodia isn't comprehensively sanctioned. But there are individual designations — specific Cambodian officials and some business figures connected to the Ream Naval Base controversy or to political repression. If a factory has business ties with a designated person or their controlled entities, the factory faces secondary risk.

Then there's the indirect level — which is actually more commonly relevant. A Cambodian factory's Chinese input supplier might be on a sanctions list. The factory probably doesn't know. But US or European importers are now required to do supply chain due diligence, and if they find a connection, they'll drop the Cambodian supplier rather than deal with the compliance problem themselves. The Cambodian factory gets the pain, even though it's two or three steps removed from the actual sanctioned entity.

---

**Sopheak:** How did the EBA suspension in 2020 function as a sanctions mechanism? Was it technically a sanction?

**Natasha Browning:** Technically no — sanctions in international law are usually responses to threats to international peace and security or human rights violations of a specific kind. The EBA suspension was a trade preference withdrawal under the EU's own GSP regulations, triggered by violations of GSP conditionality — specifically the conventions on democratic governance and labor rights. So it's a conditional trade preference withdrawal, not a sanction in the strict sense. But the economic effect is similar: it increased the cost of accessing the EU market for Cambodian exporters. Some analysts call this "regulatory conditionality as a coercive tool," which is functionally close to sanctions without the formal designation process.

---

**Sopheak:** Did the EBA suspension work?

**Natasha Browning:** By its own stated criteria, only partially. The EU's stated goals were the restoration of genuine multiparty democracy in Cambodia and improvements in labor rights. Neither has been fully achieved. Multiparty politics remain restricted. The CNRP has not been restored. However, labor inspections improved somewhat, some union rights were modestly strengthened, and garment working conditions saw incremental improvements. These are areas where compliance was cheaper for the government — they can improve safety standards without threatening political control. Where compliance required genuine power-sharing, it didn't happen.

What the suspension clearly did achieve was to push Cambodia to diversify its export markets. By 2023, Cambodia had increased exports to China, Japan, and ASEAN markets significantly. The garment sector's dependence on EU market access is lower than it was in 2019. Paradoxically, the sanction may have made Cambodia's economy more resilient against future EU pressure, not less.

---

**Sopheak:** For ordinary Cambodian factory workers — mostly women working in garment factories — what has been the practical impact of these sanctions-adjacent pressures?

**Natasha Browning:** The honest answer is that it's mixed. The EBA suspension contributed to factory closures and order reductions in specific product categories. Some workers lost jobs, particularly in footwear and some garment categories facing higher tariffs. On the other hand, the labor rights conditionality framework in EBA — before the suspension — had been a meaningful driver of union rights improvements and working condition upgrades in the sector. Brands that sourced from Cambodia under EBA had reputational incentives to ensure their suppliers met minimum standards. The complex regulatory environment created by sanctions pressure may have, counterintuitively, raised the bar on compliance in a way that benefited some workers.

---

**Sopheak:** What about the US-China tech sanctions and their spillover into Cambodia? Is that a real risk for Cambodian businesses?

**Natasha Browning:** It's real and growing. The US export control regime — specifically the Entity List and the Huawei-related restrictions — creates risk for any Cambodian company that uses US-origin technology and has Chinese partners. The concern is re-export: if a Chinese entity on the US Entity List can access US technology through a Cambodian intermediary, the Cambodian company becomes a sanctions violation conduit. We've seen US enforcement actions targeting companies in Southeast Asia for exactly this. A Cambodian tech distributor, for example, needs to know whether any of its Chinese customers appear on the Entity List. This is no longer optional compliance — it's liability management.

---

**Sopheak:** What should Cambodian businesses be doing to manage sanctions risk that they're probably not doing right now?

**Natasha Browning:** Three practical things. First, screen your business partners — not just direct customers and suppliers, but their ultimate beneficial owners. OFAC's SDN list is publicly searchable. The EU's consolidated sanctions list is publicly searchable. A basic screening costs almost nothing. Second, document your supply chain. Know where your inputs come from, at least two tiers back. If you can't document it, you can't defend yourself when a buyer's compliance team asks. Third, talk to your major buyers' compliance teams proactively — don't wait for them to find a problem. If you're transparent about your supply chain and can demonstrate good-faith due diligence, you're in a much better position than a factory that ignored the issue until an audit found something.

---

**Sopheak:** Final question. Is Cambodia particularly vulnerable to sanctions, or is its exposure typical for an emerging Southeast Asian economy?

**Natasha Browning:** It's somewhat elevated, for two reasons. First, Cambodia's political alignment with China creates a secondary sanctions overhang that Vietnam, Thailand, and Malaysia — which have more balanced great-power relationships — don't face to the same degree. Second, Cambodia's institutional environment — weak rule of law, less transparent beneficial ownership registries, less robust commercial courts — makes it harder for businesses here to demonstrate the kind of clean governance that sanctions compliance requires. It's not a crisis, but it's a real competitive disadvantage versus regional peers when sophisticated buyers and investors are assessing sanctions exposure.
