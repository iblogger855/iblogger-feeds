# បែបផែនផ្ទះក្រញ — ស្ពានប្រៀបប្រដូច

## Analogy 1: The Car Parked in the Sun (រថយន្តចតក្រោមថ្ងៃ)

**The analogy:** A car parked in direct sunlight gets far hotter inside than outside. Sunlight enters through the glass, heats the seats and dashboard, and the heat cannot escape easily because the glass blocks the outgoing infrared radiation.

**Mapping:**
| Car element | Greenhouse effect element |
|---|---|
| Glass windows | Greenhouse gas layer (CO₂, CH₄, H₂O) |
| Sunlight entering | Shortwave solar radiation passing through atmosphere |
| Heated seats radiating warmth | Earth's surface emitting infrared radiation |
| Glass blocking heat escape | GHGs absorbing and re-emitting infrared downward |
| Interior temperature rise | Global surface temperature increase |
| Adding extra tinting to glass | Increasing GHG concentration from emissions |

**Cambodian connection:** Any driver who has returned to a parked car in Phnom Penh at noon understands this immediately. The car cabin at 70°C while outside air is 35°C — the ratio mirrors Earth's 33°C greenhouse warming.

**Where the analogy holds:** The basic physics of selective transmission is correct — glass and greenhouse gases both allow shortwave in while impeding longwave out.

**Where it breaks down:** A car's glass is a physical barrier; greenhouse gases absorb and re-emit (not merely block) infrared. Also, opening a car window instantly cools it; there is no equivalent "window" for the atmosphere on human timescales.

## Analogy 2: Sleeping Under Extra Blankets (គេងក្រោមភួយបន្ថែម)

**The analogy:** You sleep comfortably under one blanket on a cool night. Your body generates heat; the blanket traps just enough to keep you warm. Add three extra blankets and you overheat — not because your body produces more heat, but because the insulation is too thick.

**Mapping:**
| Blanket element | Greenhouse effect element |
|---|---|
| Your body heat | Earth's surface infrared emission |
| One blanket | Natural greenhouse gas concentration |
| Comfortable sleep temperature | Pre-industrial 15°C average |
| Adding extra blankets | Increasing CO₂/CH₄ concentration |
| Overheating, sweating | Global warming, extreme heat events |
| Cannot easily remove blankets at night | Cannot quickly remove CO₂ from atmosphere |

**Cambodian resonance:** Khmer families know that the hottest nights are when the air is thick and humid — water vapor is already acting as a blanket. Adding more CO₂ is like adding more blankets to an already warm night.

**Where the analogy holds:** The insulation metaphor is intuitive and physically accurate. More GHGs = more effective insulation = higher equilibrium temperature.

**Where it breaks down:** Blankets work by convection (trapping still air); GHGs work by radiative absorption. Also, you can throw off blankets immediately — removing atmospheric CO₂ takes decades even if all emissions stop today.

## Analogy 3: The Greenhouse Nursery (ផ្ទះខ្ទម/ផ្ទះរុក្ខជាតិ)

**The analogy:** Cambodian nurseries growing orchids or seedlings often use plastic-sheeting shelters. The plastic lets sunlight in, warms the air and plants, and traps the warm air inside. This is ideal for orchids — but if you keep adding more plastic layers, the temperature rises too high and the plants die.

**Mapping:**
| Nursery element | Greenhouse effect element |
|---|---|
| Plastic sheeting | Greenhouse gas layer |
| Sunlight in | Solar shortwave radiation |
| Trapped warm air | Trapped infrared heat |
| Orchids thriving | Life on Earth at 15°C |
| Too many plastic layers | Excess GHG concentration |
| Orchids wilting and dying | Ecosystems stressed by excess warming |
| Farmer adding more layers | Continued industrial GHG emissions |

**Where the analogy holds:** It captures why some greenhouse effect is beneficial (orchids need warmth) and why the *enhanced* greenhouse effect is harmful (too much warmth kills). This mirrors the natural vs. anthropogenic distinction.

**Where it breaks down:** The nursery farmer can immediately remove plastic sheets. Atmospheric CO₂ has a residence time of hundreds of years. Even if all emissions stopped today, warming would continue for decades due to heat already stored in the oceans.
