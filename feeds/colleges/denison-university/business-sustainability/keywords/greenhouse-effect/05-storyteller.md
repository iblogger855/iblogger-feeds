## The Invisible Blanket | ភួយដែលមើលមិនឃើញ

Malis had grown up in a village outside Kampong Thom, planting rice the same week every year — the third week of May, when the first rains came reliably and the soil held enough moisture to transplant seedlings without losing them overnight.

Her mother had planted that same week. Her grandmother had planted that same week. They knew this not from a calendar but from the sky — the particular color of the clouds, the smell of the rain on dry earth, the behavior of certain birds that arrived before the monsoon.

In 2022, Malis planted the third week of May and lost half her seedlings to drought. She replanted in June. The rains came late, then came violently — a week of flooding that drowned the replanted crop. She harvested barely enough to repay the seed loan.

In 2023, she tried to start earlier. Too early. A dry spell in April killed two rounds of seedlings before she gave up and waited.

She was not alone. At the agricultural extension office in town, a young officer named Chenda was seeing the same pattern across forty villages. He showed Malis a chart: the monsoon onset in Kampong Thom province, mapped from 1980 to 2025. The line zigzagged wildly in recent years — sometimes two weeks early, sometimes three weeks late, rarely on time.

"Why?" Malis asked.

Chenda had studied in Phnom Penh and had learned the answer, though he sometimes struggled to explain it in a way that felt real rather than theoretical.

He told her about the sun. How the sun sends light to Earth. How the ground absorbs that light and radiates warmth back upward, in a different form — longer waves, slower, invisible heat. How certain gases in the sky catch that rising heat and send some of it back down, like a blanket. How, for thousands of years, the blanket was just thick enough to keep the planet at exactly the right temperature. How every civilization — every rice paddy, every irrigation canal, every seasonal calendar — had been built around that blanket's thickness.

He told her how the blanket had been getting thicker.

"By how much?" she asked.

"Since your grandmother's time, CO₂ in the air has gone up about 25 percent. The blanket is meaningfully thicker. The planet is holding about 1.2 degrees Celsius more heat than it did then."

"That doesn't sound like much."

"For a planet, it is enormous. Think of a person with a fever. 37°C is normal. 38.2°C and you feel ill. 40°C and you can barely move. The difference between a glacial ice age and today is only about 5 degrees. One degree of warming at the planetary scale changes where monsoons form, how strong they are, when they arrive."

Malis stared at the chart. "My grandmother's calendar doesn't work anymore."

"No," Chenda said quietly.

She drove home through flat rice country that stretched to the horizon, flooded in patches, dry in others, the pattern wrong in ways she could feel but could not name. She thought about the invisible blanket thickening over her fields. She thought about her daughter, who would inherit these fields. She thought about how the problem had been made by factories and cars in countries whose names she barely knew — and how it arrived at her fields in the form of a ruined planting calendar.

She could not remove the blanket. But she could talk to the extension office about planting schedules adjusted for the new patterns. She could plant drought-tolerant varieties in the early plots and flood-tolerant varieties in the low-lying fields. She could advocate, through the farmers' cooperative, for the government to bring the issue to international climate negotiations.

It wasn't enough. She knew it wasn't enough. But it was something. You start with what you can do.

The next May, she planted four days later than her grandmother would have, based on the new planting calendar the extension office had designed from recent weather data. The rains came six days later than old-normal. The overlap was almost right.

She saved most of the crop.

---

*This story illustrates the **greenhouse effect** (បែបផែនផ្ទះក្រញ) — the natural atmospheric process by which greenhouse gases trap heat, and how human-enhanced concentrations are disrupting seasonal patterns that agricultural societies depend on.*
