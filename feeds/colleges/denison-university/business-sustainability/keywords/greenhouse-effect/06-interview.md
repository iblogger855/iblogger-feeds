# Greenhouse Effect — Journalist Interview
# បែបផែនផ្ទះក្រញ — បទសម្ភាសន៍អ្នកសារព័ត៌មាន

*Author: ichamrong | Date: 2026-05-29*
*Cambodian journalist Sreymom interviews Dr. Vannak Rith, climate physicist, Royal University of Phnom Penh*

---

**Sreymom:** Dr. Rith, what is the greenhouse effect in one sentence?

**Dr. Rith:** Certain gases in the atmosphere allow sunlight in but slow the escape of heat — just like the glass of a greenhouse (ផ្ទះក្រញ) — and this keeps Earth's surface about 33°C warmer than it would otherwise be.

**Sreymom:** Is the greenhouse effect a natural thing or a human-caused problem?

**Dr. Rith:** It is both. The natural greenhouse effect (បែបផែនផ្ទះក្រញធម្មជាតិ) is absolutely essential for life — without it, Earth would average −18°C and be covered in ice. The problem is the *enhanced* greenhouse effect: human industrial activity is adding greenhouse gases to the atmosphere faster than natural processes can absorb them, thickening the atmospheric blanket and warming the planet beyond the range that our agriculture and ecosystems were built around.

**Sreymom:** Why should ordinary Cambodians care about a physical process happening in the upper atmosphere?

**Dr. Rith:** Because the consequences arrive at the rice paddy level. The monsoon system that Cambodian farmers have relied on for millennia is driven by the temperature difference between the Indian Ocean and the Asian landmass. Change the heat balance of the atmosphere and you change the monsoon — its timing, intensity, and reliability. Farmers in Kampong Speu and Battambang are already reporting that the planting calendar their grandparents taught them is no longer reliable. That is the greenhouse effect expressing itself in a village.

**Sreymom:** What are the main greenhouse gases relevant to Cambodia?

**Dr. Rith:** Carbon dioxide (CO₂ — ឧស្ម័នកាបូនឌីអ៊ូស៊ីត) from burning fossil fuels and deforestation. Methane (CH₄ — ម៉ីតាន) from Cambodia's flooded rice paddies and livestock — this is actually our largest domestic greenhouse gas source. Nitrous oxide (N₂O) from nitrogen fertilizers. And smaller contributions from industrial processes. Cambodia's total emissions are small globally — less than 0.1 percent — but the methane from our rice paddies is significant within our own national inventory.

**Sreymom:** Rice paddies causing climate change — is that something farmers should feel guilty about?

**Dr. Rith:** Absolutely not. Rice paddies have produced methane for thousands of years, long before climate change became a crisis. The global problem was caused by the industrialization of rich countries. Cambodian rice farmers are not responsible for the enhanced greenhouse effect. However, reducing rice paddy methane is a practical near-term action Cambodia can take as part of our own NDC commitments, and there are simple techniques — like alternate wetting and drying (AWD — ការស្រោចទឹកជំនួស) — that reduce methane by 30 to 70 percent with minimal yield loss. Farmers who adopt AWD may also save water and reduce irrigation costs.

**Sreymom:** What does a 2°C temperature increase mean for Cambodia specifically?

**Dr. Rith:** Several things. First, sea level rise of roughly 0.3 to 0.5 meters by 2100, threatening coastal provinces in Koh Kong and Kampot, and affecting the Mekong Delta immediately across the border. Second, more intense flooding during peak monsoon, followed by longer dry seasons — a whiplash pattern that is very damaging for rain-fed agriculture. Third, heat stress on rice during the grain-filling period — yields drop measurably when temperatures exceed 35°C during flowering. Fourth, changes to the Tonle Sap flood pulse as Mekong upstream hydrology becomes less predictable. These are not distant projections; they are already visible in historical data.

**Sreymom:** Cambodia's garment industry is our main export sector. What is its connection to the greenhouse effect?

**Dr. Rith:** Garment factories use significant electricity — mostly from coal and oil generators — and that combustion emits CO₂. European buyers are beginning to require carbon footprint data from their supply chains, partly because the EU Carbon Border Adjustment Mechanism applies a carbon price to imports. Cambodia's garment competitiveness in 2030 and beyond will partly depend on how quickly we can decarbonize our electricity grid. Hydropower already provides a significant share, but coal capacity added in Sihanoukville is moving in the wrong direction. Investment in solar energy — which Cambodia has excellent potential for — would both reduce emissions and lower long-term electricity costs.

**Sreymom:** Cambodia signed the Paris Agreement and submitted an NDC. Is that meaningful or just paperwork?

**Dr. Rith:** It is meaningful if implemented. Cambodia's updated NDC commits to a 42 percent reduction in emissions below business-as-usual by 2030, conditional on international financial support. The key measures are forest protection and restoration, rice paddy methane reduction, and clean energy. These are achievable targets. The "conditional" part is important — Cambodia is saying: we will do this if richer countries provide climate finance as they promised. That is a fair position. Cambodia should not have to sacrifice development to fix a problem it did not create.

**Sreymom:** Final thought for readers?

**Dr. Rith:** The atmosphere is a shared global resource, like the Mekong River. What goes into it in one country affects everyone downstream. Cambodia has a right to demand that those who have put the most into the atmospheric commons take the most responsibility for cleaning it up — and that those of us who will suffer most from the consequences receive meaningful support to adapt.
