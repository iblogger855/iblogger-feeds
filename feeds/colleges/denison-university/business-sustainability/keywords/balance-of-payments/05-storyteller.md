## The Two Ledgers of the Port Town / បញ្ជីពីរនៃទីក្រុងកំពង់ផែ

Sopheap ran the busiest customs desk at the river port, and over twenty years she had come to see the whole town as two ledgers that had to agree.

The first ledger she could watch with her own eyes from the window. Boats came upriver loaded with foreign things — diesel, machines, motorbikes, bolts of cloth — and the town's money flowed out to pay for them. Other boats went downriver heavy with what the town made: rice, dried fish, rubber, and lately container loads of stitched garments bound for distant ports. That money flowed in.

For most of those twenty years, Sopheap noticed, more value came *up* the river than went *down*. The town bought more from the world than it sold. By the simple arithmetic of her first ledger, the town was running what the young auditors from the capital called a deficit.

This is the first thing the story teaches: a place that buys more from the outside world than it sells is, on its trade ledger, in deficit — money flowing out faster than it flows in.

For years a worried elder would point at the first ledger and say the town was bleeding, that one day there would be no money left at all. And yet there was always money. The motorbikes kept arriving. The diesel kept flowing. Sopheap could never quite explain to the elder why the town hadn't run dry, until a patient young woman from the central bank came to audit the port and showed her the *second* ledger.

The second ledger Sopheap had never thought to keep, because it did not pass through her window. It recorded the foreigners who built the garment factory at the edge of town, the ones who lent the cooperative money for a new rice mill, the company from abroad that bought a share in the river warehouse. Every one of those was money flowing *into* the town — not for goods, but in exchange for *ownership* and *promises to repay*.

"Look," the young auditor said, laying the two ledgers side by side. "Your first ledger shows money leaving to buy imports. My second ledger shows money arriving as investment and loans. They have to add up. Every riel that left to buy a foreign machine is a riel that came in because some foreigner chose to put their wealth here. The town can run a trade deficit *only* because outsiders are willing to finance it. The two ledgers always reconcile to zero."

This is the second thing the story teaches: a trade deficit is never the whole story. It is mirrored, exactly, by money flowing in on the other ledger — foreign lending and investment. The books always balance; the only question is what's on each side.

The elder, hearing this, was only half comforted. "And if those foreigners stop sending their money? If they lose their nerve?"

The auditor nodded slowly. "Then the town can no longer afford to buy more than it sells. The imports stop, prices jump, and the adjustment comes all at once and painfully. A deficit is safe only as long as someone is willing to finance it — and only wise if the money buys things that pay off later." She tapped the new rice mill on the second ledger. "That machine will earn its keep. A town that borrows for mills can carry a deficit for years. A town that borrows only to consume cannot."

Sopheap kept both ledgers side by side from that day on. When her granddaughter, years later, came home from university talking about the *balance of payments* and the *current* and *financial* accounts, Sopheap smiled and pulled out her two worn books.

"I know," she said. "The river taught me. What goes out one ledger comes in the other. The trick is never the deficit. The trick is what you bought with it."
