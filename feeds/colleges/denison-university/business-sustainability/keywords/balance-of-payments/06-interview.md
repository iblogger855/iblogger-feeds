# Balance of Payments — Journalist Interview
# ជញ្ជីងទូទាត់ — បទសម្ភាសន៍សារព័ត៌មាន

*Author: ichamrong | Date: 2026-06-01*

---

*Interview between Ratana Chan of the Phnom Penh Economic Tribune and Dr. Sokunthea Pich, international finance economist at the Royal University of Agriculture.*

---

**Ratana Chan:** Dr. Sokunthea, "balance of payments" sounds like dry accounting. Why does it matter to an ordinary Cambodian?

**Dr. Sokunthea Pich:** Because it's the scoreboard of a country's entire financial relationship with the world, and it quietly governs things people feel — the value of the riel, the price of imported fuel, whether the country can afford to invest in its future. It records every dollar that crosses our borders: what we earn from exports and tourism, what we pay for imports, the money our workers send home, and the investment that flows in and out. Get it wrong and you get a currency crisis. Read it well and you understand the limits of what a nation can afford.

**Ratana:** Break it into its parts for us.

**Dr. Sokunthea Pich:** Two parts matter most. The *current account* is the everyday flow — goods and services traded, income earned abroad, and transfers like remittances. The *financial account* is about assets — foreign investment coming in, our investments going out, and the central bank's reserves. There's a small third piece, the capital account, but for most countries it's minor. The crucial fact is that these accounts must sum to zero. They are two sides of the same coin.

**Ratana:** Explain that "sum to zero" idea simply.

**Dr. Sokunthea Pich:** If Cambodia buys more from the world than it sells — a current-account deficit — it must pay for the difference somehow. That payment comes from foreigners lending to us or investing here, which shows up as a financial-account surplus. The deficit on one side is mirrored by the surplus on the other. It's not a coincidence; it's bookkeeping. You literally cannot import more than you export unless someone is financing the gap.

**Ratana:** People hear "trade deficit" and assume disaster. Are they right?

**Dr. Sokunthea Pich:** Usually not. A deficit is just information. A young, growing economy *should* often run a current-account deficit — it's importing the machines, equipment, and technology it can't yet make, financed by investors betting on its growth. That's healthy, like a family borrowing to buy tools that earn a living. The danger is borrowing to consume — financing today's spending with debt that builds nothing to repay it. And the real risk is a *sudden stop*: if foreign financing dries up overnight, the country can no longer afford its imports, the currency falls, and the adjustment is brutal.

**Ratana:** Where does Cambodia stand?

**Dr. Sokunthea Pich:** Cambodia typically runs a sizeable goods trade deficit — we import fuel, machinery, and inputs — partly offset by garment and footwear exports, tourism, and substantial remittances from workers abroad. The remaining gap is financed largely by foreign direct investment and concessional loans. That makes us dependent on continued inflows of investor confidence. It's not inherently dangerous, but it means we must watch that the borrowing funds productive capacity, not just consumption.

**Ratana:** How does this connect to sustainability?

**Dr. Sokunthea Pich:** Directly, and it's underappreciated. A green transition requires importing solar panels, wind turbines, electric buses, and grid technology — all of which widen the current-account deficit in the short run. That has to be financed. The question is *how*: with cheap, patient green bonds and climate finance, or with expensive short-term debt that leaves us fragile? There's also a deeper risk — a country that depends on exporting a single commodity, say timber or oil, has a balance of payments hostage to one price. Diversifying exports is itself a form of sustainability, protecting the nation from shocks.

**Ratana:** One sentence for our readers?

**Dr. Sokunthea Pich:** The balance of payments is the ledger that always balances — so the real question is never the deficit itself, but whether the world's money flowing in is building Cambodia's future or merely paying for its present.

**Ratana:** Thank you, Dr. Sokunthea.
