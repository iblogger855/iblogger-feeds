# Realism vs. Liberalism: Explain It Simply
# រេអាលីស ទល់នឹង លីបឺរ៉ាលីស៖ ពន្យល់សាមញ្ញ

---

## Imagine the School Playground / ស្រមៃទីលានកូនសាលា

Think about your school's playground. There are some kids who are much bigger and stronger than others. Now imagine two different ideas about how the playground should work:

**Idea 1 — The Realist View:**
"The biggest, strongest kids will always get the best spots by the swings. They might make rules, but really the rules only work because they're the strongest. If you're small, you have to be friends with a big kid to be safe. There's no teacher watching — it's every kid for themselves."

**Idea 2 — The Liberal View:**
"We should all agree to rules — no hitting, take turns, share the equipment. Even the big kids benefit from having clear rules because chaos is bad for everyone. We can create a student committee to settle disputes. If everyone follows the rules, even small kids are safe."

Now ask yourself: **which view is more correct?**

Most adults would say: *it depends on the playground.*

Some playgrounds really do have fair rules that everyone follows. Others are dominated by the biggest kids, and the rules only work when the big kids feel like following them.

International relations — the way countries deal with each other — has exactly this debate.

ស្រមៃទីលានសាលា។ គំនិតទី ១ (Realism): ក្មេងខ្លាំងបំផុតឈ្នះ — ក្ខន្ធច្បាប់ ប៉ុន្តែអំណាចជាការពិតជាង។ គំនិតទី ២ (Liberalism): ច្បាប់ច្បាស់ ដែលទាំងអស់គ្នាយល់ព្រម — ជំនួយឱ្យ ក្មេងតូចក៏ត្រូវបានការពារ។

---

## How This Affects Cambodia / ប៉ះពាល់ដល់កម្ពុជាយ៉ាងណា?

**The Realist Story of Cambodia:**

China is much bigger than Cambodia. China has money, military power, and influence. Cambodia's relationship with China is partly about a small country getting protection and investment from a big one. In return, Cambodia supports China's positions in international forums. That's the realist logic — big country protects small country, small country serves big country's interests. No apology needed. That's just how power works.

**The Liberal Story of Cambodia:**

Cambodia joined the WTO. It got EBA trade preferences from the EU. It participates in ASEAN. These institutions gave Cambodia access to European and American markets that it couldn't get through power alone. The liberal bet was: if Cambodia integrates into the rules-based economic order, it grows, and growing economies tend to improve governance. Rules, not power, were supposed to guarantee Cambodia's development.

**What Actually Happened:**

Both stories are partially true. The liberal rules helped Cambodia's garment sector grow massively. But the realist dynamic — China's protection being the backbone of the regime's survival — limited how far liberal governance reforms could go. When the EU suspended EBA preferences to push for democracy, Cambodia responded by moving *closer* to China, not toward democracy. Realist logic won that round.

**ករណីកម្ពុជា:** ទស្សនៈ Liberal ជួយឱ្យរោងចក្រសម្លៀកបំពាក់រីកលូតលាស់ ប៉ុន្តែ ទស្សនៈ Realist — ចិនជាអ្នកការពាររបបគ្រប់គ្រង — ជំនះ ពេលអ៊ឺរ៉ុបព្យាយាមអំពាវនាវ ដោយកែទម្រង់ប្រជាធិបតេយ្យ។

## The Simple Difference / ភាពខុសគ្នាសាមញ្ញ

| Realism | Liberalism |
|---------|------------|
| Power rules everything | Rules tame power |
| States only care about themselves | States can cooperate genuinely |
| Agreements only last when the strong want them to | Institutions outlast the intentions of any one state |
| War is always possible | Interdependence makes war too expensive |
| Cambodia survives by picking the right powerful friend | Cambodia thrives by joining institutions that protect everyone |

---

## Which Is Right? / ណាជាការពិត?

Both, depending on the situation. Smart businesses — and smart countries — think about *both* at the same time:

- "The institution says we have rights" (liberal lens)
- "But does the powerful party in this situation want to honor those rights?" (realist lens)

If the answer to both is "yes" — invest confidently.
If the liberal rules exist but the power balance has shifted — be cautious.
If power dynamics have changed but the rules haven't caught up yet — that's where the biggest risks and opportunities live.
