# Realism vs. Liberalism: Three Analogies from Cambodian Life
# រេអាលីស ទល់នឹង លីបឺរ៉ាលីស៖ ការប្រៀបធៀបបីយ៉ាង

---

## Analogy 1: The Village Water Committee vs. The Strong Man's Well
## ការប្រៀបធៀបទី ១៖ គណៈកម្មការទឹក ប្រឆាំងនឹងស្ទឹងអ្នកមានអំណាច

**The Analogy:** A rural village in Kandal province has two water sources. One is the village's shared well, managed by a democratically elected water committee with rules about fair access, maintenance schedules, and dispute resolution. The other is a deeper well owned by the strongest, wealthiest family in the village — the Ly family — who allow access in exchange for loyalty and labor.

In dry season, the village well sometimes runs low. The Ly family's well never does. The rules of the committee say everyone has equal access to the shared well. But in practice, families who are close to the Ly family drink better.

**The Mapping:**
| Village Water | International Relations |
|--------------|------------------------|
| Village water committee | Liberal international institutions (WTO, UN, ASEAN) |
| Equal access rules | International law and norms |
| Ly family's private well | Great power's unilateral resources and patronage |
| Dry season shortage | Crisis situations when institutions are under stress |
| Families close to Ly family | Client states of great powers |
| Committee rules vs. Ly family preference | Liberal rules vs. Realist power |

**Why It Works:** In normal times, the committee's rules work fine — disputes are settled, everyone gets water. But in crisis, the power dynamic reasserts itself. Similarly, liberal institutions function well in stable times but buckle under great-power pressure. The WTO worked until the US decided its interests weren't being served.

**Where It Breaks Down:** The village is small enough that the Ly family can monitor and enforce their power personally. Global international relations involve too many actors for any single power to control completely — which is why liberal institutions persist even after great-power dominance wanes.

## Analogy 2: Trading at the Cambodia-Vietnam Border Market
## ការប្រៀបធៀបទី ២៖ ការជួញដូរនៅផ្សារព្រំដែន កម្ពុជា-វៀតណាម

**The Analogy:** At the Bavet-Moc Bai border crossing, there are two types of traders. Some have formal licenses, pay official duties, carry receipts, and rely on the trade agreements between Cambodia and Vietnam to protect them. Others have informal arrangements — they know the right officers, pay unofficial fees, and rely on personal relationships with border guards on both sides. 

In normal times, the formal traders have lower costs (official tariffs are lower than informal payments) and more legal protection. But when a new commander is assigned to the border post who doesn't honor the old relationships, the informal traders lose their advantage overnight. The formal traders, backed by treaty rules, continue unaffected.

**The Mapping:**
| Border Trading | Realism vs. Liberalism |
|---------------|----------------------|
| Formal licensed traders | States relying on liberal institutions and rules |
| Informal relationship traders | States relying on bilateral power relationships |
| Trade agreements between countries | Liberal international framework |
| New border commander | Leadership change / power shift |
| Formal traders' continuity | Institutional resilience under liberal order |
| Informal traders' sudden risk | Realist vulnerability when patrons change |

**Why It Works:** This analogy captures the *stability advantage* of liberal institutions — rules outlast individual relationships. But it also captures the *flexibility advantage* of power relationships — they can get things done that rules don't cover. The best border traders (and the best states) use both.

**Where It Breaks Down:** Border trading operates under ultimate Cambodian and Vietnamese state authority. In international relations, there is no higher authority — which is what makes the anarchy problem in realist theory genuinely different from domestic analogies.

## Analogy 3: The Angkor Conservation Consortium
## ការប្រៀបធៀបទី ៣៖ សមគម្មការពារអង្គរ

**The Analogy:** Multiple foreign countries contribute to Angkor's conservation under a UNESCO framework. Japan, China, India, France, Germany — each has been allocated specific temples or areas. The international coordination committee sets rules: environmental standards, documentation requirements, methodological protocols. These rules apply to everyone, regardless of how much money they contribute.

But in practice, a country that contributes more money gets more prestigious assignments. The most photogenic temple — Ta Prohm, with its famous tree roots — was assigned to India. Angkor Wat's central galleries, the most iconic, went to Japan. When a new country wants in, they negotiate not just through the formal UNESCO process but through diplomatic channels with Cambodia's Ministry of Culture, where political relationships matter as much as technical credentials.

**The Mapping:**
| Angkor Conservation | Realism vs. Liberalism |
|--------------------|----------------------|
| UNESCO coordination framework | Liberal multilateral institutions |
| Equal rules for all participating countries | International norms and law |
| Prestige assignments going to biggest donors | Realist power dynamics within liberal frameworks |
| Negotiating through formal UNESCO channels | Liberal pathway to access |
| Negotiating through Cambodia's political networks | Realist pathway to access |
| Both paths existing simultaneously | Hybrid liberal-realist reality |

**Why It Works:** This analogy captures the most sophisticated insight — that realism and liberalism are not opposites in practice. They coexist. The formal liberal rules set the floor for behavior; the realist power dynamics determine who gets the best floor positions. Savvy actors navigate both simultaneously.

**Where It Breaks Down:** Angkor conservation is relatively low-stakes — no one goes to war over temple restoration rights. The higher the stakes (military alliances, nuclear agreements, major trade frameworks), the more realist logic dominates and liberal institutional constraints weaken.
