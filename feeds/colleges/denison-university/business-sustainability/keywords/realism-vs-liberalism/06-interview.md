# Realism vs. Liberalism: A Cambodian Journalist Interviews an IR Scholar
# រេអាលីស ទល់នឹង លីបឺរ៉ាលីស៖ អ្នកសារព័ត៌មានខ្មែរសំភាសន៍អ្នកប្រាជ្ញ IR

*Journalist: Virak Noun, political affairs reporter, VOD (Voice of Democracy) Cambodia*
*Expert: Professor Tanaka Hiroshi, International Relations, Waseda University, Tokyo*

---

**Virak:** Professor Tanaka, most Cambodians who study international relations learn that there's a big debate between realism and liberalism. Can you summarize what's actually at stake in that debate?

**Professor Tanaka:** At its core, the debate is about what actually governs international behavior. Realism says: power. States pursue their national interest, and in an anarchic world without a global government, military and economic power determine outcomes. Rules and institutions matter, but only when the powerful want them to. Liberalism says: not only power, but also interdependence, institutions, and norms. Countries have strong incentives to cooperate because modern economies are deeply integrated, and breaking the rules is increasingly costly even for powerful states.

---

**Virak:** Which theory better explains what has happened to Cambodia over the past twenty years?

**Professor Tanaka:** It's an extraordinary case study for testing both theories simultaneously. The liberal story is this: Cambodia joined the WTO, received EBA trade preferences from the EU, integrated into global supply chains through garment manufacturing, and saw remarkable GDP growth. That's liberal theory in action — economic integration raising living standards. But the realist story is equally compelling: China's patron-client relationship with Cambodia's ruling elite has been the bedrock of regime stability. When the EU suspended EBA preferences to push for democracy, Cambodia moved closer to China, not toward liberal reform. Power — specifically the asymmetric dependency on Chinese patronage — overrode the liberal incentive structure.

---

**Virak:** Cambodia has been criticized for blocking ASEAN consensus statements on the South China Sea. How do you analyze that through these two frameworks?

**Professor Tanaka:** It's a textbook realist outcome inside a liberal institutional structure. ASEAN was designed with a consensus rule to protect every member's sovereignty — a very realist principle embedded in a liberal institution. When China's interests required Cambodia to block statements, Cambodia used that realist institutional design to achieve a realist objective. The liberal advocates who designed ASEAN's consensus mechanism created a vulnerability they didn't fully anticipate: any member could become a veto-player for a great power patron. Cambodia's behavior wasn't a failure of the liberal system — it was the realist logic that was always latent in it being activated.

---

**Virak:** Some argue that Chinese BRI investment in Cambodia represents a new form of what liberals call "complex interdependence" — that China and Cambodia are now so economically linked that conflict between them is too costly. Is that a liberal or realist reading?

**Professor Tanaka:** It's a fascinating case where the surface argument is liberal but the underlying dynamic is realist. Yes, economic interdependence exists. But the interdependence is *asymmetric* — Cambodia needs China more than China needs Cambodia. Asymmetric interdependence doesn't create the mutual deterrence that liberal theory predicts; it creates leverage for the dominant party. So China can threaten to withdraw investment from Cambodia far more credibly than Cambodia can threaten to withdraw political support from China. The liberal language of "mutual benefit" obscures a deeply realist power relationship.

---

**Virak:** What about Cambodia's smaller regional relationships — with Vietnam, Thailand, Laos? Are those more liberal or realist?

**Professor Tanaka:** They're complex, and historically shaped by violence. The Cambodia-Vietnam border has been fought over for centuries. ASEAN's liberal framework has stabilized these relationships considerably — trade, investment, and institutional norms have replaced military tension as the primary mode of interaction. This is actually one of liberalism's genuine successes in Southeast Asia: ASEAN has created a zone where territorial disputes are managed diplomatically rather than militarily. But the realist undercurrent remains — Vietnam's historical interest in Cambodian political outcomes, Thailand's economic leverage over its neighbors, Laos's dependence on China. The liberal institutions are real. So are the power differentials.

---

**Virak:** For a Cambodian entrepreneur thinking about international expansion — say, they want to export garments to Europe or invest in Vietnam — which theory should guide their planning?

**Professor Tanaka:** Both, as complementary analytical lenses. Start with realism for your risk assessment: Who has power in this market? What are the major players' actual interests? Where does my supply chain intersect with great-power competition? Can the institutions I'm relying on be overridden when powerful interests conflict? Then use liberalism for your opportunity assessment: What institutions can I leverage to protect my rights? Where does interdependence create stable, predictable relationships? What multilateral frameworks give my business rules that outlast any individual political leader? If realism tells you the risks you need to hedge, liberalism tells you the opportunities you can build on.

---

**Virak:** Final question. Does Cambodia have more agency than realism suggests?

**Professor Tanaka:** Much more. Small states are not simply passive recipients of great-power decisions. Cambodia has skillfully used its ASEAN membership, its cultural heritage, its geographic position, and its diplomatic flexibility to extract concessions and investments from multiple competing powers simultaneously. This is exactly what liberal theory predicts: institutions and multilateral engagement amplify small-state leverage. But Cambodia's agency operates within limits that realism identifies: the harder China's pressure, the less room Cambodia has. The trend in US-China rivalry is toward those limits shrinking. That's the strategic challenge of the next decade.
