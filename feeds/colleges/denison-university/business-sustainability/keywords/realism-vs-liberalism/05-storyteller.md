# The Diplomat's Daughter and the Trade Officer
# កូនស្រីអ្នកការទូត និង មន្ត្រីពាណិជ្ជកម្ម

*A story from between the sessions at an ASEAN summit*

---

Leakhena had spent four years at the National University of Singapore studying international relations, and she believed — genuinely, with the optimism of someone who had read all the right books — that international cooperation was the natural endpoint of human rationality.

She came back to Phnom Penh and joined the Ministry of Commerce as a junior trade officer. Her first major assignment was to help prepare Cambodia's position paper for an ASEAN trade negotiation on digital services. She spent three months on it. It was thorough, evidence-based, and carefully aligned with Cambodia's treaty commitments and WTO obligations.

Her supervisor, a fifty-year-old man named Director Vann, read it in six minutes and handed it back.

"This is the argument we *should* make," he said. "Now write me the argument we *will* make."

She asked him what the difference was.

"This," he said, tapping her document, "assumes everyone in the room will be persuaded by the best argument. That is not how the room works." He poured tea. "Thailand has a powerful digital economy lobby. Vietnam is protecting its state telecom companies. Malaysia is managing Chinese tech investment. Singapore wants to be the regional hub. None of them will vote for Cambodia's position because it is well-reasoned. They will vote for whatever serves their national interest."

"But we have legal commitments," she said. "The ASEAN Economic Community charter—"

"The charter says what everyone agreed to when it suited them. It doesn't enforce itself."

She went home that evening and stared at her textbooks. Keohane. Ikenberry. The literature on complex interdependence. She had believed — was still trying to believe — that institutions mattered, that rules created stable expectations, that the trading relationships Cambodia had built over two decades would function as advertised.

But two weeks later, she sat in the actual negotiation session and watched as Thailand used procedural motions to delay Cambodia's agenda items, not because Thailand had a legal argument, but because a Thai electronics firm had competing interests and the Thai delegation was protecting them. Vietnam made a speech about ASEAN solidarity and then voted against the proposal it had endorsed in writing three months earlier.

After the session, she found herself in a corridor conversation with a trade officer from the Philippine delegation — a woman named Isabel who had twenty years of ASEAN experience.

"You look like you just discovered something," Isabel said.

"I discovered that everything I was taught is slightly wrong," Leakhena said.

Isabel laughed. "Not wrong. Incomplete. The institutions are real. The rules are real. They constrain behavior at the margins. But the realists are right that states pursue their interests. And the liberals are right that institutions shape what states *believe* their interests are. The mistake is thinking it's either/or."

"So which framework should I use?"

"Use both, always, simultaneously. When you're in the room, use liberal logic — argue from principles, invoke treaty text, build coalitions. That's your public language. But before you enter the room, use realist logic — map every delegation's actual interest, identify who has power, find out which powerful patron is backing which position. That's your private preparation."

"That sounds exhausting."

"It is," said Isabel. "Welcome to trade diplomacy."

Leakhena rewrote her position paper that night. She kept the principled legal arguments. But she added a section she called *Strategic Considerations* — a clear-eyed map of every party's national interest, every bilateral relationship Cambodia needed to manage, and every point of leverage Cambodia had that wasn't mentioned in the WTO treaty text.

Director Vann read the revised version in twelve minutes. He nodded once.

"Now," he said, "this is the argument we will make."

---

*The tension Leakhena discovered had two names: Realism — the theory that power and national interest fundamentally drive international behavior — and Liberalism — the theory that institutions, interdependence, and norms genuinely constrain that behavior. Both are true. Neither is complete. The practitioner who ignores either one works with half a map.*
