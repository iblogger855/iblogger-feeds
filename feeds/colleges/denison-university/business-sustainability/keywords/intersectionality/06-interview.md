# Intersectionality — Journalist Interview
# អន្តរផ្នែកសង្គម — បទសម្ភាសន៍សារព័ត៌មាន

*Author: ichamrong | Date: 2026-06-01*

---

*Interview between Ratana Chan of the Phnom Penh Economic Tribune and Dr. Sokunthea Pich, a sociologist of labour and inequality.*

---

**Ratana Chan:** Dr. Sokunthea, "intersectionality" gets used a lot and understood a little. What's the actual idea?

**Dr. Sokunthea Pich:** It's deceptively simple. People hold many identities at once — gender, class, ethnicity, religion, age, disability — and the disadvantages tied to them don't just add up like separate weights. They interact and compound. A poor Indigenous woman doesn't experience poverty, gender, and ethnicity as three tidy, separable burdens. They braid together into one distinct situation that none of the three describes on its own. Kimberlé Crenshaw coined the term in 1989, studying Black women who were failed by courts that could only think one category at a time.

**Ratana:** Can you explain that court failure? It's the origin of the whole concept.

**Dr. Sokunthea Pich:** Courts would ask: "Were Black men discriminated against here? No. Were white women? No. Then there's no discrimination." But the discrimination was aimed specifically at Black women — a group that fell into the gap between "race" cases (centred on Black men) and "sex" cases (centred on white women). The single-axis framework literally made the most affected people invisible. That invisibility is the heart of intersectionality: the people at the crossing fall through frameworks built around one road at a time.

**Ratana:** Why isn't it enough to just add the disadvantages together?

**Dr. Sokunthea Pich:** Because the axes reshape each other. A wealthy woman and a poor woman face different *kinds* of sexism. A poor man and a poor woman face different *kinds* of class hardship. When you're a poor woman, the gender and class don't sit side by side — they twist into something specific. It's less like stacking bricks and more like mixing paint: blue and yellow make green, a new colour you can't pull apart. The intersection is its own position, not a sum.

**Ratana:** How does this connect to the sustainability themes of this program — environment and labour?

**Dr. Sokunthea Pich:** Directly, because harm never falls evenly. When a river is polluted or a forest cleared, who pays most? Very often a poor, rural, ethnic-minority woman: she depends most directly on the land for water and food, carries the care work when a child gets sick, has the least money to relocate, and the least power to be heard. Three or four axes converge on her. A response framed only as "helping the poor" or only as "Indigenous land rights" or only as "women's empowerment" each captures a slice and misses the person. Environmental justice without intersectionality systematically undercounts the worst-affected.

**Ratana:** Give me the Cambodian version of that.

**Dr. Sokunthea Pich:** Take a young woman from a highland Indigenous community whose family lost land to a concession and who migrated to a garment factory. She faces gender — low-paid "women's work" and harassment risk; class — no capital, wholly wage-dependent; migrant status — no urban networks or fallback; and ethnicity — language barriers and discrimination. Each shapes the others. The harassment is harder to report because of the language; the poverty is sharper because the land that would have caught her is gone. A program for "factory women" run in fluent urban Khmer can sail right past her. Designing for the intersection — a liaison in her language, a grievance channel that doesn't assume city networks — is what actually reaches her.

**Ratana:** Is there a risk the concept just becomes a fashionable label?

**Dr. Sokunthea Pich:** Yes, and that's the real danger — using "intersectional" as a slogan while still designing single-axis programs. The discipline is in the doing: when you build a policy, a factory grievance system, or a relief effort, you ask "who lives where these categories cross, and does our design actually fit them?" If the answer is no, you've named intersectionality without practising it. The test is always whether the person at the crossing is finally visible in your design.

**Ratana:** Final question. One thing for a young Cambodian to carry from this?

**Dr. Sokunthea Pich:** That no one is ever just one thing, and the people everyone else overlooks are usually standing exactly where several disadvantages meet. Learn to see all of a person at once, and you'll see the people the single-issue world walks past.

**Ratana:** Thank you, Dr. Sokunthea.
