# Intersectionality — Feynman Explanation
# អន្តរផ្នែកសង្គម — ការពន្យល់បែប Feynman

*Author: ichamrong | Date: 2026-06-01*

---

Picture a road intersection — two roads crossing. Cars come from both directions. If you're standing right in the middle of the crossing and there's an accident, it could be a car from the first road, or the second road, or both at once smashing into you from two sides. And if someone asks "which road caused the accident?", the honest answer might be: *both, together, in a way neither road alone explains.*

That picture is where the word **intersectionality** comes from. Kimberlé Crenshaw used exactly this image. The "roads" are different parts of who you are — being a woman, being poor, being from a minority group. Where they cross, you can get hit from several directions at once.

---

Here's the core idea in one sentence: **the disadvantages a person faces don't just add up like separate weights — they combine and feed each other, creating a situation none of them describes on its own.**

Let me show you why "adding up" is wrong.

Suppose we study discrimination. One study looks only at *women* and finds they're paid less. Another looks only at *poor people* and finds they're stuck in bad jobs. You might think a poor woman just gets both problems, stacked: woman-penalty plus poor-penalty.

But it doesn't work that cleanly. A rich woman and a poor woman face *different* kinds of sexism. A poor man and a poor woman face *different* kinds of class struggle. When you're a poor woman, the two things twist together into something specific — a position that the "women" study missed (because it mostly saw richer women) and the "poor people" study missed (because it mostly saw poor men).

---

Now the dangerous part: **people at the crossing become invisible.**

Crenshaw studied real court cases. Black women were turned away because the court said: "We checked — Black *men* weren't discriminated against here, and white *women* weren't either, so there's no discrimination." But the harm was aimed precisely at Black *women* — a group that fell into the gap between the two boxes. The categories were drawn in a way that erased the very people sitting where they overlap.

---

Why does this matter for, say, a polluted river or a hard factory job? Because harm doesn't fall evenly. When a forest is cleared, who suffers most? Often a poor, rural, minority *woman* — she depends on the land for food and water, has the least money to move, the least power to complain, and the most work caring for a family that just lost its livelihood. Three or four "roads" cross right on her. A program that helps only "the poor" or only "women" will keep missing her.

---

**The simple version:**

You are never just one thing. You're a woman *and* a worker *and* maybe a minority *and* poor — all at once. Intersectionality says: don't study these one at a time and add them up, because they blend and amplify each other, and the people standing right at the crossing — who suffer the most — are exactly the ones a one-box-at-a-time view can't even see.
