# Intersectionality — Analogy Bridge
# អន្តរផ្នែកសង្គម — ស្ពានប្រៀបធៀប

*Author: ichamrong | Date: 2026-06-01*

---

## Analogy 1: The Road Crossing / ផ្លូវប្រសព្វ

**The Story:**
Crenshaw's own image: stand in the middle of a crossroads where traffic flows from several directions. If you're struck, the harm might come from one road, another, or several at once. A doctor who insists on labelling the injury as caused by "only the north road" or "only the east road" will misdiagnose someone hit from two sides. A person standing where gender, class, and ethnicity cross can be harmed by all of them together — and an analyst who insists on one road at a time will misread the wound.

**The Mapping:**

| Analogy Element | Intersectionality Element | ខ្មែរ |
|---|---|---|
| Each road | An axis of identity (gender, class…) | អ័ក្សអត្តសញ្ញាណ |
| Standing at the crossing | Living at the intersection | រស់នៅចំណុចប្រសព្វ |
| Hit from several roads | Compounded disadvantage | គុណវិបត្តិគរបន្ថែម |
| "Only one road" diagnosis | Single-axis analysis | ការវិភាគអ័ក្សតែមួយ |

**Where the Analogy Breaks Down:**
Roads are separate physical things; identities are fused in one person and continuously reshape each other, so the "roads" aren't really independent the way streets are.

## Analogy 2: Mixing Colours, Not Stacking Bricks / ការលាយពណ៌ មិនមែនការគរឥដ្ឋ

**The Story:**
If disadvantages simply stacked, they'd be like bricks — two bricks are just twice one brick, and you can pull them apart. But identities behave more like mixing paint. Blue and yellow don't sit side by side as "blue plus yellow"; they become *green* — a new colour you can't separate back out. A poor Indigenous woman's experience isn't "poverty brick + gender brick + ethnicity brick." It's a single blended colour with its own distinct shade that none of the original tins contained.

**The Mapping:**

| Analogy Element | Intersectionality Element | ខ្មែរ |
|---|---|---|
| Stacking bricks | The additive (wrong) model | គំរូបូក (ខុស) |
| Mixing paint into a new colour | Compounding interaction | អន្តរកម្មគរបន្ថែម |
| The new shade (green) | Distinct intersectional position | ទីតាំងប្រសព្វដាច់ដោយឡែក |
| Can't unmix the paint | Can't reduce to single axes | មិនអាចបំបែកជាអ័ក្សតែមួយ |

**Where the Analogy Breaks Down:**
Paint mixes to a single uniform colour; a person's identities can stay distinct and even conflict in different settings, so the blend isn't always smooth or fixed.

## Analogy 3: A Net With Different-Sized Holes / សំណាញ់ដែលមានរន្ធទំហំខុសគ្នា

**The Story:**
Imagine social programs as nets meant to catch people who are falling. One net is woven to catch "women." Another to catch "the poor." Another for "ethnic minorities." Each net has holes shaped by what it ignores. A person who is a poor minority woman can fall straight through *all* of them — too poor for the women's net (designed around better-off women), too female for the poverty net (designed around men), too rural and minority for both. The very people most in need slip through the gaps between single-issue nets.

**The Mapping:**

| Analogy Element | Intersectionality Element | ខ្មែរ |
|---|---|---|
| Each net | A single-axis program | កម្មវិធីអ័ក្សតែមួយ |
| Shape of the holes | What each program overlooks | អ្វីដែលកម្មវិធីមើលរំលង |
| Falling through all nets | Intersectional invisibility | ភាពមើលមិនឃើញនៃប្រសព្វ |
| A net woven to fit the gaps | Intersectional policy design | ការរចនាគោលនយោបាយប្រសព្វ |

**Where the Analogy Breaks Down:**
A net is passive; real institutions can actively discriminate, so the gaps aren't only accidental oversights but sometimes deliberate exclusions.

---

## Core Insight Across All Three Analogies / ការយល់ដឹងស្នូល

All three show the same principle: **overlapping identities don't add like bricks — they blend, compound, and create distinct positions that single-category thinking cannot see**. Whether it's the crossroads, the mixed paint, or the gaps between nets, the lesson is that the people standing where the categories meet are simultaneously the most burdened and the most easily overlooked — which is precisely why intersectionality is indispensable for fair labour and environmental policy.
