# Geopolitical Risk: A Cambodian Journalist Interviews a Strategist
# ហានិភ័យភូមិសាស្ត្រនយោបាយ៖ អ្នកសារព័ត៌មានខ្មែរសំភាសន៍អ្នកយុទ្ធសាស្ត្រ

*Journalist: Channara Ros, foreign affairs correspondent, Radio Free Asia Khmer Service*
*Expert: Dr. Maria Santos, Southeast Asia Geopolitical Strategist, Institute for Strategic and International Studies, Kuala Lumpur*

---

**Channara:** Dr. Santos, Cambodia is often described as being too close to China. What does that actually mean for businesses operating here?

**Dr. Santos:** It means businesses here are operating in a geopolitically contested environment. China's BRI investment — the Sihanoukville port, the SEZs, the road infrastructure — has created deep economic ties that also carry strategic implications. For businesses, this translates to three layers of risk: US and EU compliance risk (are your Cambodia supply chains inadvertently connected to Chinese military or surveillance entities?), trade preference risk (will Cambodia's preferential access to Western markets continue?), and reputational risk (do your shareholders and consumers care that you source from a country perceived as enabling authoritarian alignment?).

---

**Channara:** Let's talk about the Ream Naval Base specifically. How significant is it geopolitically?

**Dr. Santos:** It's a symptom more than a cause. The US and its partners are concerned because a Chinese military presence on the Gulf of Thailand would give China strategic access to important shipping lanes — the same lanes through which a significant share of global energy supplies move. If China can project naval power from Ream, it changes the strategic calculus across the entire South China Sea and into the Malacca Strait. For businesses, this matters because any military escalation in those waters — even below the threshold of war — would affect insurance costs, shipping times, and investor risk premiums for all of Southeast Asia.

---

**Channara:** You mentioned the Malacca Strait. How does Cambodia fit into the broader Indo-Pacific geopolitical picture?

**Dr. Santos:** Cambodia sits at the pivot of ASEAN. ASEAN operates on consensus — meaning any one member can block a statement. Cambodia has repeatedly, as a Chinese-aligned ASEAN chair and member, prevented ASEAN from issuing strong language on the South China Sea. This matters enormously for businesses because ASEAN's institutional credibility as a rule-based framework for investment is partly contingent on it being able to take collective positions. When that credibility erodes, the risk premium for all Southeast Asian markets increases.

---

**Channara:** Has Cambodia tried to manage this geopolitical position? Does it have any strategic agency?

**Dr. Santos:** More than it's given credit for. Cambodia's traditional foreign policy — what Hun Sen called "omni-enmeshment" — is actually a sophisticated small-state strategy: engage all major powers simultaneously, create mutual dependencies, avoid binding alliances. The problem is that this strategy becomes harder to execute as the US-China rivalry intensifies. When great powers demand clearer alignment, the middle position shrinks. Cambodia has been pushed further toward China by the EBA suspensions and US pressure, which ironically reinforces the dependency that Western critics were worried about.

---

**Channara:** Cambodian garment workers — mostly women — were among those hurt by the EBA partial suspension. How should we think about that from a geopolitical risk perspective?

**Dr. Santos:** It's the cruelest demonstration of how geopolitical risk is distributed. The decision-makers in Brussels were responding to the political behavior of Cambodia's elite. The garment workers had no say in that political behavior. But they bore significant economic consequences — factory closures, reduced hours, pressure on wages. The geopolitical risk was absorbed by the most economically vulnerable actors, not by the political actors who generated it. This is not unusual — it's structurally typical of how geopolitical costs are distributed in small, export-dependent economies.

---

**Channara:** What is Angkor Wat's role in Cambodia's geopolitical positioning?

**Dr. Santos:** Angkor is Cambodia's greatest diplomatic asset. Restoration partnerships with Japan, China, India, France, Germany — these are not purely philanthropic. Each country is investing in cultural capital that translates into diplomatic goodwill. Japan's decades-long restoration work at Angkor Wat has built a level of trust and positive association that Japanese businesses and diplomats benefit from. China's restoration of Baphuon and other sites serves similar purposes. Cambodia is actually quite skillful at leveraging Angkor as a soft-power multiplier — the question is whether that leverage is sufficient to maintain space between the great powers as they compete more aggressively.

---

**Channara:** Final question. If you were advising the Cambodian government on geopolitical risk management — not a foreign company, but Cambodia itself — what would you recommend?

**Dr. Santos:** Three things. First, diversify economic dependencies — reduce the concentration of Chinese investment as a share of total FDI, not by rejecting China, but by actively courting Japanese, Korean, Indian, and Gulf investment in parallel. Second, strengthen institutions — independent courts, functioning legislature, free press — not just because it's right, but because institutional quality is the best single insurance policy against geopolitical vulnerability. Countries with strong institutions can negotiate from positions of credibility. Third, invest in ASEAN — a stronger, more credible ASEAN provides Cambodia with collective cover that no bilateral relationship can match. Small states thrive when multilateral rules work. They suffer most when great powers bypass them.
