# The Bridge at the Edge of Two Empires
# ស្ពានខ្ទង់ជ្រោះដើមទ្វីប

*A story from the Cambodia-Vietnam border*

---

Pheakdei's grandmother always said the river didn't belong to anyone.

She had grown up in a village near Bavet, on the Cambodian side of the border with Vietnam, where the Mekong tributaries braided through flat paddies on their way toward the delta. When she was young, the border barely existed in practice — her family sold dried fish in Moc Bai, bought Vietnamese medicines, had cousins across the water who came for weddings and left before dark. The line on the map was an administrative detail, not a lived reality.

By the time Pheakdei was twenty-five, the line had become everything.

He ran a small import-export business — dry goods, electronics, motorcycle parts — out of a warehouse near the Bavet crossing. The border checkpoint had become a Special Economic Zone, part of Cambodia's development corridor with international investment. Chinese and Korean factories had opened on both sides. Container trucks moved through day and night.

Business was excellent. Until it wasn't.

In 2019, the United States began its trade war with China. In 2020, it added more restrictions. A Chinese company that manufactured components in a factory in Bavet — supplying inputs to Pheakdei's import chain — found itself on a US export restriction list. The factory continued operating. The goods were technically Cambodian-origin, not Chinese. But the compliance teams at the American retail company downstream said: *"We can't risk it. Too much exposure."*

Then, in 2022, Cambodia's relationship with China deepened further. The Ream Naval Base modifications became public knowledge. American congresspeople gave speeches. An EU parliamentarian visited Phnom Penh and said Cambodia was becoming a *"satellite of Beijing."* The EBA trade preference suspension, already partial, was under review for further tightening.

Pheakdei watched his Vietnamese counterparts — suppliers and buyers across the border — navigate differently. Vietnam had cultivated relationships with both the US and China. It had participated in US-led trade frameworks while maintaining close ties with Beijing. It had positioned itself as the safe harbor when companies needed to shift production out of China. In 2022 and 2023, supply chains that would once have passed through Cambodia began diverting north, through Vietnam.

He tried to explain this to his brother-in-law, Ratha, who worked at the provincial planning office.

"It's not about us," he said. "We didn't do anything. Our goods are fine. Our prices are competitive. It's just that we're on the wrong side of a line that someone else drew."

"What line?" Ratha asked.

"The line between Washington's friends and Beijing's friends. And we're on the Beijing side of that line right now. Which means their sanctions and their trade restrictions apply to us — or to the companies that buy from us. So they move somewhere else."

Ratha was quiet for a moment. Then he said: "So the river does belong to someone after all."

Pheakdei thought about that for a long time. His grandmother was right that the river itself was indifferent. But the routes along the river — the roads, the checkpoints, the trade corridors, the diplomatic permissions that allowed goods to move — those belonged to a complicated web of great-power interests, and small countries like Cambodia had to navigate that web without ever having designed it.

He started diversifying. He opened a new channel with Thai partners. He explored Malaysian certification for some products. He kept his Chinese supply relationships but began auditing which inputs could be sourced locally or from ASEAN alternatives.

It wasn't comfortable. It wasn't as profitable, at first. But he had finally understood something his economics textbooks hadn't quite captured: your supply chain doesn't just connect factories. It connects *alignments*. And alignments, in a world where great powers compete, are never neutral.

---

*The risk Pheakdei encountered had a name: geopolitical risk — the way that strategic competition between great powers reshapes the trade and investment landscape for small economies and ordinary businesses that never asked to be part of anyone's grand strategy.*
