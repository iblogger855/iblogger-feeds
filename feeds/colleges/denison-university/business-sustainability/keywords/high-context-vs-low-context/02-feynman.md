# High-Context vs. Low-Context Communication — Feynman Explanation
# ការទំនាក់ទំនងបរិបទខ្ពស់ ទល់ ទាប — ការពន្យល់បែប Feynman

*Author: ichamrong | Date: 2026-05-29*

---

Imagine you are sitting with your grandmother at dinner. She puts your favorite dish on the table. She says nothing. She just puts it in front of you.

You understand immediately: she knows you had a hard day. She is saying "I love you" and "I am here for you" without saying a single word.

Now imagine explaining this scene to someone who grew up in Sweden or Germany and has never met your grandmother. They might say: "But she didn't say anything. How do you know what she meant?"

This is the difference between **high-context (HC)** and **low-context (LC)** communication.

---

**High-context communication (ការទំនាក់ទំនងបរិបទខ្ពស់)** means that most of the meaning is *not* in the words — it is in the relationship, the setting, what everyone already knows, what is *not* said, the tone, the timing, and the social position of the people involved.

Cambodia is a high-context country. Japan, Korea, China, and most of Southeast Asia are also high-context.

**Low-context communication (ការទំនាក់ទំនងបរិបទទាប)** means that most of the meaning *is* in the words — explicit, direct, written down, stated clearly. What you say is what you mean, and nothing important is left unsaid.

Germany, the Netherlands, and the United States tend to be more low-context.

---

**A Cambodian example that everyone knows:**

In Cambodia, if you ask someone "តើអ្នកចង់ទៅញ៉ាំអ្វីទេ?" (Do you want to eat something?) and they say "ចុះថ្ងៃ អ្នក?" (How about you first?), they are almost certainly saying "yes, I am hungry, but I do not want to seem too eager." You are supposed to understand this without them saying it.

If a German colleague asked the same question and got that answer, they might think: "They are not sure. Maybe they do not want to eat." They would take the words at face value.

**The Cambodian person was communicating. The German person was not receiving the full message.** That is a high-context to low-context communication failure.

---

**Why does this matter in business?**

Imagine a Japanese company is negotiating an investment with a Cambodian government agency. The Japanese team says: "We will need to consider this carefully." In Japanese business culture, this almost always means "no" — but politely, without causing embarrassment.

A Cambodian official who is used to indirect communication might understand this as a soft "no" and respond accordingly. But an American lawyer on the same team might say: "Great, how long do you need to consider? Can we schedule a follow-up for next Thursday?" — because in his culture, "we need to consider this" means exactly those words, nothing more.

The Japanese team feels confused. The American seems to not understand the message. The Cambodian is caught between the two.

---

**The key insight:**

There is no "correct" communication style. High-context works beautifully within communities that share the same cultural knowledge. Low-context works beautifully when you need to be precise across different backgrounds — legal contracts, technical documents.

Problems happen when people assume everyone is using the same code.

The solution is not to change your culture. It is to **recognize that other people might be using a different code, and learn to switch consciously when the context requires it**.
