# Oligopoly — Journalist Interview
# អូលីហ្គោប៉ូលី — បទសម្ភាសន៍សារព័ត៌មាន

*Author: ichamrong | Date: 2026-06-01*

---

*Interview between Ratana Chan of the Phnom Penh Economic Tribune and Dr. Sokunthea Pich, industrial-organization economist at the Royal University of Agriculture.*

---

**Ratana Chan:** Dr. Sokunthea, what exactly makes a market an "oligopoly" rather than ordinary competition?

**Dr. Sokunthea Pich:** Numbers and awareness. An oligopoly is a market dominated by a *few* firms — large enough that each one knows its own decisions move the market, and that its rivals are watching. In a vegetable market, no seller cares what the seller two stalls down charges. In an oligopoly — fuel, cement, telecoms, airlines — every major player obsessively tracks the others. That mutual awareness, what we call strategic interdependence, is the defining feature.

**Ratana:** Why should ordinary consumers care?

**Dr. Sokunthea Pich:** Because oligopoly is where a lot of the prices in your life are actually set. Your mobile data, your bag of cement, your tank of petrol. These markets often produce prices higher than vigorous competition would — not because anyone is necessarily breaking the law, but because a few firms naturally learn to avoid bruising price wars. They reach an unspoken truce. Consumers pay for that quiet.

**Ratana:** Is that truce illegal? It sounds like collusion.

**Dr. Sokunthea Pich:** There's a crucial line. *Explicit* collusion — secret meetings, agreed prices, a cartel — is illegal in most countries. But *tacit* collusion, where firms simply observe each other and independently choose not to start a price war, is very hard to prosecute because no agreement exists. It's just rational behavior repeated over time. The same few firms, facing each other year after year, learn that peace is more profitable than war.

**Ratana:** And yet price wars do happen.

**Dr. Sokunthea Pich:** They do, and they're dramatic. The whole structure sits on a knife-edge that economists model as a prisoners' dilemma. Every firm would do best if all held prices high — but each is individually tempted to undercut and grab market share. The moment one cracks, the others must follow or lose customers, and prices can collapse toward cost. Consumers love these episodes; the firms dread them. Often a war ends with one player exiting, and the survivors quietly rebuild the high-price truce.

**Ratana:** How does oligopoly connect to sustainability, the theme of this program?

**Dr. Sokunthea Pich:** Most powerfully through energy. The global fossil-fuel industry is a textbook oligopoly — a handful of national oil companies plus the OPEC cartel coordinating output to manage prices. That concentration is a double-edged sword for the climate. Incumbents can use their power to defend fossil assets and slow the transition. But concentration also means that if a few dominant players decide to pivot to renewables together, they can move the entire market with startling speed. Few hands on the wheel can steer toward catastrophe or toward transition.

**Ratana:** Does Cambodia have meaningful oligopolies?

**Dr. Sokunthea Pich:** Several. Cement, beer, telecoms, and fuel distribution are each dominated by a small number of firms. A telltale sign is "sticky" pricing — when global crude falls, our pump prices often drift down slowly and partially, because no firm wants to be the one to trigger a cut that forces everyone's margins down. That asymmetry — quick to rise, slow to fall — is a classic oligopoly fingerprint.

**Ratana:** What should a young entrepreneur take from this?

**Dr. Sokunthea Pich:** Understand the structure of the market you're entering. If a few entrenched giants dominate, a head-on price war is usually suicidal — they can outlast you, as many a hopeful newcomer has discovered. The smarter path is often differentiation: serve a niche, or build something genuinely new, rather than fighting incumbents on price they can crush you on. And if you ever become one of the few, remember that the regulator and the public are watching how you use that power.

**Ratana:** One sentence to remember?

**Dr. Sokunthea Pich:** In an oligopoly, a handful of players hold the market in their hands — and whether they cooperate or clash shapes not just prices, but, increasingly, the planet's energy future.

**Ratana:** Thank you, Dr. Sokunthea.
