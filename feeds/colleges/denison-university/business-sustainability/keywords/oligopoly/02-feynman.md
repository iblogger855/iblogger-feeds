# Oligopoly — Feynman Explanation
# អូលីហ្គោប៉ូលី — ការពន្យល់បែប Feynman

*Author: ichamrong | Date: 2026-06-01*

---

Imagine one small town with just three petrol stations and no others for fifty kilometres. Not a hundred sellers, not one — three. That's an **oligopoly** (អូលីហ្គោប៉ូលី): a market run by a *few* big sellers.

Now here's the whole drama, and it's a drama, because three is a magic, dangerous number.

---

Each station owner wakes up and stares at the same question: what price should I post today? But unlike a vegetable seller lost among hundreds, this owner knows something heavy — *whatever I do, the other two are watching, and they will react.*

If I drop my price to steal customers, the other two will see my new sign within the hour and drop theirs too. Now we all have the old customers back, but everyone earns less. I started a fight and we all lost.

So the three of them, even without ever meeting, slowly learn: *let's just all keep prices high and leave each other alone.* Nobody signs anything. They just... match each other. That quiet truce is called **tacit collusion**.

---

But the truce is fragile, and here is why.

Picture the three owners as prisoners who'd be better off staying silent together — but each one is tempted to betray for a private reward. Each station owner thinks: "If I secretly drop my price just a little while the others keep theirs high, I'll grab all the customers and get *rich*."

Every one of them thinks this. And the moment one cracks and cuts the price, the others must cut too or lose everyone. The truce shatters and you get a **price war** — prices tumbling, everyone bleeding money, customers briefly delighted.

This tug between "let's all keep prices high" and "but I could win big by cheating" is the beating heart of oligopoly. Economists call the trap a **prisoners' dilemma**.

---

So where does the price actually land? Somewhere in the middle. Higher than if there were a hundred fierce competitors. Lower than if there were a single monopoly. It wobbles between cosy truce and brutal war, depending on whether the owners trust each other to behave.

---

**The simple version:**

An oligopoly is a few big sellers locked in a staring contest. Each could earn more by keeping prices high together, but each is tempted to cheat and undercut. When the truce holds, prices stay high and customers pay more. When someone breaks ranks, a price war erupts. The whole subject is really one question: *will the few players cooperate, or stab each other in the back?* That's why oligopoly is where economics meets game theory.
