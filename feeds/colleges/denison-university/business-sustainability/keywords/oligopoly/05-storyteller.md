## The Three Ice Factories of the Delta Town / រោងចក្រទឹកកកបីនៅទីក្រុងដីសណ្ត

In a fishing town on the Tonle Sap, ice was life. The catch spoiled in hours under that sun, so every fisherman, every collector, every market stall needed blocks of ice each dawn. And in that whole town there were exactly three ice factories: Sok's, Mealea's, and the old Chan family works by the jetty.

Not one. Not a hundred. Three.

For years the three owners had reached, without ever sitting at a table, a comfortable understanding. Ice sold at 2,000 riel a block at all three. Each kept its loyal customers. Each made a steady, easy profit. They nodded to one another at weddings and funerals and never spoke of price, because they did not need to — they simply watched, and matched.

This is the first thing the story teaches: when only a few sellers share a market, they do not need a contract to keep prices high. They only need to watch each other and refuse to be the one who breaks the calm.

Then came the year a fourth man, Bunthoeun, back from working in Thailand — bought a failing cold-store and decided he would *win* the town. He cut his block to 1,400 riel and painted it huge on a board by the road.

Sok saw the board by mid-morning. His customers were already drifting toward Bunthoeun's gate. By noon Sok dropped to 1,300. Mealea, watching her loyal stalls vanish, went to 1,100 before dark. The Chan works, oldest and proudest, refused for one day, lost half its buyers, and then crashed its own price to 1,000.

By the end of the week ice sold at 700 riel a block — barely above the cost of the diesel to freeze it. The fishermen rejoiced; they had never bought ice so cheap. But the four factories bled. Bunthoeun, who had started it, discovered his cold-store could not survive the very war he had lit. Within two months he sold out and went back to Thailand.

This is the second thing the story teaches: in an oligopoly the temptation to cheat the truce is enormous — grab everyone's customers — but the moment one cheats, the others must follow, and the price war that follows can ruin the very person who started it.

The three survivors emerged from the war exhausted. And slowly, without a word, the old understanding crept back. 1,000. Then 1,400. Within a season, 2,000 a block again, the three of them nodding at the next wedding as if nothing had happened.

A young woman home from university watched all this and said to her father, Sok, "It's a prisoners' dilemma, Pa. You'd all do best holding prices high together, but each of you is tempted to undercut, and when one does, you all lose."

Sok lit a cigarette and looked at the river. "We didn't have a word for it," he said. "But yes. The trick of three factories is never being the fool who swings first — and never trusting that the others won't."
