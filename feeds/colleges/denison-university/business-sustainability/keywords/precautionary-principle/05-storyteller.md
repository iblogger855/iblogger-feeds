## The Engineer Who Listened to the Fishermen / វិស្វករដែលស្ដាប់ចំណែកអ្នកនេសាទ

Sophearith had worked for the Ministry of Water Resources for eleven years before they assigned him to the environmental assessment for the proposed dam on the Sesan River in Ratanakiri.

He was a thorough man. He ran the hydraulic models. He reviewed the geological surveys. He examined the downstream flood-risk calculations. His 200-page report took fourteen months to produce.

But there was one section he almost did not write.

In his third month on the project, he visited a cluster of Jarai indigenous villages in the Sesan valley. He had gone to collect water-flow data at a monitoring station near the river bend. While he was there, a group of older men — fishermen, most of them — gathered to watch him work. One of them, a man named Bun Reth who was perhaps seventy and who had fished the Sesan since childhood, asked what the monitoring station was for.

Sophearith told him about the dam.

Bun Reth was quiet for a long time. Then he said: "The big fish come through here in April. They have always come through here in April. My father's father fished for them here. The same fish, the same month."

Sophearith nodded politely. He knew about the fish. The ecological models predicted a 15–20% reduction in fish biomass downstream of the dam. This was within accepted international parameters for large dam projects.

"If the fish stop coming," Bun Reth said, "what do we eat?"

"You would likely adapt," Sophearith said. "There would be aquaculture opportunities in the reservoir—"

"We do not know aquaculture. We know this river."

Sophearith finished his data collection and drove back to his lodgings in Banlung.

He almost did not include what Bun Reth had said in his report. It was qualitative, anecdotal, not peer-reviewed. His models were peer-reviewed. The 15–20% figure came from a reputable international consultancy.

But something stayed with him.

He knew that fish migration in the Sesan was not a simple system. The Sesan connected to the Mekong. The Mekong connected to the Tonle Sap. The fish populations were interlinked in ways that no model fully captured. The "15–20%" figure had confidence intervals that stretched from 5% to 65% — a range so wide it was nearly meaningless for planning purposes.

He knew that the Jarai communities had no alternative water source, no alternative protein source, and no savings to transition. If the disruption was at the high end of the confidence interval — 60%, 65% — the communities would face food insecurity, and there would be no way to restore the fish migration.

He spent three weeks writing a section in his report titled: *"Irreversible Impacts Under High-Uncertainty Scenarios."*

He argued that the confidence intervals were too wide to treat the 15–20% figure as reliable. He argued that the downstream communities faced a potentially irreversible catastrophic impact if the high-end scenario materialized. He recommended a minimum two-year delay for further ecological study, and that the dam design be modified to include a fish passage corridor.

His supervisor told him the delay was not feasible. The financing was already secured. The contractor was already mobilized.

Sophearith attached a formal dissent to his report and submitted it anyway.

The dam was built on schedule. Sophearith was assigned to a different department.

Eight years later, a major downstream fishery survey found that migratory fish species in the Sesan had declined by 48% — well above the 20% maximum in the consultancy's central estimate. Bun Reth's village had relocated twice.

The survey report cited, in a footnote, a 2018 environmental assessment that had flagged the uncertainty of fish impact modeling and recommended additional study before construction. The footnote was followed by the assessment's author: S. Chhun, Ministry of Water Resources.

No one called Sophearith to apologize. No one called Bun Reth to explain.

But a junior researcher who read the footnote tracked down the original assessment. She wrote a widely-shared analysis about the difference between scientific certainty and the precautionary logic that should govern decisions where irreversible harm is possible and where vulnerable communities bear the cost of being wrong.

She called the principle she was describing one of the oldest ideas in environmental ethics, formalized by a philosopher named Hans Jonas: when the stakes are permanent, caution is not weakness — it is the only rational position.
