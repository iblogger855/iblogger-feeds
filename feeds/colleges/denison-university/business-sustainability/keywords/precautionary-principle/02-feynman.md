# Precautionary Principle — Feynman Explanation
# គោលការណ៍ប្រុងប្រយ័ត្ន — ការពន្យល់បែប Feynman

*Author: ichamrong | Date: 2026-05-29*

---

Imagine you are crossing the Old Kampot Bridge over the Tek Chhou River. The bridge is old. You are not sure it is safe.

Now, someone says: "We have not *proven* that it will fall. So just walk across."

And you say: "But we have not *proven* that it is safe either."

Who is right?

The question is: **what happens if you are wrong?**

If you walk across and the bridge is fine — good, you saved time.

If you walk across and the bridge collapses — you are dead. And you cannot undo that.

Now, if you wait and check the bridge first, and it turns out to be safe — you lost twenty minutes.

If you wait and check, and it turns out the bridge really was dangerous — you saved your life.

The **precautionary principle (គោលការណ៍ប្រុងប្រយ័ត្ន)** says: **when the worst possible outcome is irreversible and catastrophic, be careful first and prove it is safe before proceeding — do not wait to prove it is dangerous.**

---

**Why is this hard for people to accept?**

Because we are used to a different rule: "Don't worry until you have proof there is a problem."

That rule works fine for small, reversible problems. If you eat a new kind of fruit and your stomach hurts, you stop eating it. No permanent damage.

But it does not work for big, irreversible problems. You cannot un-extinct a species. You cannot un-contaminate an underground water source that a million people drink from. You cannot restore a river ecosystem once the fish are gone.

For those situations, waiting for proof of harm is waiting too long.

---

**A Cambodian example:**

Think about the fish in the Tonle Sap (ទន្លេសាប). The Tonle Sap is connected to the Mekong and floods every rainy season. The fish swim from the river into the flooded forests, grow up, and swim back when the water drops. This cycle has fed Cambodians for thousands of years.

When engineers proposed building large dams on the Mekong, some scientists said: "We don't know exactly how this will affect the fish migration — the Mekong system is very complex. We need more research."

Some decision-makers said: "We don't have proof the dam will harm the fish. So let's build it."

The precautionary principle would say the opposite: "We don't have proof the dam WON'T harm the fish — and if it does, the harm may be permanent. So the builders should prove it is safe first."

Once the dam is built and the fish migration is disrupted, you cannot take the dam apart.

---

**The easy version to remember:**

> "Better safe than sorry — especially when 'sorry' means permanent damage."

The precautionary principle is not about being paralyzed by fear. It is about asking: **who bears the burden of proof?**

Regular situations: the person warning about danger must prove the danger.

Big, irreversible situations: the person proposing the action must prove it is safe.

That is the flip.

---

**In everyday Cambodian life:**

Farmers in Kampong Cham practice something like the precautionary principle naturally. Before trying a new pesticide on their whole rice field, they try it on a small plot first. If it damages the crop, they have not lost everything. They are cautious about the irreversible before they are sure about the reversible.
