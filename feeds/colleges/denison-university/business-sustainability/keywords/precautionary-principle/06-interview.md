# Precautionary Principle — Journalist Interview
# គោលការណ៍ប្រុងប្រយ័ត្ន — បទសម្ភាសន៍សារព័ត៌មាន

*Author: ichamrong | Date: 2026-05-29*

---

*Interview between Piseth Nhem of the Cambodian Environment and Development Weekly and Dr. Chanratana Sok, environmental policy researcher at the Cambodia Development Resource Institute (CDRI).*

---

**Piseth Nhem:** Dr. Chanratana, what exactly is the precautionary principle, and why does it matter for Cambodia?

**Dr. Chanratana Sok:** The precautionary principle is a framework for making decisions when you face serious potential harm but incomplete scientific certainty about whether or how that harm will occur. The principle says: do not wait for full proof of harm before acting to prevent it — especially if the harm is irreversible. It matters enormously for Cambodia because our most critical ecosystems — the Mekong, the Tonle Sap, the Cardamom Mountain forests — are being subjected to development decisions at a pace that far exceeds our scientific capacity to fully model the consequences.

**Piseth:** Where does the principle come from?

**Dr. Chanratana:** It was formalized in international law through Principle 15 of the 1992 Rio Declaration on Environment and Development. But the philosophical roots go deeper — the German-American philosopher Hans Jonas wrote in 1979 that modern technology creates the possibility of planetary-scale irreversible harm, and that our ethical frameworks had not caught up with that reality. Jonas said we have a duty to prefer the pessimistic scenario when catastrophe is at stake — what he called the "heuristics of fear." The precautionary principle is the institutional expression of that ethical insight.

**Piseth:** Critics say the precautionary principle can be used to block any development project. How do you respond?

**Dr. Chanratana:** That critique confuses the strong form of the principle with the proportionality requirement. The principle is not "prove safety before any action." It is "when harm is potentially serious AND irreversible, and scientific certainty is incomplete, require better evidence and proportional protective measures." A project with minor, reversible impacts does not trigger precautionary logic. The principle is specifically designed for situations where the consequences of being wrong cannot be undone.

**Piseth:** Can you give a specific Cambodian case where you believe the precautionary principle should have been applied but was not?

**Dr. Chanratana:** The Sesan, Sre Pok, and Sekong Rivers — the 3S rivers that feed into the lower Mekong. CDRI and international researchers produced models showing that dams on those rivers would significantly disrupt fish migration and reduce protein availability for downstream communities. The confidence intervals on those models were extremely wide — the actual harm could range from modest to catastrophic. Under precautionary logic, those wide confidence intervals should have triggered additional study and modified designs before construction. Instead, the narrow central estimate was used as if it were reliable, construction proceeded, and communities bore the full burden of the high-end outcome when it materialized.

**Piseth:** The companies and governments that built those dams would say they followed standard environmental impact assessment procedures. Is the precautionary principle different from an EIA?

**Dr. Chanratana:** A standard EIA is a technical exercise that asks: "What are the likely impacts?" The precautionary principle asks a different question: "What are the possible impacts under the worst plausible scenario, and are those scenarios reversible?" A well-designed EIA should incorporate precautionary reasoning, but in practice many EIAs focus on central estimates and underweight tail risks. The Mekong dam EIAs are a case study in this limitation.

**Piseth:** What about the argument that Cambodia needs development, and that excessive caution slows growth and perpetuates poverty?

**Dr. Chanratana:** It is a genuine trade-off, and I take it seriously. But the trade-off is often framed falsely — as though the choice is between development and no development. The precautionary principle does not necessarily stop projects. It may require modified designs, monitoring programs, smaller initial scales, or sequenced development with evaluation periods. The Jarai communities whose fisheries were disrupted by the Sesan dams were not benefiting from the electricity — most of it was exported. The poverty-versus-environment framing is often deployed to benefit commercial interests, not to actually address poverty.

**Piseth:** How should the Cambodian government integrate the precautionary principle into its environmental governance?

**Dr. Chanratana:** Three concrete steps. First, require that environmental impact assessments quantify uncertainty ranges explicitly — not just central estimates. Second, establish a legal standard that places the burden of proof on project proponents for activities in irreplaceable ecosystems: the Mekong floodplain, Tonle Sap basin, and Cardamom forest corridors. Third, create binding community consent mechanisms so that the communities who bear the highest irreversibility risk have a formal role in the approval process. None of these require abandoning development — they require doing development in a way that does not transfer all the risk onto the people with the least power.

**Piseth:** One final question: is the precautionary principle primarily an environmental concept, or does it apply more broadly?

**Dr. Chanratana:** It applies wherever you have the combination of deep uncertainty, serious potential harm, and irreversibility. That includes emerging technologies like artificial intelligence, novel chemicals in food systems, new financial instruments. The principle is fundamentally about rational decision-making when the standard probability-and-payoff calculus breaks down. Cambodia faces this challenge in its environmental governance most acutely — but the principle is relevant to any sector where the cost of being wrong is permanent.

**Piseth:** Thank you, Dr. Chanratana.
