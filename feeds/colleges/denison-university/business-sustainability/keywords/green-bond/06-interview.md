# Green Bond — Journalist Interview
# សញ្ញាប័ណ្ណបៃតង — បទសម្ភាសន៍សារព័ត៌មាន

*Author: ichamrong | Date: 2026-06-01*

---

*Interview between Ratana Chan of the Phnom Penh Economic Tribune and Dr. Sokunthea Pich, a corporate finance specialist advising on sustainable capital markets.*

---

**Ratana Chan:** Dr. Sokunthea, "green bond" is everywhere in finance news now. Mechanically, what is it?

**Dr. Sokunthea Pich:** Strip away the marketing and it's an ordinary bond — a loan to a company or government, repaid with interest. The only difference is a promise about the *use of proceeds*: the money must fund environmental projects, like renewable energy, clean water, or low-carbon transport. The borrower's obligation to repay is unchanged; what's added is a commitment about where the cash goes, plus reporting to prove it went there.

**Ratana:** If repayment doesn't depend on the project's greenness, why do green bonds exist at all? Why not just borrow normally?

**Dr. Sokunthea Pich:** Because there's a large and growing pool of investors who *want* their money to be green — pension funds with mandates, ethical funds, institutions under pressure to decarbonise their portfolios. When many of them compete to buy your green bond, demand pushes the price up and the yield down a little. We call that small discount the "greenium." So a credible green bond can let an issuer borrow marginally cheaper, while signalling its environmental commitment to the market.

**Ratana:** You keep saying "credible." What makes one credible and another not?

**Dr. Sokunthea Pich:** Four things, codified in the Green Bond Principles. One, a clear statement of which projects the money funds. Two, a sound process for selecting those projects. Three, the proceeds kept separate so they can't quietly leak into ordinary spending. Four — and this is the one that matters most — annual reporting on where the money actually went and what environmental impact it had, ideally checked by an independent verifier. Without that fourth piece, the label is just a sticker.

**Ratana:** That brings us to the obvious risk: greenwashing.

**Dr. Sokunthea Pich:** It's the central danger. Money is fungible — it flows like water and takes the shape of any container. An issuer can raise "green" funds for a solar project it was going to build anyway, then use the cash it freed up to expand a polluting business. On paper, the bond funded the solar farm. In reality, it financed more pollution. The test that catches this is *additionality*: did the bond fund something that wouldn't otherwise have happened? A green bond with no additionality is greenwashing with a coupon attached.

**Ratana:** How relevant is any of this to Cambodia?

**Dr. Sokunthea Pich:** Very, and increasingly so. Cambodia's clean-energy and water-infrastructure needs require long-term capital that local banks, built for short-term lending, can't easily supply. The regional green-bond market is deep and hungry for emerging-market projects. A well-structured Cambodian green bond — sovereign or corporate — could tap that capital for solar, clean water, or sustainable agriculture. But our issuers start with a trust deficit; they'd need rigorous, verified frameworks and honest reporting, or international investors will assume greenwashing and either stay away or charge a premium that erases the benefit.

**Ratana:** So the discipline is the point, not the label?

**Dr. Sokunthea Pich:** Exactly. The label opens the door; the discipline keeps it open. The first Cambodian issuers who report honestly and let outsiders verify will build a reputation that lowers borrowing costs for years. The ones who treat it as a marketing exercise will get caught, and one scandal can poison the well for everyone.

**Ratana:** Final question. One thing for a young Cambodian finance professional to remember about green bonds?

**Dr. Sokunthea Pich:** That a green bond is not a promise you announce — it's a promise you document, every year, in numbers anyone can check. The borrowing is the easy half. The proof is the real product.

**Ratana:** Thank you, Dr. Sokunthea.
