# Green Bond — Feynman Explanation
# សញ្ញាប័ណ្ណបៃតង — ការពន្យល់បែប Feynman

*Author: ichamrong | Date: 2026-06-01*

---

First, forget the word "green" for a moment. Let's understand a plain **bond**.

When you put money in a bank, the bank owes you. A bond flips it around: instead of lending to a bank, you lend directly to a company or a government. You hand over, say, 1,000 dollars today. They promise to pay you a little interest each year, and give your 1,000 back at the end. You are not an owner of the company — you are a lender. That's all a bond is: an IOU you can buy.

---

Now add the word "green." A **green bond** is exactly the same IOU, with one extra promise stapled to it: *the money you lend will be spent only on environmental projects* — a solar farm, a clean-water system, an electric bus fleet.

Here's the whole idea in a sentence: **a green bond is an ordinary loan to a company, with a written promise that the borrowed money goes to green things — and a report afterward proving it did.**

---

Now, a sharp question. The company still owes you the same money whether it builds a solar farm or a coal plant. Your chance of being repaid depends on whether the *company* is healthy, not on whether the *project* is green. So why would the "green" promise matter to you as a lender?

For two reasons.

Some lenders genuinely care where their money goes — pension funds, ethical investors, people who don't want to profit from pollution. They'll happily lend to the green bond and refuse the coal one. When lots of them want green bonds, the company can borrow a tiny bit cheaper — because so many people are competing to lend. Economists nickname that small discount the **"greenium."**

---

But here's the trap, and it's the most important part. The promise is mostly *self-made*. The company says "this money is green." Who checks?

Money is slippery. Suppose a company raises green money for a solar farm — a farm it was going to build anyway — and quietly uses the cash it freed up to expand its coal business. The green bond funded the solar farm on paper, but the company got *greener-looking* without getting *greener*. That's **greenwashing**: a green label on something that isn't really doing new green good.

That's why serious green bonds come with rules — a checklist called the Green Bond Principles, an outside auditor, and a yearly report saying exactly where the money went and what it achieved. Without that paperwork, the word "green" is just paint.

---

**The simple version:**

A green bond is a loan you give a company that promises to spend it on environmental projects and prove afterward that it did. People who care about the planet line up to lend, which can make the borrowing slightly cheaper. But because money is easy to shuffle around, a green bond is only as honest as its reporting. No proof, no green — just paint.
