## The Water Utility That Borrowed Green / សហគ្រាសទឹកដែលខ្ចីបៃតង

The provincial water utility serving a fast-growing town outside Phnom Penh had a problem older than its pipes. Its pumps ran on diesel; its old network leaked nearly a third of the water it treated; and the river it drew from ran lower and dirtier each dry season. The director, a careful engineer named Sambath, knew what the fix looked like — solar pumps, replaced mains, a new filtration plant. He also knew it cost far more than the utility could ever save up. He needed to borrow, and he needed to borrow long.

The local banks offered him short loans at rates that made the project impossible. Then an advisor from the finance ministry mentioned a different path: a **green bond**. Investors abroad, the advisor explained, were actively hunting for clean-water and clean-energy projects to put money into. If Sambath could package his upgrade as a credible green project, those investors might lend for fifteen years — and perhaps a touch cheaper than ordinary lenders, because so many of them wanted exactly this.

This is the first thing the story teaches: **a green bond connects a long-term green project to a pool of investors who specifically want to fund green things — and that hunger can make the borrowing cheaper and longer than ordinary finance.**

But the advisor was blunt about the price of entry. "You cannot just call it green," she said. "You will have to write down exactly what the money funds. You will have to keep that money in a separate account so no one can claim it wandered off into salaries or the director's car. And every single year, you will have to publish a report: how many litres of diesel you stopped burning, how much leakage you cut, how much cleaner the discharge is. An outside firm will check it. If your numbers are vague, the investors will smell greenwashing and walk away — or demand a higher rate that wipes out your savings."

Sambath did the work. He defined the eligible projects precisely. He ring-fenced the proceeds. He hired a verifier to bless the framework before issuance. The bond sold, and the careful, honest framework drew enough green investors that the utility borrowed at a slightly lower yield than a plain bond would have fetched — the small "greenium" the advisor had promised.

This is the second thing the story teaches: **the green label was worth nothing by itself; it became worth something only because the money was kept separate, the projects were real and additional, and the results were reported and checked.**

There was a tempting shortcut Sambath did not take, and it is worth naming. A neighbouring utility had issued its own "green" bond, used it to build a solar array it had already budgeted from other funds, and quietly diverted the freed-up cash to a diesel-pump expansion. On paper, green. In reality, the bond had funded more diesel. When an investigative reporter pulled the thread a year later, the neighbouring utility's reputation — and its access to future green capital — collapsed. The label had been paint, and paint peels.

Three years on, Sambath's solar pumps hummed, the leakage was down by half, and the town's water ran cleaner through the dry season. A student came to interview him about green finance. She asked whether the green bond had been worth the extra paperwork.

Sambath thought about the neighbouring utility, then about his own annual reports stacked on the shelf. "The bond was easy," he said. "Anyone can borrow money and call it green. The reports were the hard part — and the reports were the *real* part. A green bond is not a promise you make. It is a promise you keep, in writing, every year, where anyone can check."
