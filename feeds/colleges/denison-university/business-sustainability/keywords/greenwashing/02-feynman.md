# Greenwashing — Feynman Explanation
# ការធ្វើលំអបញ្ញត្តិបៃតង — ការពន្យល់បែប Feynman

*Author: ichamrong | Date: 2026-05-29*

---

Imagine you are at Phsar Thmei market (ផ្សារថ្មី) in Phnom Penh and there is a seller with a beautiful green sign. It says: **"Our fabric is 100% eco-friendly. We protect forests. We love the earth."** The bag is wrapped in leaves. The sign shows a turtle. Even the receipt is printed on recycled paper.

You feel good. You pay extra. You walk home proud.

But here is what you did not see: the factory is in an industrial zone near Phnom Penh where the drainage goes straight into the canal. The workers earn below minimum wage. The "recycled paper" receipt is the only green thing in the entire supply chain.

**That is greenwashing (ការធ្វើលំអបញ្ញត្តិបៃតង).**

Greenwashing means using *the look and language of being environmentally responsible* to make people think you care about nature — when actually you are doing nothing different, or even doing harm.

Think of it like painting rust with green paint. The rust is still there. But the paint is green, so people think it is healthy.

---

**Why do companies do this?**

Because being "green" makes people willing to pay more. In surveys, more than half of shoppers say they prefer products that are good for the environment. Some will pay 10–20% more for eco-certified goods.

So companies have two choices:

1. Actually invest in cleaner factories, better processes, real recycling programs. This costs money.
2. Just say you do those things. Print a green logo. Put a leaf on the package. This costs almost nothing.

If you can get the same price premium by just *saying* you are green, why spend the money to actually *be* green?

That is why greenwashing happens. It is not stupidity — it is a rational business decision when nobody checks.

---

**A Cambodian example you know:**

Several microfinance companies in Cambodia marketed themselves to foreign investors as "empowering rural women." They raised money cheaply because investors believed they were doing good.

But journalists and human rights groups discovered that some of these same companies were seizing land from poor families who missed payments. They were not protecting women — they were trapping families in debt.

The "empowerment" story was the green paint. The debt trap was the rust underneath.

---

**How do you protect yourself?**

Three questions every smart buyer should ask:

1. **Who checked this claim?** (Not the company itself — an independent organization?)
2. **What exactly does "eco-friendly" mean here?** (Recyclable packaging? Or the entire factory process?)
3. **Does the company publish real numbers?** (Carbon emissions? Water use? Worker wages?)

If a company cannot answer these three questions clearly, the green label is just decoration.

---

**The big lesson:**

Words are cheap. Paper is cheap. Printing a leaf on a bottle costs almost nothing.

Real sustainability costs real money — in engineering, in supply chain changes, in paying workers fairly.

The difference between greenwashing and genuine sustainability is the same as the difference between putting a sticker of muscles on your arm and actually going to the gym.
