# Greenwashing — Journalist Interview
# ការធ្វើលំអបញ្ញត្តិបៃតង — បទសម្ភាសន៍សារព័ត៌មាន

*Author: ichamrong | Date: 2026-05-29*

---

*The following is an interview between Cambodian journalist Bopha Keo of the Mekong Business Review and Dr. Sothea Prum, environmental economist and advisor to the Cambodia Climate Finance Taskforce.*

---

**Bopha Keo:** Dr. Sothea, we keep hearing the word "greenwashing" in the news. Can you explain what it actually means in plain terms?

**Dr. Sothea Prum:** Greenwashing is when a company or organization claims to be environmentally responsible in its marketing and public communications, but its actual operations tell a different story. The key word is "claim." The claim is not backed by real action, verified data, or independent scrutiny.

**Bopha:** Is this happening in Cambodia?

**Dr. Sothea:** Very much so, and in multiple sectors. The most documented cases involve microfinance institutions that market themselves internationally as champions of financial inclusion and women's empowerment while their field practices — aggressive collection, over-indebtedness, land title seizures — contradict those claims entirely. But we also see it in agriculture, tourism, and garment manufacturing.

**Bopha:** You mentioned garment manufacturing. Cambodia's garment sector employs hundreds of thousands of people. Can you give a specific example?

**Dr. Sothea:** Several international brands source from Cambodian factories and then publish sustainability reports that highlight worker welfare programs — meal subsidies, health clinics. These are often real and genuinely positive. But the same reports do not disclose water consumption in fabric dyeing processes, chemical waste discharge near factory zones in Kandal Province, or the percentage of total retail price returned to workers. The positive is foregrounded; the negative is omitted. That selective disclosure is a textbook greenwashing structure.

**Bopha:** Is selective disclosure actually illegal?

**Dr. Sothea:** Under current Cambodian law, no. There is no mandatory environmental disclosure framework comparable to financial reporting. Companies are essentially self-policing their sustainability narratives. That is the governance gap.

**Bopha:** What about the microfinance sector specifically? Some of these institutions have received ESG investment from major international funds because of their "social mission" branding.

**Dr. Sothea:** That is the most troubling pattern. When an MFI markets itself to impact investors as an empowerment vehicle, it borrows at concessional rates — cheaper than commercial lending. That is a direct financial benefit from the green or social premium. If the label is not matched by practice, the investors are mispriced and the communities pay the cost. LICADHO's reports on household over-indebtedness in Kampong Chhnang and Takeo documented thousands of families who sold land under distress after MFI debt spirals. Those families are the true cost of the social washing that was invisible to European pension funds.

**Bopha:** What is the difference between a company that is genuinely trying to improve and one that is genuinely greenwashing?

**Dr. Sothea:** Intent matters, but it is very difficult to verify from the outside. The structural difference I look for is whether the company's internal incentives have actually changed. Are the bonuses of operations managers tied to environmental metrics? Are environmental costs internalized in pricing? Or is all the sustainability activity happening only in the communications department? A real sustainability program costs the company money on operations. Greenwashing costs money only on marketing.

**Bopha:** How should a Cambodian consumer — or a Cambodian government regulator — tell the difference?

**Dr. Sothea:** Three questions: First, who verified the claim? Self-certification is meaningless. Second, what exactly is being claimed? "Eco-packaging" is not the same as "sustainable supply chain." Third, what are the hard numbers? Energy use, water discharge, worker wages as a percentage of retail price. If a company cannot or will not answer those three questions, the green label is decoration.

**Bopha:** Some would argue that greenwashing, even if imperfect, at least moves companies in a better direction — they have to say green things, and eventually they might feel pressure to do green things.

**Dr. Sothea:** That is a charitable reading. The evidence from financial markets is the opposite: greenwashing actually crowds out genuine sustainability. If I can capture the ESG premium by marketing rather than investing, then I never invest. And the honest company that did invest cannot compete on price. The market equilibrium slides toward performance, not substance. We saw this with "organic" food labels in the 1990s before third-party certification became mandatory. Greenwashing degraded the entire category until regulation enforced real standards.

**Bopha:** What reform would have the greatest impact in Cambodia?

**Dr. Sothea:** Mandatory, standardized environmental and social impact reporting for companies above a certain revenue threshold — modeled on what the EU is now requiring under the Corporate Sustainability Reporting Directive. Every company discloses the same metrics, audited by licensed third parties, published in a public registry. That single reform would change the cost-benefit calculation of greenwashing overnight.

**Bopha:** Dr. Sothea, thank you for your time.

**Dr. Sothea:** Thank you. I would only add: the communities near Boeung Kak Lake, the families along the Mekong, the garment workers in Kampong Speu — they already know the difference between a promise and a practice. The question is whether the regulatory framework will catch up to what they have known for years.
