## The Silk That Saved Nothing / សូត្រដែលមិនបានជួយសង្រ្គោះអ្វីឡើយ

The marketing woman arrived from Singapore with three suitcases and a presentation deck that used the word "sustainable" forty-seven times. Chamroeun counted. She was sitting in the back row of the factory conference room in Kampong Cham, watching slides of green fields and happy weavers and a graph that showed carbon emissions going down.

The Singapore woman's name was Ms. Lim. She had been hired by a European textile brand to find a Cambodian silk producer they could label "ethically sourced." The brand needed a story for its website, something with photographs of village women at their looms, something that would justify a 40% price premium to buyers in Amsterdam and London.

Chamroeun had been weaving silk since she was twelve. She ran a cooperative of nineteen women in Kampong Cham, all of them growing their own mulberry trees, feeding silkworms by hand, boiling cocoons over wood fires. The process was slow, expensive, and genuinely traditional. They sold at the local market for about $8 per meter.

Ms. Lim offered $9.50 per meter. She also offered to put the cooperative's name and photograph on the brand's website. "Impact partnership," she called it.

Three weeks later, the website went live. There were photographs of Chamroeun and her women, smiling at their looms. The text said: *"Our silk is hand-woven by empowered Cambodian artisans using traditional, low-carbon methods. Every purchase supports sustainable livelihoods in rural Cambodia."*

The European brand began selling the silk at €180 per meter.

Chamroeun received her first wire transfer and bought a new loom.

Then a graduate student from a European university arrived to do research on sustainable textile supply chains. Her name was Amélie. She spent a week with the cooperative, asking questions, measuring things, running calculations.

She asked how much the cooperative received per meter.

$9.50, said Chamroeun.

Amélie showed her the brand's website. €180 per meter. Retail.

Chamroeun looked at the screen for a long time.

Then Amélie asked a different question. She had visited the brand's main garment factory — not the cooperative, but the factory in Phnom Penh where the silk was cut, sewn, and finished. She had walked along the canal behind the factory.

"What do you know about the factory's wastewater?" she asked.

Chamroeun knew nothing. She only delivered raw silk. What happened after was not her business.

Amélie showed her photographs. The canal behind the factory was grey. The dye chemicals — synthetic, not the natural indigo the cooperative used — flowed directly into the drainage. The brand's website said nothing about this factory. All the photographs were of Chamroeun's cooperative. All the words about sustainability described the weaving, not the cutting, not the dyeing, not the shipping.

"They are not lying exactly," Amélie said carefully. "Your part of the supply chain really is traditional and low-carbon. But they are showing only your part, and hiding the rest."

Chamroeun sat with this.

"And the $9.50?" she finally asked.

Amélie showed her that the raw silk cost represented less than 6% of the retail price. The cooperative's entire income from this partnership was smaller than the brand's annual marketing budget for the Cambodia campaign.

The brand called itself a pioneer in **ethical sourcing**. It published a sustainability report every year with photographs of smiling weavers. It won two industry awards for "commitment to artisan communities."

None of those awards required third-party verification of supply chain conditions beyond the photographed portion. None measured water quality in the canal behind the Phnom Penh factory. None asked what percentage of retail price returned to the artisans.

Chamroeun renewed the contract the following year. She needed the income. She smiled in the new photographs.

But she understood, now, that a story about a clean beginning does not tell you anything about what happens at the end — and that the people with cameras only ever stayed for the part that looked beautiful.

What Amélie had documented — the selective disclosure, the highlighted positive, the hidden harm, the inflated price premium justified by a narrow slice of genuine practice — is the economic structure that scholars call **greenwashing**: capturing the value of a sustainability claim without bearing the full cost of sustainable practice.
