# Planetary Boundaries — Journalist Interview
# ព្រំដែនភពផែនដី — បទសម្ភាសន៍អ្នកសារព័ត៌មាន

*Author: ichamrong | Date: 2026-05-29*
*Cambodian journalist Sreymom interviews Dr. Nara Phanith, Earth system scientist, Ministry of Environment*

---

**Sreymom:** Dr. Phanith, you work on planetary boundaries — what exactly are you measuring?

**Dr. Phanith:** We are measuring nine key properties of the Earth system that together define what scientists call the "safe operating space" (តំបន់សុវត្ថិភាព) for human civilization. These are the conditions under which our planet has remained stable for the past 11,700 years — the Holocene epoch (ហូឡូស៊ីន). The nine boundaries include climate change, biodiversity loss, land-system change, freshwater use, biogeochemical flows, ocean acidification, ozone depletion, aerosol loading, and novel chemical entities.

**Sreymom:** That sounds very global and abstract. Why should ordinary Cambodians care?

**Dr. Phanith:** Because Cambodia is on the front line of every one of these boundaries, even though we contributed almost nothing to crossing them. Let me give you a direct example: the climate boundary has been transgressed by the combined emissions of industrialized countries over 150 years. Cambodia's per-capita emissions are tiny. But the consequence — a warming, drying, more erratic Mekong River system — falls on Cambodian farmers and fishermen. When the flood pulse to the Tonle Sap weakens because the upstream glaciers that feed the Mekong are shrinking, and because dams are changing water timing, the families in Kampong Chhnang or Siem Reap who depend on that flood for fish protein lose food security. They didn't cause the problem. They absorb the damage.

**Sreymom:** How many of the nine boundaries have been crossed as of now?

**Dr. Phanith:** As of the 2023 update from the Stockholm Resilience Centre, at least six of the nine boundaries have been transgressed. Climate change — transgressed. Biodiversity loss — deeply transgressed; current extinction rates are 100 to 1,000 times the natural background rate. Land-system change — transgressed, including through deforestation in Southeast Asia. Freshwater — recently added to the transgressed list. Nitrogen and phosphorus flows from fertilizers — transgressed. Novel entities, including plastics and synthetic chemicals — transgressed. Only ozone depletion, ocean acidification, and atmospheric aerosols are still within safe limits, though some of those are approaching their boundaries.

**Sreymom:** Is there any good news in that list?

**Dr. Phanith:** Yes, actually. The ozone boundary is the great success story. In the 1980s, the ozone layer over Antarctica was thinning dangerously — that was a planetary boundary transgression in progress. The world signed the Montreal Protocol in 1987, banned ozone-depleting chemicals, and today the ozone layer is measurably recovering. It shows that planetary boundaries can be respected and partially restored when there is political will and international cooperation. That is the most hopeful data point in the whole framework.

**Sreymom:** Cambodia has been deforesting rapidly. What boundary does that affect?

**Dr. Phanith:** Primarily the land-system change boundary, but also biodiversity and freshwater. Cambodia's forest cover dropped from about 73 percent in 1993 to roughly 46 percent by 2020. The Cardamom Mountains (ភ្នំកាដាម៉ូម) still hold significant intact forest, but the pressure from economic concessions and agricultural expansion continues. Importantly, forests in the Cardamom Mountains regulate water flows into rivers that feed the Tonle Sap system. Lose that forest and you lose the water regulation service — more flooding during peak rains, more drought during dry season.

**Sreymom:** Cambodia's REDD+ program — does that actually help with planetary boundaries?

**Dr. Phanith:** It addresses two boundaries simultaneously: land-system change and climate change, through carbon sequestration. Cambodia has registered carbon credits from protected forest areas that bring in international payments for forest conservation. It is genuinely useful — communities protecting forests in Koh Kong and Mondulkiri receive income that makes conservation economically rational. But the payments per hectare are still modest relative to the economic pressure to clear land. We need much higher carbon prices, or additional payments for biodiversity and water services, to make REDD+ fully competitive with land conversion.

**Sreymom:** What happens if Cambodia's government ignores planetary boundaries in its development planning?

**Dr. Phanith:** The consequences will not be abstract. They will be felt in rice yields, fish catches, flood damage costs, and water availability. The Mekong is already showing symptoms — in 2019 and 2020, the river reached historically low levels, the Tonle Sap flooded to its smallest recorded extent, and fish catches collapsed. Families that had fished for generations could not fill their nets. That is not a future scenario. That already happened.

**Sreymom:** Are there Cambodian-scale solutions, or is this entirely a global problem?

**Dr. Phanith:** Both. At the national scale, Cambodia can protect its remaining forests, restore degraded watersheds, and advocate strongly in international climate negotiations for both emission reductions and compensation for damages. At the community scale, revival of traditional fishery management practices — seasonal closures, protected spawning areas — mirrors planetary boundary logic at a local level. And Cambodia should be vocal in global forums about loss and damage: the principle that those who transgressed the boundaries must compensate those who are suffering the consequences.

**Sreymom:** Final question — are you hopeful?

**Dr. Phanith:** I am determined. The science is clear. The consequences are visible. What remains is the will to act, which is ultimately a political and moral question. Cambodia has a very strong interest in that question being answered correctly.
