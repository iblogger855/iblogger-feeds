## The Old Man and the Nine Rules of the River | ចាស់ទុំ និងក្បួនទាំងប្រាំបួននៃទន្លេ

In the village of Prek Toal, at the edge of the flooded forest where the Tonle Sap met the sky each rainy season, there lived an old fisherman named Chom. He was ninety-one years old and remembered things that his grandchildren found impossible to believe.

He remembered when the lake flooded so deeply that children could swim between the crowns of the trees. He remembered when the fish ran so thickly that you could hear them at night, a sound like rain on a tin roof. He remembered the nine rules that his grandfather had taught him — rules not written anywhere, but carved into the practice of every family who fished these waters.

*Don't fish during the three sacred weeks when the lake is rising. Don't drag nets through the deep holes where the big fish shelter the young. Don't use fire on the water at night. Don't clear the trees on the bank above the waterline. Don't wash poison into the stream from the rubber grove. Don't take more than one basket per family per day during the thin months. Don't build walls across the small channels where the fish move inland. Don't sell to the traders who come with ice trucks — they take too much, too fast. And always, always give back the females with eggs.*

His grandfather had called these the Nine Rules. Not rules from the government. Rules from the river itself.

Chom had kept the rules. His son had kept them. But his son's son — young Virak, who drove a Lexus and had a phone that never left his hand — had laughed at them. "Grandfather rules for a grandfather world," Virak had said.

Virak had sold permits to three ice-truck companies. He had approved a rubber plantation on the hill above the spawning channels. He had taken the money and bought land near Phnom Penh.

The first year, the catches were enormous — the companies had better nets, faster boats, cold storage. Everyone said development had arrived.

The second year, the catches were good.

The third year, the big fish were rarer.

By the fifth year, the families of Prek Toal were buying fish from Kampot, carried up by truck. They were buying fish to live beside a lake.

Old Chom sat on his porch each morning and watched the water. It was lower than it used to be. The flooded forest, without the trees on the hillside to hold the watershed, drained faster. The fish that had always moved inland to breed couldn't find their channels — they had been blocked by the rubber plantation's drainage ditches.

One evening, a young researcher from Phnom Penh came to the village. She had a laptop and a model of the Tonle Sap's hydrology. She showed Chom a diagram of nine circles, like a target, each circle representing one "boundary" that the lake and the world around it needed to stay within.

"Your grandfather knew these boundaries," she told him. "He just called them the Nine Rules."

Chom nodded slowly.

The researcher said that six of the nine global circles had now been crossed — not just in Prek Toal, but everywhere. The air was warming, the forests were shrinking, chemicals were building up in places they didn't belong. She said the lake's flood pulse was already weakening from the combination of upstream dams and changing rains and lost forest cover.

"Can it come back?" Chom asked.

"If we stop crossing the lines," she said. "Some of them."

"Which ones cannot come back?"

She was quiet for a moment. "We don't know exactly. That's what makes it frightening."

Chom was quiet too. He thought of the fish sound at night, like rain on a tin roof.

"My grandfather didn't know why the rules worked," he said finally. "He only knew that the river had taught them to his grandfather through disaster, and his grandfather had remembered. We forgot, and now the river is teaching us again."

The researcher didn't write that down. But she thought about it for a long time afterward, walking back along the bank in the dark, the lake low and still and very, very quiet.

---

*This story illustrates the concept of **planetary boundaries** (ព្រំដែនភពផែនដី) — the nine biophysical thresholds that define a safe operating space for human civilization. Crossing these boundaries risks triggering irreversible changes to the Earth system.*
