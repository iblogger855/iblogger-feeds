## Analogy 1: The Motorbike Red Zone (តំបន់ក្រហមនៃម៉ូតូ)

**The analogy:** A motorbike engine has a tachometer with a red zone. You can push the engine hard — up to the red line — and it handles fine. Cross the red line for long enough and the engine seizes.

**Mapping:**
| Motorbike element | Planetary boundary element |
|---|---|
| Engine | Earth's biophysical system |
| RPM (revolutions per minute) | Measure of a boundary variable (e.g., ppm CO₂) |
| Red zone on tachometer | Boundary threshold |
| Engine seizing | Tipping point — irreversible state change |
| Multiple red zones for different components | Nine separate planetary boundaries |
| Mechanic's warning | Scientific boundary assessment |

**Cambodian connection:** Cambodia has very few motorbikes causing planetary boundary stress. But if the global engine seizes — if the Mekong's flow pattern tips into a new low-flow regime — Cambodia's fishermen bear the consequences without having caused the problem.

**Where the analogy holds:** The nonlinearity is accurate. You can run an engine at 90% of red zone indefinitely. But cross it and the damage is rapid and self-reinforcing.

**Where it breaks down:** A motorbike engine either seizes or it doesn't — a fairly binary outcome. Planetary tipping points are more probabilistic; at each level of transgression, the *risk* of a tipping point increases but is not guaranteed. Also, some planetary boundaries (like ozone depletion) have proven reversible once pressure was removed.

## Analogy 2: Village Water Tank (ធុងទឹករបស់ភូមិ)

**The analogy:** A village shares a water tank fed by a spring in the Cardamom Mountains. The spring refills at a certain rate each season. As long as the village draws less than the recharge rate, the tank never empties. But there are three other rules: don't contaminate the water, don't block the spring inlet, and don't let the surrounding forest be cleared (because the forest roots feed the spring).

**Mapping:**
| Tank rule | Planetary boundary |
|---|---|
| Don't draw faster than recharge | Freshwater boundary |
| Don't contaminate | Novel entities / chemical pollution |
| Don't block spring inlet | Biogeochemical flows (N/P) |
| Keep the forest intact | Land-system change boundary |
| Don't trap spring heat | Climate change boundary |
| Multiple rules together define "tank safety" | Nine boundaries defining safe operating space |

**Where the analogy holds:** The interdependence is well captured. Breaking one rule (clearing the forest) can violate another rule (reducing recharge), illustrating how boundaries interact.

**Where it breaks down:** A village tank is small and manageable — the spring can be observed and measured by anyone. Planetary boundaries require satellite data, international scientific coordination, and complex Earth system models. The governance problem is vastly harder than a village agreement.

## Analogy 3: Traditional Cambodian Fishing Grounds Rules (ក្បួនអ្នកនេសាទប្រពៃណី)

**The analogy:** Traditional Khmer fishing communities observed customary rules: no fishing during spawning season, no fishing in certain deep holes (ដំបូង) where fish gather, and no use of poison or electricity. These rules were planetary boundaries at a local scale — they defined the "safe operating space" for the fishery's long-term productivity.

**Mapping:**
| Fishing rule | Planetary boundary equivalent |
|---|---|
| No fishing in spawning season | Biodiversity / biosphere integrity boundary |
| No fishing in sacred holes | Land-system / freshwater boundary |
| No poison or electricity | Novel entities boundary |
| Community enforces collectively | Global governance of planetary boundaries |
| Violators risk collapsing the whole fishery | Tipping point — fishery collapse |

**Cambodian resonance:** Under the Khmer Rouge and during decades of conflict, these customary rules collapsed. Fishing with electricity and explosives destroyed spawning grounds. Recovery took a generation and is still incomplete in some areas. This is a Cambodia-scale demonstration of what happens when boundaries are transgressed.

**Where the analogy holds:** It shows that boundary-keeping is an ancient human wisdom, not a modern academic invention. Communities that survived for centuries did so by observing these limits intuitively.

**Where it breaks down:** Local fishing rules could be renegotiated by the same community that set them. Global planetary boundaries cannot be renegotiated with nature — they are biophysical realities, not social contracts.
