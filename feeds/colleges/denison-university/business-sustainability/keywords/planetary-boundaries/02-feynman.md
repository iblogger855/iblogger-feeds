# Planetary Boundaries — Feynman Explanation
# ព្រំដែនភពផែនដី — ការពន្យល់បែប Feynman

*Author: ichamrong | Date: 2026-05-29*

---

You know how every motorbike has a red line on the speedometer? If you go past it, the engine overheats, something breaks, and the motorbike stops working.

Planet Earth has red lines too. Scientists call them **planetary boundaries** (ព្រំដែនភពផែនដី).

For thousands of years — since long before your grandparents' grandparents were born — Earth stayed within these red lines. The rains came when expected. Rivers flooded on schedule. Seasons were predictable enough to plant rice, wait for fish, and harvest on time. Scientists call this comfortable period the Holocene (ហូឡូស៊ីន). All of human civilization — every city, every rice paddy, every road — was built during this calm, stable time.

But in the last 70 years, we started revving the engine very hard.

Scientists have found 9 major "dials" on Earth's engine. Each dial has a safe zone:

1. How much CO₂ is in the air (ចំនួន CO₂ ក្នុងអាកាស)
2. How fast species are going extinct (ល្បឿនបាត់ប្រភេទសត្វ-រុក្ខជាតិ)
3. How much forest we have left (ទំហំព្រៃឈើដែលនៅសល់)
4. How clean and available our freshwater is (ភាពស្អាតនៃទឹកសាប)
5. How much nitrogen and phosphorus we're putting in soil and water (ជីក្នុងដី-ទឹក)
6. How acidic the ocean is becoming (ការជូរចត់ប្រព័ន្ធសមុទ្រ)
7. The ozone layer protecting us from the sun (ស្រទាប់អូហ្សូន)
8. Tiny particles floating in the atmosphere (ភាគល្អិតក្នុងបរិយាកាស)
9. New chemicals and plastics we've invented (សារធាតុសំយោគថ្មី)

As of today, we've pushed **6 out of those 9 dials** past the red line. Climate change dial — past the line. Biodiversity dial — way past the line. Forest loss — past the line.

Now, here's the scary part: some of these dials don't just slowly get worse. They have what scientists call **tipping points** (ចំណុចបត់). Think of a tipped canoe. You can tilt it a little and it comes back. But at a certain angle, it flips completely and won't right itself.

For Cambodia, the biggest worry is the Mekong River. The Tonle Sap Lake fills up every year because the Mekong floods and pushes water backward up the Tonle Sap River. Fish migrate into flooded forests, grow up, and come back. This has worked for thousands of years. But upstream dams and climate change are changing the flood timing. Scientists watching planetary boundaries are worried that this system could "flip" — go from productive to almost dead — faster than anyone expects.

The good news? Some dials are coming back. The ozone layer is healing because countries agreed to stop using the chemicals that damaged it. That shows planetary boundaries can be respected — if people choose to.
