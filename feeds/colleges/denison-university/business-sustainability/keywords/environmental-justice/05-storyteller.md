## What the River Remembers / អ្វីដែលទន្លេចាំ

Sreymom's grandmother had a saying: *"The river does not forget where it came from."*

Sreymom had been hearing this her whole life without understanding it. She understood it the year she turned twenty-two, in the spring when the fish did not return.

Her family had lived in their village on the bank of the Prek Thnot River for four generations. Her great-grandmother had fished here. Her grandmother had taken the surplus to market in a wooden boat every Thursday. Her mother had taught her to read the water — the color, the smell, the way the current moved — to know what was coming.

What came that spring was nothing. The nets came up empty three days in a row.

By the end of the week, the other fishermen were gathering on the bank, pulling their nets out and staring at them, saying nothing.

Sreymom walked upstream for two days. She found what she was looking for on the second afternoon: a drainage pipe emerging from the concrete wall of a new cassava processing facility, discharging a pale grey liquid directly into the river.

She took photographs on her phone.

She brought them to the commune chief. He told her to be patient — the factory brought jobs. He told her the facility had all its permits in order.

She contacted an environmental organization in Phnom Penh. A young staff member came out to take water samples. The laboratory analysis found levels of chemical oxygen demand — a measure of organic waste — forty times above the standard permitted for surface water discharge.

The organization helped her draft a complaint to the Ministry of Environment. Three months passed with no response.

In the meantime, her family's fishing income dropped by 70%. Her younger brother stopped attending school because the family needed him to help find other income. Two elderly neighbors who had no other food source began eating less regularly.

The facility, she learned later, had originally considered a site on the outskirts of a district town, closer to the paved road and more convenient for logistics. But the land there was owned by families with connections to the district administration. There had been objections. The site had changed.

There was no public record of that change. There was no record of any consultation with her village, which was ten kilometers downstream from the new site.

The facility's manager, when a journalist called him, said: "We are in compliance with all regulations. Any issues should be raised through proper channels." He cited the permits. He did not mention that the effluent treatment system had not been completed when the facility began operations — a fact that Sreymom had documented with dated photographs.

The complaint was eventually acknowledged. An inspection was scheduled. It was postponed twice. When it happened, the effluent testing registered normal levels — because the facility had been informed of the inspection date in advance.

Sreymom's grandmother died that autumn. She had said, the year before: "I have fished this river my whole life. I do not know this river anymore."

Two years later, an environmental law clinic at a Phnom Penh university took up the case. Their researchers documented the pattern: of eight new agro-processing facilities permitted in the province in the previous decade, seven had been sited within five kilometers of communities below the median income for the province. Not one had been sited near a district town or near communities with politically connected landowners.

The pattern was not coincidence. It was not malice. It was the path of least resistance, operating with ordinary efficiency, year after year.

The clinic's report named this pattern. It used a term that Sreymom had not heard before.

**Environmental injustice** — the systematic concentration of environmental harm in communities with the least power to prevent it.

The term did not restore the fish. It did not return the income. It did not give back Sreymom's grandmother's last healthy years on a river she had known since childhood.

But it gave Sreymom a word for what she had witnessed. And it told her that what happened to her village had happened to people in Black communities in the American South, to Indigenous communities in the Amazon, to fishing villages on the Mekong — and that in every case, the pattern was the same, and in every case, naming it was the beginning of changing it.
