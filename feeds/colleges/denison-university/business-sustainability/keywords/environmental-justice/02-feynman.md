# Environmental Justice — Feynman Explanation
# យុត្តិធម៌បរិស្ថាន — ការពន្យល់បែប Feynman

*Author: ichamrong | Date: 2026-05-29*

---

Here is a question: When a factory needs to put its smokestack somewhere, where does it usually end up?

Not next to the house of the minister. Not next to the wealthy neighborhood where lawyers live. Almost always next to the poor community, the village that has no political connections, the neighborhood where people do not know how to file a complaint or hire a lawyer.

Is this an accident?

No. It is completely predictable.

This pattern — where environmental pollution, toxic waste, dirty water, and contaminated soil end up concentrated in the poorest and least powerful communities — is what researchers call **environmental injustice**. And its opposite — designing a world where those burdens are shared fairly — is called **environmental justice (យុត្តិធម៌បរិស្ថាន)**.

---

**Let us make it very concrete.**

Imagine two villages on the same river. Village A is upstream. The families there have land titles, connections to city officials, and a local representative who sits on the provincial council. Village B is downstream. The families are mostly poor rice farmers. Many do not have formal land titles. They have never spoken to a government official.

A company wants to build a tannery — a leather factory. Tanneries produce very toxic wastewater. The company needs to discharge it somewhere.

Which village is the company more likely to build next to?

Village B, obviously. Because Village A will fight back effectively, and Village B cannot.

So Village B's river gets poisoned. Village B's children get sick from the water. Village B's rice harvest shrinks because the irrigation water is contaminated. Meanwhile, Village A's river is fine.

**This is environmental injustice.** The burden — the pollution — falls on the people who had the least power to say no.

---

**It works the other way too.**

Think about who gets the nicest parks, the cleanest drinking water, the best-maintained rivers. In most countries, the answer is: the wealthy neighborhoods and communities with political voice. They advocate for clean water. They hire lawyers to protect their parks. Their children grow up with safe playgrounds.

Environmental justice says: **this should not be acceptable.** A clean environment is not a luxury for the rich. It is a basic right for everyone.

---

**Three questions that measure environmental justice:**

1. **Who gets the pollution?** (Distributional — ការចែកចាយ)
   Is environmental harm spread equally, or does it concentrate among poor and marginalized communities?

2. **Who gets to decide?** (Procedural — ដំណើរការ)
   Are the communities most affected actually consulted before decisions are made? Or are they told after the fact?

3. **Whose knowledge counts?** (Recognition — ការទទួលស្គាល់)
   When indigenous communities say "this river feeds us, this forest is sacred to us" — is that taken seriously? Or dismissed as unscientific?

---

**Cambodia's most famous example:**

Boeung Kak Lake in Phnom Penh was a large urban lake where thousands of poor families lived and fished. A developer got permission to fill the lake with sand to build a commercial development. The families were displaced — often with very little compensation, sometimes with none. Some who protested were arrested.

The families who lost their homes were not powerful. The developer was. Environmental justice asks: why do the least powerful communities always pay the highest price for development? And what would it look like if that changed?

---

**The simple version:**

Environmental justice means: the person who cannot say no should not be the person who gets the most pollution. Clean air, clean water, safe land — these belong to everyone, not just to those with power.
