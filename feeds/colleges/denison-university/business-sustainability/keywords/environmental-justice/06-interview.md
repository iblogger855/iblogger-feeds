# Environmental Justice — Journalist Interview
# យុត្តិធម៌បរិស្ថាន — បទសម្ភាសន៍សារព័ត៌មាន

*Author: ichamrong | Date: 2026-05-29*

---

*Interview between Dina Khmer of the Mekong Environment Monitor and Sovannara Pich, human rights lawyer and environmental justice researcher at the Cambodian Center for Human Rights.*

---

**Dina Khmer:** Sovannara, environmental justice is a phrase we hear more often now. What does it actually mean?

**Sovannara Pich:** At its most basic, environmental justice means that no community should bear a disproportionate share of environmental harm because of its race, ethnicity, poverty, or lack of political power. The positive version: all communities deserve meaningful participation in environmental decisions and equal protection from environmental hazards. It is not just about the environment — it is about who has power and who does not.

**Dina:** Is this relevant to Cambodia specifically, or is it a concept that applies mainly to wealthy countries?

**Sovannara:** It is urgently relevant to Cambodia, and in some ways more visibly so than in wealthier countries where the pattern is more obscured by legal complexity. In Cambodia, you can see the pattern directly: economic land concessions for plantations and mining are concentrated in provinces where communities have the weakest land tenure security, the least legal representation, and the least political access. Industrial facilities are sited near poor communities who cannot mount the organized resistance that a politically connected community could.

**Dina:** Can you give me a specific example?

**Sovannara:** The Boeung Kak Lake case is the most documented. Around 4,000 families, mostly poor, were displaced when the lake was filled for commercial development. Compare this to the experience of middle-class neighborhoods in Phnom Penh when development has been proposed near them. Those communities have successfully blocked or significantly modified projects through legal advocacy, media campaigns, and connections to city officials. The Boeung Kak families had none of those resources. The outcome directly reflected that power differential.

**Dina:** Some argue that development — factories, plantations, infrastructure — brings economic benefits that outweigh the costs to affected communities. How do you respond to that?

**Sovannara:** The argument assumes the benefits and costs land on the same people. They rarely do. The economic concession in Mondulkiri generates revenue for a company registered in Singapore and taxes for the national government. The cost — displacement, loss of forest land, disrupted water sources — falls entirely on the indigenous community that was using that land. That is not a trade-off; that is a transfer of wealth from the most vulnerable to the most powerful, subsidized by the environment.

**Dina:** You mentioned indigenous communities. Is the environmental justice framework particularly relevant for Cambodia's indigenous population?

**Sovannara:** Extremely so. Indigenous communities in Ratanakiri, Mondulkiri, Kratie, and Preah Vihear represent a disproportionate share of Cambodia's environmental justice cases. They have three overlapping vulnerabilities: they live in areas with high natural resource value and therefore high development pressure; they have insecure formal land tenure; and their traditional resource management systems and ecological knowledge are typically not recognized in formal regulatory processes. This is exactly what recognition justice addresses — the question of whether the knowledge and values of the community are taken seriously in governance.

**Dina:** What is the difference between distributional justice, procedural justice, and recognition justice? Can you explain those in plain terms?

**Sovannara:** Distributional justice asks: who bears the pollution and who gets the benefits? Procedural justice asks: who actually has a voice in the decisions? And recognition justice asks: whose way of understanding and valuing the environment is treated as legitimate? A project can satisfy one dimension while failing the others. You could, theoretically, consult a community extensively and then ignore everything they said — that is procedural performance without procedural substance. You could compensate people financially for losing a forest that has spiritual significance to them — that is distributional without recognition.

**Dina:** What legal tools currently exist in Cambodia for communities to assert environmental justice claims?

**Sovannara:** The legal framework is limited but not empty. The 2001 Land Law theoretically provides protections for collective land rights of indigenous communities. The 1996 Environmental Protection Law requires environmental impact assessments. There are free prior informed consent principles in ASEAN guidelines that Cambodia has endorsed. The problem is enforcement. EIAs are often prepared by consultants hired by the project proponent, reviewed by ministries with development mandates, without meaningful independent oversight. Community consultation processes are frequently pro forma — one meeting, inadequate notice, information not in local languages.

**Dina:** What reforms would most improve environmental justice in Cambodia?

**Sovannara:** Three things would make the largest difference. First, mandatory independent environmental oversight — EIAs reviewed by a genuinely independent body, not just the line ministry overseeing development. Second, legal standing for affected communities to challenge environmental decisions in court, with access to legal aid. Currently, the barriers to environmental litigation are prohibitive for poor communities. Third, recognition of community environmental knowledge in formal impact assessments — requiring that indigenous and community land use records, ecological knowledge, and cultural valuations be part of the official assessment, not treated as peripheral.

**Dina:** Is there a connection between environmental justice and economic development that sometimes gets lost in this conversation?

**Sovannara:** Absolutely. Sustainable development requires healthy ecosystems. The communities most dependent on healthy ecosystems for food, water, and livelihoods are the communities that environmental injustice harms first. So environmental injustice is not just a moral problem — it is an obstacle to sustainable development. The fishing communities on the Mekong, the forest communities in the Cardamoms, the farming villages along rivers like the Prek Thnot — these communities are, in a very real sense, the stewards of the ecological systems that Cambodian food security depends on. Environmental justice protects those communities, and in doing so, protects the ecological foundation of development itself.

**Dina:** Thank you, Sovannara.
