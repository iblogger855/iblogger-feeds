## The Duck Farmer and the Fish Pond / អ្នកចិញ្ចឹមទា និងស្រះត្រី

In a commune in Takéo, two families shared a slope. At the top lived Mealea, who raised ducks — a few hundred of them, waddling and fouling the water that ran downhill. At the bottom lived Rith, who raised fish in three ponds fed by exactly that runoff. The duck droppings clouded Rith's water and now and then his fish sickened.

For two seasons they quarrelled at the well and the pagoda. Rith said Mealea's ducks were poisoning his livelihood. Mealea said her ducks had grazed that slope since her grandmother's time, and Rith was the newcomer who'd dug ponds in the wrong place. Each was, in his and her own way, right. The harm needed both of them: no ducks, no fouling — but also no ponds, no harm. Neither could see this; they only saw the other as the villain.

The commune chief, an unhurried man who had once attended an agricultural college, sat them both down. He did not declare who was poisoning whom. He did something stranger. He said: "First, let us simply decide who holds the right. The slope is Mealea's by old custom — she has the right to keep her ducks as she pleases. Now. Rith, what is a clean pond worth to you?"

Rith, doing the arithmetic of a man with a debt and a family, named a figure: the sick fish cost him perhaps 400,000 riel a season.

"And Mealea," the chief asked, "what would it cost you to fence the ducks from the upper stream and water them at a separate trough?"

Mealea thought. The fence and the trough would cost her maybe 150,000 riel, once, and a little daily bother.

The chief let the numbers sit in the air. Then Rith understood. The fouling cost him far more than the fix would cost Mealea. So Rith offered to pay for the fence and the trough himself — 150,000 riel out of his pocket — if Mealea would keep her ducks off the upper stream.

This is the first thing the story teaches: **even though the right belonged to Mealea, the two of them could trade their way to the outcome that was worth the most overall — Rith paid, because clean water was worth more to him than the fence cost her.**

Mealea accepted. The fence went up. Her ducks were no worse off at the trough; Rith's fish recovered; he made back his 150,000 in a single good harvest. Both were better off than before, and neither had needed a court, a tax, or a regulation. They had needed only a clear answer to "who holds the right?" and a quiet room to talk.

Here is the part the chief understood and the families did not: **had he given the right to Rith instead** — declaring the stream must run clean — the outcome would have been the *same fence*, only now Mealea would pay for it, or pay Rith to tolerate some fouling, up to what it was worth. Who held the right decided whose wallet opened. It did not decide where the fence ended up.

But the chief also knew the limit of his trick. The next valley over, a single rubber estate's runoff fouled streams used by *forty* families spread across three communes. He tried the same patient method there and it collapsed. Forty families could not agree; some demanded too much, some refused to chip in while hoping to enjoy cleaner water anyway, and the meetings dragged for months with nothing settled. The cost of *getting everyone to agree* was greater than the problem itself.

"Two neighbours can shake hands," the chief told a visiting student years later. "Forty cannot. For two, you need only clear rights and a cup of tea. For forty, you need the government — a rule, or a tax. Knowing which situation you are in," he said, "is the whole of the wisdom."
