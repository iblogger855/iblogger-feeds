# Coase Theorem — Analogy Bridge
# ទ្រឹស្តីបទ Coase — ស្ពានប្រៀបធៀប

*Author: ichamrong | Date: 2026-06-01*

---

## Analogy 1: The Shared Apartment Wall / ជញ្ជាំងផ្ទះល្វែងរួម

**The Story:**
Two flatmates share a wall. One loves loud music at night; the other needs silence to study. If we declare the music-lover has the right to play, the studier — if quiet matters more to her — pays him to use headphones. If instead we declare the studier has the right to silence, the music-lover — if the music matters more to him — pays her to tolerate it. Either way, headphones-or-music ends up wherever it's valued most. The rule about who has the "right" only decides whose wallet opens. Because they're two people who can simply talk over coffee, they settle it themselves.

**The Mapping:**

| Analogy Element | Coase Theorem Element | ខ្មែរ |
|---|---|---|
| Who may play music | Assignment of the right | ការប្រគល់សិទ្ធិ |
| Paying for headphones | Bargaining payment | ការទូទាត់ការចរចារ |
| Two flatmates, one wall | Low transaction costs | ថ្លៃប្រតិបត្តិការទាប |
| Music lands where valued | Efficient outcome | លទ្ធផលមានប្រសិទ្ធភាព |

**Where the Analogy Breaks Down:**
Two flatmates can talk in a minute. If a hundred neighbours were affected by the noise, gathering and agreeing would be so costly the deal would never happen — the theorem would fail.

## Analogy 2: The Mango Tree Over the Fence / ដើមស្វាយលើបង្គោលរបង

**The Story:**
A mango tree grows on Sophea's land, but half its branches — and falling fruit — hang over Dara's yard, dropping mangoes that rot and draw ants. Who owns the overhanging fruit? Suppose custom says Sophea owns all the fruit. Then Dara, bothered by the rot, pays Sophea to prune the branches back. Suppose instead custom says fallen fruit belongs to the yard it lands in. Then Sophea, who wants those mangoes, pays Dara to leave the branches and hand back the fruit. Either way the branches end up at the length that serves the higher value — fewer ants or more mangoes — and the neighbours, who see each other daily, work it out directly.

**The Mapping:**

| Analogy Element | Coase Theorem Element | ខ្មែរ |
|---|---|---|
| Who owns the fallen fruit | Property right | សិទ្ធិកម្មសិទ្ធិ |
| Paying to prune or keep | Side payment | ការទូទាត់បន្ថែម |
| Neighbours who know each other | Low transaction costs | ថ្លៃប្រតិបត្តិការទាប |
| Branch length that fits best | Efficient allocation | ការបែងចែកមានប្រសិទ្ធភាព |

**Where the Analogy Breaks Down:**
The fruit harms one neighbour. Real pollution often harms strangers across a whole region who never meet, making the friendly fence-line chat impossible.

## Analogy 3: The Beekeeper and the Orchard / អ្នកចិញ្ចឹមឃ្មុំ និងចម្ការ

**The Story:**
A beekeeper's hives sit beside a longan orchard. The bees pollinate the orchard (a benefit to the grower) and the blossoms feed the bees (a benefit to the keeper). Whoever is granted the "right" — the keeper to place hives, or the grower to control who's near the trees — the two can pay each other to get the number of hives just right for both. If pollination is worth a lot, the grower may even pay the keeper to bring *more* hives. The right decides who pays; the bargaining gets the hive count to where total value is highest.

**The Mapping:**

| Analogy Element | Coase Theorem Element | ខ្មែរ |
|---|---|---|
| Right to place hives | Assigned property right | សិទ្ធិកម្មសិទ្ធិ |
| Grower pays for more hives | Bargaining over the activity level | ការចរចារកម្រិតសកម្មភាព |
| Two known parties | Low transaction costs | ថ្លៃប្រតិបត្តិការទាប |
| Optimal number of hives | Efficient outcome | លទ្ធផលមានប្រសិទ្ធភាព |

**Where the Analogy Breaks Down:**
Bees and blossoms involve two cooperative parties. When an externality has many uncoordinated victims and a hostile polluter, the cooperative bargaining of the beekeeper story rarely materialises.

---

## Core Insight Across All Three Analogies / ការយល់ដឹងស្នូល

All three show the same principle: **when the right is clear and the parties can cheaply talk, they bargain their way to the most valuable outcome — and that outcome is the same no matter who was handed the right**. The right decides who pays; bargaining decides what happens. The whole fragility of the idea lies in those two conditions — clear rights and low transaction costs — and most real environmental problems fail the second one.
