# Coase Theorem — Feynman Explanation
# ទ្រឹស្តីបទ Coase — ការពន្យល់បែប Feynman

*Author: ichamrong | Date: 2026-06-01*

---

Two neighbours share a wall. On one side, a man keeps a noisy rooster that crows at dawn. On the other side, a woman works the night shift and sleeps in the morning. The rooster wakes her every day. Here is a conflict — and most people's first thought is: somebody official should decide who's right.

Ronald Coase had a stranger, deeper thought: **maybe the two of them can just sort it out themselves — if we first decide who has the right, and if talking to each other is easy.**

---

Watch how it works. Suppose we say the man has the right to keep his rooster. The woman, losing sleep, has options. If her sleep is worth more to her than the rooster is worth to him, she offers him money to get rid of it. He looks at the money, decides it's more than the rooster's worth to him, and takes the deal. The rooster goes. Both are happier.

Now suppose we'd decided the *other* way — the woman has the right to quiet. Then the man, if he really loves that rooster more than her quiet is worth to her, pays *her* to tolerate it. She takes the money. The rooster stays.

Here's the punchline that surprised everyone: **in both cases, the rooster ends up wherever it's worth more.** If the woman's sleep matters more, the rooster goes — no matter who held the right. If the rooster matters more, it stays — no matter who held the right. Who holds the right only decides who pays whom. It doesn't change what finally happens.

---

So why don't we solve every pollution problem this way, with neighbours bargaining? Because Coase added two big "ifs."

**If the right is clear.** If nobody knows who's entitled to what — who owns the air, who owns the quiet — there's nothing to bargain over.

**If talking is cheap.** Two neighbours over one wall can talk easily. But imagine a factory whose smoke bothers ten thousand people in fifty villages. Getting all of them in a room, agreeing, collecting money, stopping anyone from cheating — that costs a fortune in time and trouble. Economists call this **transaction cost**. When it's huge, the bargaining never happens.

---

That's the whole lesson, really. When transaction costs are low and rights are clear — two neighbours, one wall — people solve their own externalities, and government can stay out. When transaction costs are huge — millions of people, the whole atmosphere — bargaining collapses, and now you need a tax or a rule.

---

**The simple version:**

If you make it clear who has the right, and it's easy for the parties to talk, they'll trade their way to the best outcome by themselves — and it'll be the same outcome no matter who got the right. The only reason we ever need government to step in is that, for big problems, getting everyone to talk and agree costs more than the problem is worth.
