# Coase Theorem — Journalist Interview
# ទ្រឹស្តីបទ Coase — បទសម្ភាសន៍សារព័ត៌មាន

*Author: ichamrong | Date: 2026-06-01*

---

*Interview between Ratana Chan of the Phnom Penh Economic Tribune and Dr. Sokunthea Pich, environmental economist at the Royal University of Agriculture.*

---

**Ratana Chan:** Dr. Sokunthea, the Coase Theorem is famous but slippery. What's the one idea at its heart?

**Dr. Sokunthea Pich:** That government is not always the answer to pollution. Ronald Coase showed that if you make the property right clear — who's entitled to what — and if the affected parties can bargain cheaply, they'll negotiate their way to the efficient outcome themselves. And here's the surprise: the outcome is the same regardless of who you give the right to. Who holds the right decides who pays whom. It doesn't decide what finally happens.

**Ratana:** That feels counterintuitive. Give me the intuition.

**Dr. Sokunthea Pich:** Picture a factory and a laundry next door. If the factory has the right to emit smoke, but clean air is worth more to the laundry than the smoke is worth to the factory, the laundry pays the factory to cut back. If instead the laundry has the right to clean air, but the factory's output is worth more than the laundry's loss, the factory pays the laundry for permission to emit. Either way, the activity ends up at the level where total value is highest. The right is about distribution — who's richer afterward — not about efficiency.

**Ratana:** So does this mean we don't need carbon taxes or regulations?

**Dr. Sokunthea Pich:** No — and this is where people misuse Coase. The theorem has two demanding conditions: clear, enforceable rights, and low transaction costs. Transaction costs are the costs of finding the other party, negotiating, writing and enforcing the deal. When those are low — two neighbours over a fence — bargaining works beautifully and government can stay out. When they're enormous — billions of carbon emitters and victims across generations — bargaining is impossible, and you fall back on a tax or a cap. Coase doesn't abolish government; he tells you *when* it's needed.

**Ratana:** Can you give a Cambodian example where Coasean bargaining actually works?

**Dr. Sokunthea Pich:** Small, local, few-party disputes. An upstream duck farmer fouling a downstream fish pond; a workshop whose noise bothers the guesthouse next door; two families sharing an irrigation channel. These have few, identifiable parties who already know each other. With a clear understanding of who holds the right, they routinely settle it with a side payment — one pays the other to change behaviour. That's Coase in action, and it happens at the commune level all the time without anyone naming the theorem.

**Ratana:** And where does it fail in Cambodia?

**Dr. Sokunthea Pich:** Anything large and diffuse. Sand dredging on the Mekong affecting hundreds of riverbank households; a rubber estate's runoff crossing several communes; air pollution over a city. There are too many victims, they can't easily organise, and some will hold out hoping others pay while they enjoy the benefit free. Transaction costs explode, bargaining never forms, and you need collective rules — exactly what Coase predicts. His real gift to a policymaker is diagnostic: before reaching for a regulation, ask *why* private parties can't solve it themselves, and you'll usually find a transaction-cost problem you can sometimes lower more cheaply than you can regulate.

**Ratana:** That's a subtle policy lesson. Can you make it concrete?

**Dr. Sokunthea Pich:** Sometimes the best intervention isn't a tax — it's clarifying rights and cutting transaction costs so bargaining *can* happen. Formal land titles, a registry of water rights, a village mediation body, a standard contract template — these let parties strike Coasean deals that were previously too costly to arrange. Cambodia's land-titling efforts, whatever their flaws, are partly this: clear rights are the precondition for people to solve disputes without the state adjudicating every one.

**Ratana:** Final question. One thing for a young Cambodian to take from Coase?

**Dr. Sokunthea Pich:** That clear rights and the ability to talk can solve more problems than people expect — and that when they can't, the reason is almost always the hidden cost of getting everyone to agree. Find that cost, and you've found the real problem.

**Ratana:** Thank you, Dr. Sokunthea.
