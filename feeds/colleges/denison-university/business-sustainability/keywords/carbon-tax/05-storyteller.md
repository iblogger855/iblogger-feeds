## The Brickmaker and the Price of Smoke / អ្នកដុតឥដ្ឋ និងតម្លៃនៃផ្សែង

Sokha ran a brick kiln on the edge of Kampong Speu, the kind you see from the road with a black plume bending in the wind. He had fired bricks the same way his father had: stack the clay, feed the kiln with whatever burned cheapest — scrap wood, old tyres, sawdust soaked in oil. The tyres were the cheapest fuel of all, and they made the blackest smoke. Sokha never thought about the smoke. It went up and away. It was free.

The villages downwind thought about it. Children coughed. Washing hung out to dry came in grey. But none of that appeared in Sokha's account book, so none of it changed how he fired his bricks.

Then the province, following a new national rule, put a charge on the dirtiest imported fuels and on the tyres sold to kilns. Not a ban — Sokha could still buy tyres. But now each load carried a fee tied to how much carbon and soot it threw into the air. The blacker the fuel, the higher the charge.

The first month, Sokha simply paid it and grumbled. He treated the fee like bad weather, something to endure.

The second month, he did the arithmetic a man does when money is leaving his hands. The tyres, once the cheapest fuel, were now — with the charge — more expensive than rice husk, an agricultural waste sold cheap by the mills nearby. Rice husk burned cleaner and, after the charge, cost him less per brick. He switched. Not out of conscience. Out of the account book.

This is the first thing the story teaches: **a carbon charge does not order anyone to be clean. It changes which choice is cheapest, and people follow the cheapest choice on their own.**

Sokha was not the only one. The kiln across the valley, run by a careful woman named Bopha, went further. She had been eyeing a better-sealed kiln design that burned far less fuel but cost money to build. Before the charge, the savings were too small to justify it. After the charge, the fuel she would save was now expensive fuel, and the new kiln paid for itself in two seasons. She built it.

This is the second thing the story teaches: **the same charge pushes different emitters to different solutions — some switch fuel, some rebuild entirely — and each finds the cheapest path for their own situation.** No inspector could have known that Sokha should switch fuel while Bopha should rebuild. The price knew, because it let each of them decide.

The money the province collected did not vanish. Part of it funded a clinic in one of the downwind villages and subsidised cleaner cookstoves for the poorest households — people who had breathed Sokha's smoke for years and now, for once, got something back.

A year on, a student from Phnom Penh came to interview Sokha for a project. She asked if he supported the carbon charge. He thought about it, wiping clay from his hands.

"I did not like it," he said. "But my bricks cost the same, my lungs are better, and the smoke is grey now instead of black." He paused. "The smoke was never free. We just were not the ones paying. Now the price tells the truth."
