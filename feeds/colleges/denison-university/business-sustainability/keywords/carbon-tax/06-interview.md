# Carbon Tax — Journalist Interview
# ពន្ធកាបូន — បទសម្ភាសន៍សារព័ត៌មាន

*Author: ichamrong | Date: 2026-06-01*

---

*Interview between Ratana Chan of the Phnom Penh Economic Tribune and Dr. Sokunthea Pich, environmental economist at the Royal University of Agriculture.*

---

**Ratana Chan:** Dr. Sokunthea, a carbon tax sounds like just another tax. Why do economists across the political spectrum tend to like it?

**Dr. Sokunthea Pich:** Because it fixes a specific failure cheaply. Right now the atmosphere is a free dumping ground for carbon. When something is free, we overuse it. A carbon tax simply puts a price on that dumping equal to the harm it causes. What economists love is *how* it cuts emissions: it doesn't tell anyone what technology to use. Every emitter compares the tax to their own cost of cutting and chooses. The cheapest cuts happen first, everywhere, automatically. No regulator could ever gather that much information.

**Ratana:** Give me a concrete example of that "cheapest cuts first" idea.

**Dr. Sokunthea Pich:** Take two factories facing the same carbon price. One can switch to solar for very little — maybe it already has the roof space and good sun, which most of Cambodia does. It cuts deeply. The other runs a process that is genuinely hard to clean, so it cuts a little and pays the tax on the rest. The total reduction is achieved at the lowest possible cost to the economy, because the easy cuts were not wasted on the hard cases. A command like "everyone cut 30 percent" would force the hard factory to spend a fortune while the easy one barely tried.

**Ratana:** Critics say a carbon tax hurts the poor. Is that true?

**Dr. Sokunthea Pich:** A bare carbon tax can be regressive, yes — poorer households spend a larger share of income on fuel and electricity. But that is only half the policy. The tax raises revenue, and what you do with it decides everything. Return it as an equal per-person dividend, and a poor family that emits little gets back more than it paid, while a wealthy household with three cars and a big house pays more than it gets. Done that way, the policy is progressive. Judging the tax without the rebate is like judging a meal after eating only the chili.

**Ratana:** How is this different from cap-and-trade, which Cambodia's trading partners use?

**Dr. Sokunthea Pich:** They are cousins. A carbon tax fixes the *price* of carbon and lets the quantity of emissions land wherever it lands. Cap-and-trade fixes the *quantity* — the cap — and lets the market set the price of permits. If you most want certainty about how much you cut, you cap. If you most want certainty about the cost businesses face, you tax. For a developing economy worried about investment costs, the price certainty of a tax is often easier to plan around.

**Ratana:** Is there a risk that factories just move to a country with no carbon tax?

**Dr. Sokunthea Pich:** That's called carbon leakage, and it's a real concern. The emerging answer is a carbon border adjustment — major importers like the EU now charge a carbon fee on imports from countries without their own carbon price. The irony is that this gives Cambodia a reason to adopt its *own* carbon tax: if our exporters will be charged at the border anyway, we would rather collect that revenue at home and invest it here than hand it to a foreign treasury.

**Ratana:** So a carbon tax could actually be in Cambodia's economic interest, not just its environmental interest?

**Dr. Sokunthea Pich:** Increasingly, yes. The world is moving toward pricing carbon at the border. A country that prices its own carbon, recycles the revenue into clean energy and protects poor households, and keeps the money at home is positioning its exporters for that world. The carbon tax stops being a sacrifice and becomes a strategy.

**Ratana:** Final question. If a young Cambodian policymaker remembers one thing about the carbon tax, what should it be?

**Dr. Sokunthea Pich:** That a price is the most honest instrument we have. It does not preach, it does not micromanage — it just makes the cost of carbon visible and lets a million decisions sort themselves out. Set the price near the real damage, give the money back wisely, and the rest takes care of itself.

**Ratana:** Thank you, Dr. Sokunthea.
