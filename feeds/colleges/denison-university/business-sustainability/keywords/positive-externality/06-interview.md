# Positive Externality — Journalist Interview
# ផលជះក្រៅប្រព័ន្ធវិជ្ជមាន — បទសម្ភាសន៍សារព័ត៌មាន

*Author: ichamrong | Date: 2026-06-01*

---

*Interview between Ratana Chan of the Phnom Penh Economic Tribune and Dr. Sokunthea Pich, environmental economist at the Royal University of Agriculture.*

---

**Ratana Chan:** Dr. Sokunthea, most people have heard that pollution is a market problem. But you say the *opposite* — beneficial things — can also be a market failure. How?

**Dr. Sokunthea Pich:** Yes, and it's the more hopeful half of the story. A positive externality is a benefit your action creates for people who didn't pay for it. When you vaccinate, you protect others. When you plant trees, the whole watershed gains. When you educate a child, the community gains a capable adult. The catch is that the person deciding only weighs the benefit to *themselves*. So they do less of these wonderful things than would be best for everyone. The market quietly underproduces good.

**Ratana:** Why doesn't the market just sort it out, the way it sorts out the price of rice?

**Dr. Sokunthea Pich:** Because the producer can't bill the beneficiaries. If I plant a forest that protects your village downstream, I have no way to send your village an invoice. The benefit leaks out for free, so I have no financial reason to plant the optimal amount. The price mechanism only works when the person who creates value can capture it. With positive externalities, they can't, so they create too little value.

**Ratana:** What's the remedy?

**Dr. Sokunthea Pich:** A subsidy — economists call it a Pigouvian subsidy. You pay people to do more of the beneficial thing, so their private incentive lines up with society's interest. Free vaccines, free or cheap schooling, payments for reforestation, feed-in tariffs for solar power. Each one is the public stepping in to reward a benefit the market wouldn't pay for on its own.

**Ratana:** Give me a Cambodian example.

**Dr. Sokunthea Pich:** Mangrove restoration in Koh Kong is a clear one. A coastal community that replants mangroves gets some direct benefit — more crabs and fish nearby. But the bigger benefits flow outward: storm protection for villages down the coast, nursery grounds for offshore fisheries, and carbon stored for the entire planet. The community can't charge for any of that, so on their own they'd replant far too little. Payments for ecosystem services — from the government or international funds — act exactly like the subsidy theory prescribes, lifting replanting toward the level society actually needs.

**Ratana:** How central is this idea to the sustainability agenda?

**Dr. Sokunthea Pich:** It's one of the two pillars. Pollution is a *negative* externality — the market does too much of something harmful, so you tax it. Green investment is a *positive* externality — the market does too little of something beneficial, so you subsidize it. Almost every climate policy is one or the other: a carbon tax on the bad, a green subsidy on the good. Understanding positive externalities is what justifies public money going into clean energy, public transit, and conservation, rather than leaving it all to private profit.

**Ratana:** Critics say subsidies are wasteful or get captured by special interests. Fair?

**Dr. Sokunthea Pich:** A fair warning, not a refutation. The theory says subsidize *up to the value of the external benefit* — no more. Overshoot, and you waste public money or prop up things that don't deserve it. The discipline is to estimate the genuine spillover benefit and target the subsidy to it. Done sloppily, subsidies are indeed captured and wasted. Done well, they're how a society buys the clean air, healthy population, and resilient coastlines that no individual would purchase alone.

**Ratana:** One sentence for our readers?

**Dr. Sokunthea Pich:** When good spills onto others for free, the market makes too little of it — so a wise society pays for the spillover, and that is the economic heart of every argument for public health, education, and a green transition.

**Ratana:** Thank you, Dr. Sokunthea.
