# Positive Externality — Feynman Explanation
# ផលជះក្រៅប្រព័ន្ធវិជ្ជមាន — ការពន្យល់បែប Feynman

*Author: ichamrong | Date: 2026-06-01*

---

Your neighbor plants a big shade tree in front of her house. She planted it for herself — for her own cool veranda. But here's the thing: *you* sit in that shade too when you walk by. So do the fruit-sellers who park their carts under it. So does the whole street, which is a little cooler and prettier because of one tree she paid for alone.

That spillover of good — the benefit she creates for everyone else without being paid for it — is a **positive externality** (ផលជះក្រៅប្រព័ន្ធវិជ្ជមាន).

---

Now ask the quiet, important question: *did she plant the right number of trees?*

She decided based on what *she* gets — her own cool veranda — weighed against what *she* pays. She didn't count the cool shade she gives the whole street, because nobody pays her for it. So she planted, let's say, one tree.

But if you added up all the good she does — her shade plus everyone else's shade plus the cleaner air — the street would have been better off if she'd planted *three* trees. She stopped at one because she only counts her own slice of the benefit.

This is the whole idea: **when the good of an action spills onto others, the person doing it does too little of it.** Not because she's selfish exactly — just because she's counting only her share.

---

It happens everywhere good leaks outward:

- You get a vaccine to protect yourself — but you also protect everyone you didn't infect. So left alone, fewer people vaccinate than would be best for all.
- A farmer plants trees on his hill — but the whole valley downstream gets cleaner water and firmer soil. So he plants fewer trees than the valley would want.
- A family sends a child to school — but the whole community gains a more capable, more honest citizen. So, left to private cost alone, less schooling happens than is good for everyone.

In every case: lots of good spills out, the doer can't collect for it, so we get *too little* of a wonderful thing.

---

How do you fix "too little of a good thing"? You pay people to do more of it. The government gives a **subsidy** — money toward the tree, the vaccine, the schooling — so the doer's private cost drops and they're willing to do the amount that's actually best for everybody.

---

**The simple version:**

A positive externality is good that splashes onto bystanders for free. Because the doer only counts their own splash, they do too little. So the world quietly under-supplies vaccines, trees, education, clean energy — all the things whose benefits leak outward. The cure is to reward those activities, with a subsidy, until we get the generous amount everyone would have chosen if they'd been counting all the good.
