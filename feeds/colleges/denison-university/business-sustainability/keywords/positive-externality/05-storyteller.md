## The Woman Who Planted on the Bare Hill / ស្ត្រីដែលដាំដើមឈើលើភ្នំទទេ

Above the village of Prek Chrey rose a hill that had been stripped bare years ago, when the timber men came and left. After them came the rains, and the rains took the soil, and every wet season a brown flood of mud came down the slope, into the rice paddies, into the well, into the one road that connected the village to the market town.

Sreypov owned the small plot at the foot of that hill. One year, tired of digging mud out of her irrigation channel, she did a stubborn thing: she began planting trees on the lower slope. Not for the village. For herself — to stop the mud reaching *her* channel.

She planted thirty saplings. They took. Her channel ran cleaner. She was satisfied and she stopped.

This is the first thing the story teaches: a person decides how much of a good thing to do by weighing what *they* get against what *they* pay. Sreypov planted exactly enough trees to protect her own plot, and not one more, because the rest of the benefit was none of her concern.

But the rest of the benefit was real. Those thirty trees, it turned out, slowed the flood for the three plots beside hers too. The well below ran clearer. The road washed out less often. Families Sreypov barely knew were quietly better off because of trees she had paid for alone. She never saw most of that good, and certainly no one paid her for it.

This is the second thing the story teaches: when you do something good, the good often spills far past you, onto strangers who give you nothing in return. Economists call that spillover a positive externality.

Now here is the ache of it. If the whole hillside had been planted, the entire village would have been spared the floods — the paddies, the well, the road, all of it. That would have taken three hundred trees, not thirty. But who would plant three hundred? Not Sreypov; twenty-nine-thirtieths of that benefit would go to other people. Not her neighbours, each of whom reasoned the same way she had. So the hill stayed mostly bare, the floods kept coming, and everyone privately did the small sensible thing while the village as a whole got far too few trees.

A young woman from the district agriculture office came one dry season with a clipboard and an idea. The office, she said, would *pay* villagers for every tree planted on the hill that survived a year — a small sum per tree. Suddenly the arithmetic changed. Now Sreypov, and the man next door, and the widow up the lane, each found it worth their while to plant far more than their own plot needed, because the payment stood in for all the good they were doing the others.

In three seasons the hill was green. The floods shrank to a trickle of clear runoff. The road held.

Years later Sreypov's son, studying economics in the city, came home and looked up at the forested hill. "They had to pay you," he said, half to himself, "because the trees did most of their good for people who weren't you. You'd never have planted enough on your own."

"Of course not," Sreypov said, surprised he found it remarkable. "Why would I plant a forest for the whole village out of my own pocket? But pay me a little for each tree, and I'll plant the hill green. That payment was the village finally counting the good I do for them."
