# Ecosystem Services — Analogy Bridge
# សេវាកម្មបរិស្ថាន — ស្ពានប្រៀបប្រដូច

*Author: ichamrong | Date: 2026-05-29*

## Analogy 1: The Unpaid Factory (រោងចក្រដែលមិនបានទទួលប្រាក់ខែ)

**The analogy:** Tonle Sap Lake is a fish factory that runs itself, hires no workers, sends no invoice, and produces 400,000 tonnes of fish per year.

**Mapping:**
| Factory component | Ecosystem equivalent |
|---|---|
| Factory building | Lake basin and flooded forest |
| Raw materials | Nutrients from Mekong floods |
| Workers | Biological processes (algae, insects, spawning cycles) |
| Products | Fish, clean water, flood buffering |
| Salary bill | Zero — nature works for free |
| Quality control | Biodiversity and hydrological regularity |

**Where the analogy holds:** Just like a real factory needs a roof over its head and electricity to function, the lake needs intact forest cover and seasonal floods. Destroy either and the factory shuts down.

**Where the analogy breaks down:** A real factory depreciates and can be rebuilt. When an ecosystem collapses — as when the Aral Sea was drained — it may take centuries to recover, or never recover at all. The ecosystem factory has no replacement parts warehouse.

## Analogy 2: The Village Elder Who Remembers the Rules (ចាស់ទុំភូមិដែលចងចាំក្បួន)

**The analogy:** Every Cambodian village once had a customary elder who managed the community forest and fishing grounds. No one paid this elder a government salary, yet without the rules they enforced, the fish would disappear within a generation.

**Mapping:**
| Elder's role | Ecosystem service equivalent |
|---|---|
| Sets fishing season limits | Natural stock regulation |
| Protects spawning areas | Supporting services |
| Resolves water disputes | Regulating services |
| Shares traditional knowledge | Cultural services |
| Receives no formal pay | Ecosystem receives no market payment |

**Where the analogy holds:** The value only becomes visible when the role disappears — when overfishing empties the lake, or when flood-season fires destroy the spawning grounds.

**Where the analogy breaks down:** An elder can speak up, negotiate, or be replaced. An ecosystem cannot advocate for itself; it can only collapse silently. This is why economists must assign it a voice through valuation.

## Analogy 3: The Body's Organs (សរីរាង្គរបស់រាងកាយ)

**The analogy:** An ecosystem is like your body. Your liver, kidneys, and lungs do not charge you rent — but if any one of them stops working, you notice immediately.

**Mapping:**
| Body organ | Ecosystem service |
|---|---|
| Lungs — filter air | Forests and wetlands — filter air and water |
| Kidneys — remove toxins | Wetlands — absorb and break down pollutants |
| Liver — process nutrients | Soil microbes — decompose organic matter, cycle nutrients |
| Skin — regulate temperature | Forests — regulate local and regional climate |
| Heart — circulate blood | Rivers — distribute water and nutrients across landscapes |

**Cambodian application:** When mangroves in Koh Kong (កោះកុង) are cut for shrimp ponds, it is like removing the skin from the coastline. Storm surges that the mangroves would have absorbed now strike villages directly.

**Where the analogy holds:** The interdependence is real — remove one "organ" and others weaken. Cambodia's Mekong dams disrupt the flood pulse; without that pulse, the Tonle Sap's spawning cycle collapses, fishermen lose income, and protein malnutrition rises.

**Where the analogy breaks down:** A body is one organism with centralized repair mechanisms. An ecosystem is a distributed network — it can lose pieces and reorganize around them, up to a tipping point. The challenge is that we rarely know where that tipping point is until we've already crossed it.
