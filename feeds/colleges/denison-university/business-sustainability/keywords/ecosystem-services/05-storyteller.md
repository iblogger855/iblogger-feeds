## The Accountant Who Forgot the Lake | គណនេយ្យករដែលភ្លេចបឹង

Sovann had been the district accountant for eleven years, and he was proud of his numbers. Every quarter, the spreadsheet balanced. Every year, the district's revenue climbed — timber licenses, sand extraction permits, land concessions along the lake shore. The governor called Sovann's reports "proof that development was working."

The fishermen didn't read reports. They read the water.

Old Boran, who had fished the Tonle Sap since before Sovann was born, noticed it first — the flood season in 2024 came two weeks late and retreated three weeks early. The flooded forest, where the fish always swam to feed and lay eggs, was half the size it used to be. Half the trees had been logged for a resort development the previous dry season. The stumps stood in shallow brown water like broken teeth.

"The fish will not come back the same," Boran told his son. "They need the forest to grow up in."

His son didn't believe him. He had a new engine on his boat and new nets, bought on credit. Surely better tools meant better catches.

The next year, catches fell thirty percent. The year after, fifty. The young men in the village started driving to Phnom Penh, taking factory jobs sewing clothes for export. They sent remittances home. The village survived — but it no longer fished.

Sovann's spreadsheet did not record any of this. The resort showed up as investment income. The timber permits showed up as license fees. The fish that vanished showed up as nothing, because they had never been a line item.

When the Ministry of Environment sent a team to audit the district's natural assets, Sovann was confused by their questions. They wanted to know the value of the lake's flood buffering, the water regulation provided by forest cover, the protein security for 40,000 families. He had no answers. These things had no prices.

The audit team estimated that the lake and surrounding forest had been delivering services worth $47 million per year to the district — in fish protein, flood absorption, water purification, and climate cooling. In three years of poorly regulated development, permits had generated $8 million in government fees. The district had traded $141 million in ongoing services for $8 million in one-time revenue.

Sovann stared at the numbers for a long time.

He thought of his mother, who used to dry fish on the roof of their house and tell him, *"The lake is our salary. Don't spend the bank account."* He had thought she was being old-fashioned.

He had been the most careful accountant in the province. He had counted everything that had a price. He had forgotten to count the things that had a value.

That evening, he drove to the lake shore and sat until dark, watching the remaining herons hunt the shallows. He pulled out a fresh ledger page and, for the first time, wrote at the top: *Assets We Do Not Own But Cannot Afford to Lose.*

He didn't know yet what the right policy was — payments to forest communities, tighter permits, restoration funds. But he knew now that what had no price was not the same as what had no worth.

The lake was not a backdrop to the economy. It was the economy. It had just never learned to send an invoice.

---

*This story illustrates the concept of **ecosystem services** (សេវាកម្មបរិស្ថាន) — the benefits that functioning natural systems provide to human societies, which markets systematically fail to measure or protect.*
