# Ecosystem Services — Journalist Interview
# សេវាកម្មបរិស្ថាន — បទសម្ភាសន៍អ្នកសារព័ត៌មាន

*Author: ichamrong | Date: 2026-05-29*
*Cambodian journalist Sreymom interviews Dr. Chan Savuth, environmental economist at the Royal University of Phnom Penh*

---

**Sreymom:** Dr. Savuth, "ecosystem services" sounds like academic jargon. What does it actually mean in plain language?

**Dr. Savuth:** Think of nature as a workforce that never clocks off, never asks for a raise, and never goes on strike. The Tonle Sap Lake produces fish protein for three million Cambodians every year. The Cardamom Mountains filter clean water for rice farmers downstream. Mangroves in Koh Kong act as a sea wall that protects coastal villages from storm surges. None of these workers receive a salary. That is what we call ecosystem services (សេវាកម្មបរិស្ថាន) — the benefits that healthy natural systems provide to people.

**Sreymom:** Why should ordinary Cambodians care about this concept?

**Dr. Savuth:** Because ordinary Cambodians are the primary beneficiaries — and the primary losers when these services disappear. A farmer in Kampong Chhnang does not need to understand ecological theory to know that when the flood comes late, his rice yield suffers. A fisherman in Kampong Phluk does not need an economics degree to know that fewer flooded trees means fewer fish. The concept just gives us language to describe something that rural Cambodians have always known: you cannot separate your livelihood from the health of the land and water around you.

**Sreymom:** Can you give us a concrete number for what Cambodia's ecosystems are worth?

**Dr. Savuth:** Estimates vary, but the Tonle Sap fishery alone — the provisioning service — is valued at roughly $350 million per year at local market prices. When you add flood regulation, water purification, and cultural services, some studies put the lake system's total annual value above $1 billion. The Cardamom Mountains' water regulation service has been estimated at $114 million per year for downstream agriculture. These are conservative figures. And almost none of it appears in Cambodia's national accounts.

**Sreymom:** What happens when these services are lost? Are there examples from Cambodia?

**Dr. Savuth:** Yes, unfortunately. Mekong mainstream dams — particularly the ones built upstream in China and Laos — have already changed the flood pulse that drives the Tonle Sap's productivity. In 2019 and 2020, the lake reached its lowest recorded levels in modern history. Fish catches dropped dramatically. Families who had fished for generations could not feed themselves. In Koh Kong (កោះកុង), mangrove clearing for shrimp aquaculture in the 1990s and 2000s left coastal villages exposed to typhoon damage that the mangroves would have absorbed. The shrimp farms mostly failed within a decade, but the mangroves took decades to partially recover.

**Sreymom:** Cambodia has a REDD+ program. Is that related to ecosystem services?

**Dr. Savuth:** Directly. REDD+ — which stands for Reducing Emissions from Deforestation and Forest Degradation (ការកាត់បន្ថយការបំភាយឧស្ម័នពីការកាប់ព្រៃ) — is essentially a payment for ecosystem services scheme at the international scale. Developed countries pay Cambodia to keep its forests standing, because standing forests absorb carbon dioxide — a global regulating service. The forest communities that protect those forests receive a share of the payments. It is one of the most promising tools we have, but the payments must be large enough to compete with the economic pressure to clear land.

**Sreymom:** Rice paddies in Cambodia release methane, a greenhouse gas. Does that count as an ecosystem "disservice"?

**Dr. Savuth:** That is a sharp question. Yes, some ecologists do talk about ecosystem disservices — cases where natural or semi-natural systems produce costs as well as benefits. Rice paddy methane is a real issue; Cambodia's paddies are a significant source of national greenhouse gas emissions. But rice is also a provisioning service — the food security of the entire country. The policy challenge is to reduce the methane without reducing the food production, perhaps through water management techniques that minimize the anaerobic conditions that produce methane.

**Sreymom:** What is the single most important thing the Cambodian government could do to protect ecosystem services?

**Dr. Savuth:** Integrate natural capital accounting into the national budget. Right now, a government official who approves a mining concession that destroys a watershed shows a financial gain on the books — the license fee. The loss of the watershed's water services — worth potentially ten times more — does not appear anywhere. Until we change the accounting, the incentives point in the wrong direction. Cambodia is actually a signatory to international agreements on natural capital accounting. The framework exists. We just need the political will to use it.

**Sreymom:** Final question — are you optimistic or pessimistic about Cambodia's ecological future?

**Dr. Savuth:** I am cautiously hopeful. The community fisheries program, the protected area network, REDD+, and growing public awareness — these are real achievements. But the window to protect what remains is narrowing, especially with upstream dam construction continuing and climate change making the Mekong's hydrology less predictable. The Tonle Sap flood pulse that built this civilization over thousands of years is genuinely at risk. What we do in the next ten years will determine whether the lake Cambodia's children inherit is the lake of legends, or a much diminished remnant.
