# Foreign Exchange — Journalist Interview
# ការប្តូរប្រាក់បរទេស — បទសម្ភាសន៍សារព័ត៌មាន

*Author: ichamrong | Date: 2026-06-01*

---

*Interview between Ratana Chan of the Phnom Penh Economic Tribune and Dr. Sokunthea Pich, international finance economist at the Royal University of Agriculture.*

---

**Ratana Chan:** Dr. Sokunthea, the foreign-exchange market is said to be the largest market on earth, trading trillions a day. Why is it so central?

**Dr. Sokunthea Pich:** Because nearly every cross-border transaction passes through it. Whenever value crosses a currency border — an export, an import, a tourist's holiday, an investment, a loan — someone must convert one money into another. Foreign exchange is the plumbing of the entire global economy. And unlike a stock or a commodity, it touches *every* country and *every* internationally active business at once.

**Ratana:** At its simplest, what is an exchange rate?

**Dr. Sokunthea Pich:** Just the price of one currency in terms of another — how many riel for a dollar, how many dollars for a euro. And like any price, it's set by supply and demand. People who want Cambodian goods or assets create demand for riel; people who want foreign goods or assets create demand for foreign currency. Where those meet sets the rate. The twist is that capital flows — investors moving money chasing returns or fleeing risk — can dwarf trade flows and move rates sharply and suddenly.

**Ratana:** Countries handle their currencies very differently. Why?

**Dr. Sokunthea Pich:** They choose a *regime*. At one extreme, a floating rate, where the market sets the price and the government stays out. At the other, a fixed or pegged rate, where the central bank commits to a level and defends it by buying and selling reserves. Each has costs. Floating absorbs shocks but is volatile. Fixing brings stability but ties the government's hands — there's an iron constraint economists call the impossible trinity: you cannot have a fixed rate, free capital movement, and an independent monetary policy all at once. Pick two.

**Ratana:** Where does Cambodia sit?

**Dr. Sokunthea Pich:** Cambodia is an unusual case — heavily dollarized. A large share of the economy runs on US dollars alongside the riel, and the National Bank keeps the riel-dollar rate very stable. This gives us calm and predictability for dollar trade, which is valuable. But the cost is real: we effectively import US monetary conditions and have little independent monetary policy of our own. When the US raises interest rates, we feel it whether it suits our economy or not. It's the impossible trinity playing out in daily life.

**Ratana:** How does currency risk affect an ordinary Cambodian business?

**Dr. Sokunthea Pich:** Consider an exporter paid in euros or yen but paying workers in riel. Between signing a contract and being paid, the exchange rate can move against them — they deliver the goods, get paid in full, and still receive fewer riel than they counted on. That's currency risk, and it's quietly ruined more than one good business. The remedy is hedging — locking in a future rate today with a forward contract, so a swing can't ambush you. You trade away the chance of a windfall for the safety of a known number. For a business with families depending on it, that's usually wise.

**Ratana:** And the sustainability angle?

**Dr. Sokunthea Pich:** It's significant and often overlooked. Most clean-energy technology — solar panels, batteries, wind turbines — is priced in dollars or euros. If a developing country's currency weakens, that equipment suddenly costs far more in local money, and a green-investment project can stall purely on the exchange rate. International climate finance and green bonds frequently carry currency risk too, which the borrowing nation must hedge or absorb. So a stable, well-managed exchange-rate regime isn't just macroeconomic housekeeping — it directly determines how affordably a country can buy its way to a cleaner future.

**Ratana:** One sentence for our readers?

**Dr. Sokunthea Pich:** The exchange rate is the price at which your country meets the world — manage it well and you can afford the future; ignore it and the world's moods will decide what your work is worth.

**Ratana:** Thank you, Dr. Sokunthea.
