## The Exporter Who Counted in the Wrong Money / អ្នកនាំចេញដែលរាប់ខុសរូបិយប័ណ្ណ

Ratha had built something rare: a small workshop outside Phnom Penh that wove silk scarves fine enough that a boutique in Paris wanted them. The order, when it came, made her hands shake. Two thousand scarves. Payment of 80,000 euros, to arrive in four months when the shipment landed.

She sat that night with a pencil and did the arithmetic that would change her life. At the rate she saw on the money changer's board, 80,000 euros came to a sum in riel that would let her pay her sixteen weavers, buy a year of raw silk, and put a real roof on the workshop. She went to sleep counting that number.

This is the first thing the story teaches: when you do business across a currency border, you are always quietly counting in *two* monies at once — the one you'll be paid in, and the one you actually live and pay your costs in. Ratha would be paid in euros but lived in riel.

What Ratha did not yet understand was that the number on the money changer's board was not a promise. It was today's price of a euro, and a euro's price moves like the price of anything — up some weeks, down others, pushed by events in faraway places she had never heard of.

Over the four months, while Ratha wove, the euro slipped. A debt crisis on the far side of the world made investors nervous about Europe, and they sold euros, and a euro that had been worth a certain number of riel in the spring was worth noticeably fewer by the time her scarves reached Paris.

When the 80,000 euros finally arrived and she carried the payment to be converted, the riel she received was a good deal less than the number she had counted herself to sleep with. Enough less that the new roof became a patched one, and one weaver had to be let go for a season.

This is the second thing the story teaches: between the day you agree a foreign price and the day you are paid, the exchange rate can move against you — and that gap is a real loss, even though you sold every scarf and were paid in full. Economists call that exposure currency risk.

Ratha did not weep behind a truck the way some do. She went, instead, to a patient officer at a commercial bank and asked the question that mattered: *next time, how do I not lose the floor moving under my feet?*

The officer drew her a simple thing. "When you sign the next Paris order," he said, "come to me the same day. We will agree, right then, the rate at which your future euros will become riel — a rate locked in now, four months early. The euro can fall all it likes after that. It won't touch you. You'll get exactly the riel you counted on."

"And if the euro *rises* instead?" Ratha asked, because she was no fool.

"Then you don't get the windfall. You traded the chance of a lucky gain for the safety of a known number. For a workshop with sixteen families depending on it, that is almost always the right trade. You are a weaver, not a gambler on currencies."

Ratha took the next Paris order to him the very day it came, and locked her rate, and slept counting a number she could trust.

Years later her daughter studied global finance and came home talking of *forward contracts* and *hedging* and *FX risk*. Ratha listened, then said, "I learned all of that in one bad season. The euro is not the riel. The price between them moves while you sleep. So you fix it before you dream on it — or you let the world's moods decide what your scarves were worth."
