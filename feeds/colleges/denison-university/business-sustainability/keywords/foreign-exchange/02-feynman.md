# Foreign Exchange — Feynman Explanation
# ការប្តូរប្រាក់បរទេស — ការពន្យល់បែប Feynman

*Author: ichamrong | Date: 2026-06-01*

---

You go to a money changer near the market with a US dollar bill, and she hands you about 4,000 riel for it. Walk in tomorrow and maybe she gives you 4,050, or 3,980. That little number — how many riel one dollar is worth — is an **exchange rate**, and the whole business of swapping one country's money for another's is **foreign exchange** (ការប្តូរប្រាក់បរទេស).

---

Start with the simplest idea: money is just another thing that has a price.

A dollar has a price measured in riel. A riel has a price measured in dollars. And like the price of mangoes, that price is set by how many people want to buy versus sell.

Who wants riel? Anyone who needs to buy Cambodian things — a foreign company importing rice, or an investor building a factory here. Who wants dollars? Cambodians buying foreign fuel, or sending money to study abroad. When lots of people want dollars and few want riel, the dollar gets "expensive" — its price in riel rises. Same supply and demand you already know, just applied to money itself.

---

Now, who's in charge of this price? That depends on the country's *rules of the game* — what economists call the exchange-rate **regime**.

- **Floating:** the government steps back and lets the market set the price freely, like the mango market. The rate bounces around daily.
- **Fixed or pegged:** the government picks a number and *defends* it — promising to buy or sell its own currency to keep the rate glued in place.

Holding a fixed rate is expensive and hard. There's even an iron rule: a country *cannot* at the same time have a fixed rate, freely flowing money across its borders, *and* control of its own interest rates. Pick two; the third has to give. Economists call this the "impossible trinity."

---

Here's why any of this matters to a real business.

Imagine you run a Cambodian factory. A buyer in France promises to pay you 100,000 euros — in three months. Today that's worth a certain pile of riel. But in three months the euro might be worth less. You'd deliver the goods and get paid *fewer* riel than you counted on. That uncertainty — the rate moving against you before you get paid — is **currency risk**.

Smart businesses don't gamble on it. They *lock in* tomorrow's rate today, using a contract called a forward. It's like agreeing on the mango price now for a delivery next month, so a price swing can't hurt you. That's called hedging.

---

**The simple version:**

An exchange rate is just the price of one money in another, set by who wants to buy and sell it. Governments either let it float freely or fix it in place — but fixing it ties their hands. And because the rate is always moving, anyone doing business across borders faces the risk that it shifts against them before they're paid — so they lock in rates ahead of time instead of betting. Foreign exchange is simply the marketplace where all the world's monies get priced against each other.
