## Analogy 1: The Village Rice Storage System (ប្រព័ន្ធស្តុកស្រូវរបស់ភូមិ)

**The analogy:** Imagine a village where rice is the currency. There are three places rice can be stored: the family's rice barn (short-term), the monastery's long-term granary (centuries-old stocks), and ancient buried grain pits sealed underground (geological storage). The village economy stays balanced as long as rice only moves between the barn and the market — that is the natural cycle. The monastery occasionally adds a little to the market. But digging up and releasing the entire buried pit in one season overwhelms the system.

**Mapping:**
| Rice storage | Carbon equivalent |
|---|---|
| Family barn — seasonal flow | Terrestrial biosphere (trees, crops, soil) |
| Monastery granary — centuries-old | Peatlands, permafrost, old-growth forest soil |
| Buried ancient pit | Fossil fuels (coal, oil, gas) |
| Rice moving barn ↔ market | Natural carbon cycle (photosynthesis / respiration) |
| Digging up the buried pit | Fossil fuel combustion by industrialized economies |
| Market flooded with rice | Atmosphere flooded with CO₂ |

**Cambodian resonance:** Cambodia's Cardamom Mountains peatlands store ancient carbon accumulated over thousands of years — the equivalent of the monastery granary. Drainage for agriculture releases this carbon rapidly.

**Where the analogy holds:** The crucial insight — some carbon is "actively cycling" and some is "long-term stored." Burning fossil fuels is equivalent to raiding a storage system that took geological time to build.

**Where it breaks down:** Rice spoils; carbon does not vanish from the atmosphere on human timescales. CO₂ can persist in the atmosphere for centuries, so the "flood" doesn't drain away quickly.

## Analogy 2: The Mekong River as a Carbon Conveyor (ទន្លេមេគង្គជាក្រុមហ៊ុនដឹកកាបូន)

**The analogy:** Think of carbon like sediment being carried by the Mekong River. The river picks up sediment from the mountains (carbon from atmosphere into plants), carries it downstream (through food chains and decomposition), and either deposits it in the delta (soil carbon storage) or drops it into the sea (ocean carbon). Every year, roughly the same amount goes in as comes out — balance. Now imagine someone upstream dumps ten times the normal sediment load all at once. The river cannot process it; everything clogs.

**Mapping:**
| River process | Carbon cycle process |
|---|---|
| Upstream erosion — adds sediment | Fossil fuel combustion — adds CO₂ |
| Normal river transport | Natural carbon cycling |
| Sediment deposited in delta | Carbon stored in soil and ocean |
| Clogged river delta | CO₂ overload in atmosphere |
| Dam trapping sediment | Deforestation — stops carbon absorption |
| Sediment reaching sea normally | Ocean carbon absorption |

**Cambodian resonance:** The Mekong already carries less sediment than it used to because upstream dams trap it. Similarly, Cambodia's reduced forest cover means less carbon is being absorbed. Both the river and the carbon cycle are losing their natural throughput capacity.

**Where the analogy holds:** The flow metaphor captures that the cycle has a capacity — a maximum throughput. Overloading any part disrupts the whole system.

**Where it breaks down:** Sediment clogs are visible and localized. CO₂ accumulation is invisible, global, and evenly distributed in the atmosphere — no single downstream point feels the concentration most. This invisibility is why political action on carbon is harder than action on visible river pollution.

## Analogy 3: Breathing in a Sealed Room (ការដកដង្ហើមក្នុងបន្ទប់បិទជិត)

**The analogy:** If you sit alone in a well-ventilated room, you breathe in oxygen, breathe out CO₂, plants absorb the CO₂, produce oxygen — balance. If you pack 100 people into the same sealed room with no ventilation, CO₂ builds up quickly, oxygen depletes, and everyone feels sick. The room's atmosphere can only handle one person's breathing without disruption.

**Mapping:**
| Room element | Carbon cycle element |
|---|---|
| Sealed room | Earth's atmosphere (closed system on human timescale) |
| One person breathing | Pre-industrial natural carbon exchange |
| 100 people + sealed room | Industrial economy + accelerated fossil fuel burning |
| CO₂ building up | Atmospheric CO₂ rising from 280 ppm to 423 ppm |
| People feeling sick | Climate warming, ocean acidification |
| Opening a window (ventilation) | Reforestation, carbon capture |

**Cambodian resonance:** Phnom Penh's rapid motorization and construction industry growth — burning diesel and cement kilns — is adding to the CO₂ in the room, even if Cambodia's contribution is still small globally.

**Where the analogy holds:** The "closed system with limited capacity" insight is accurate. Earth's atmosphere cannot accept unlimited CO₂ without adverse effects.

**Where it breaks down:** In a sealed room, the cause (100 people) and the effect (sick people) are in the same room. In the carbon cycle, the biggest emitters (North America, Europe, China) are in a different part of the "room" from the worst sufferers (low-lying Bangladesh, Pacific islands, Cambodia's flood plains). This geographic separation is one reason international climate cooperation is so difficult.
