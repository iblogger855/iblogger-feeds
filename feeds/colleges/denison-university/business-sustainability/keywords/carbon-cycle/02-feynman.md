# Carbon Cycle — Feynman Explanation
# វដ្តកាបូន — ការពន្យល់បែប Feynman

*Author: ichamrong | Date: 2026-05-29*

---

You are made of carbon (កាបូន). So is the rice you ate this morning. So is the tree outside your school. So is the coal that powers the factory in Phnom Penh. Carbon is everywhere — and it never disappears. It just moves.

Think of carbon as the same water that gets passed around in a village water-sharing system. The same carbon atoms that are in your body today might have been in a dinosaur 200 million years ago, then in rock for 100 million years, then in oil, then burned in an engine, then floating in the air as CO₂, then absorbed by a rice plant, then eaten by your grandmother, then you.

Carbon travels in a big circle called the **carbon cycle** (វដ្តកាបូន).

Here is how it works in Cambodia:

**In the air:** Carbon floats as CO₂ (carbon dioxide — ឧស្ម័នកាបូនឌីអ៊ូស៊ីត). It is invisible and you breathe it out every time you exhale.

**Into plants:** When sunshine hits a rice leaf or a forest tree, the plant sucks CO₂ out of the air and uses it to build its own body — stems, roots, seeds. This is called photosynthesis (ដំណើរការ photosynthesis). The carbon goes from the air into the plant.

**Back to the air:** When the plant dies and rots, or when you burn wood, the carbon goes back into the air as CO₂ again. When your flooded rice paddy sits with no oxygen in the mud, special bacteria eat the dead plant matter and burp out methane (CH₄ — ម៉ីតាន) instead of CO₂. Methane is a much stronger heat-trapping gas.

**Into the deep earth:** Millions of years ago, dead plants and sea creatures got buried so deep that pressure cooked them into coal, oil, and gas. The carbon was locked underground. It was like putting money in a jar and burying it.

**The problem:** Humans started digging up all that buried carbon and burning it. Factories in Cambodia burn fossil fuel for electricity. Ships carry Cambodian garments to Europe, burning oil. The carbon that took millions of years to bury is being released in just 200 years — like flooding a village with 200 years of savings in one afternoon.

Now here is the key number: the air can only hold so much CO₂ before it starts trapping heat like a blanket around the Earth. We have already added so much carbon to the air that the planet is warming, rains are changing, and the Mekong River flows are becoming less predictable.

Cambodia is also part of this cycle in a hopeful way: the forests of the Cardamom Mountains (ភ្នំកាដាម៉ូម) and the mangroves in Koh Kong (កោះកុង) absorb and store enormous amounts of carbon. Every tree standing is carbon that is NOT in the air. That is why protecting Cambodia's forests is also fighting climate change.
