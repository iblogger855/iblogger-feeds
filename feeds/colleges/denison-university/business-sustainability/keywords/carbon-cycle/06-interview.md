# Carbon Cycle — Journalist Interview
# វដ្តកាបូន — បទសម្ភាសន៍អ្នកសារព័ត៌មាន

*Author: ichamrong | Date: 2026-05-29*
*Cambodian journalist Sreymom interviews Dr. Leap Sopheak, atmospheric scientist, Institute of Technology of Cambodia*

---

**Sreymom:** Dr. Sopheak, "carbon cycle" sounds like something from a high school textbook. Why is it front-page news in 2026?

**Dr. Sopheak:** Because humans have broken it. The carbon cycle (វដ្តកាបូន) is the planetary system that moves carbon between the air, plants, soils, oceans, and underground. For the past 800,000 years — we know this from ice cores — the atmospheric CO₂ concentration cycled between roughly 180 and 280 parts per million with the ice ages. That range produced stable, predictable climates. Today's concentration is 423 ppm. We are outside every natural range in the ice core record. That's why it's news.

**Sreymom:** Why should ordinary Cambodians care about parts per million of CO₂ in the air?

**Dr. Sopheak:** Because those numbers translate directly into the timing and intensity of the Mekong River floods that make Cambodian rice farming possible. CO₂ in the air traps heat; trapped heat changes wind patterns and monsoon intensity; changed monsoons change when and how much rain falls on the Mekong basin. The farmers in Kampong Speu or Battambang don't need to know the ppm number — they feel the result in failed harvests, unexpected floods, or drought months that arrive when the rice is still young. That is the carbon cycle, expressed in rice and fish.

**Sreymom:** Cambodia's industries are relatively small. Are Cambodians contributing to the carbon cycle problem?

**Dr. Sopheak:** Our contribution to global CO₂ is small — less than 0.1 percent of world emissions. But we are not carbon-neutral. The two main sources are land-use change and rice agriculture. When Cambodia's forests are cleared — and we lost roughly 2 million hectares of forest between 2001 and 2020 — the stored carbon in trees and soil is released as CO₂. Deforestation is our largest emission source. The second source is methane from flooded rice paddies. Flooded soil without oxygen produces methane, which is 28 times more potent than CO₂ as a greenhouse gas. With about 3 million hectares of rice paddies, this adds up significantly in our national greenhouse gas inventory.

**Sreymom:** Garment factories are Cambodia's main export industry. What is their carbon footprint?

**Dr. Sopheak:** It comes primarily from the electricity they use, most of which is still generated from coal, oil, and hydropower. Cambodia's grid is cleaner than many countries' because of hydropower, but the coal plants in Sihanoukville and elsewhere are significant CO₂ sources. As European buyers increasingly demand carbon footprint transparency — and the EU Carbon Border Adjustment Mechanism (CBAM) starts applying to some imports — Cambodia's garment sector will face pressure to track and reduce its carbon intensity. That is a business competitiveness issue, not just an environmental one.

**Sreymom:** You mentioned REDD+. Can Cambodia get paid for absorbing carbon?

**Dr. Sopheak:** Yes, and we already do, to some extent. REDD+ — Reducing Emissions from Deforestation and Forest Degradation (ការកាត់បន្ថយការបំភាយឧស្ម័នពីការកាប់ព្រៃ) — is an international mechanism that pays developing countries to keep their forests standing. Cambodia has registered carbon credits from areas like the Cardamom Mountains and the Southern Cardamom REDD+ project. Forest communities in Koh Kong and Mondulkiri receive payments that help fund forest rangers, conservation activities, and community development. This is essentially Cambodia monetizing its role as a carbon sink in the global cycle.

**Sreymom:** How does the Tonle Sap fit into the carbon cycle?

**Dr. Sopheak:** The Tonle Sap is a fascinating carbon actor. The flooded forest is a methane source during flood season — similar to rice paddies, the anaerobic conditions produce methane. But the same flooded forest is also a massive carbon store — the trees, the soil, the accumulated organic matter. The net balance depends on the extent and duration of flooding. As the Tonle Sap's flood pulse weakens — partly from climate change, partly from upstream dams — the carbon dynamics of the whole system shift. This is an active research area; we don't yet fully understand whether a shrunken Tonle Sap would be more or less of a net carbon source over time.

**Sreymom:** What about mangroves? Cambodia has been losing mangroves in Koh Kong.

**Dr. Sopheak:** Mangroves are among the most carbon-dense ecosystems on Earth — what scientists call "blue carbon" (កាបូនខៀវ). Per hectare, mangrove soils store three to five times more carbon than tropical forest soils, because the organic matter accumulates in waterlogged conditions where it decomposes very slowly over thousands of years. When you clear a mangrove for shrimp ponds, that ancient carbon store is exposed to oxygen and releases CO₂ rapidly. Cambodia has lost substantial mangrove cover over the past three decades. Restoring it is not just a fisheries issue — it is a carbon issue, and there is growing interest in blue carbon credits for mangrove restoration in Koh Kong.

**Sreymom:** What single action could Cambodia take to contribute most positively to the carbon cycle?

**Dr. Sopheak:** Halt deforestation and scale up forest restoration. The Cardamom Mountains and the Northern Plains forests are among the most important remaining tropical forest carbon stores in mainland Southeast Asia. Every year we slow or stop deforestation is a year of avoided emissions worth hundreds of thousands of tonnes. Combined with better rice paddy management — alternate wetting and drying to cut methane — and a push for renewable energy in the grid, Cambodia could potentially achieve carbon neutrality before 2050, ahead of most countries. That would be a remarkable achievement and a legitimate source of national pride.

**Sreymom:** Thank you, Doctor.

**Dr. Sopheak:** Thank you. Tell your readers: the carbon in the air above Phnom Penh was once in a tree, was once in the ocean, was once in ancient rock. It travels. Where it travels next depends on what we do now.
