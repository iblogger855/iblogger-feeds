## The Monk Who Measured Breath | ព្រះដែលវាស់숨ដង្ហើម

In a monastery on the edge of the Cardamom Mountains (ភ្នំកាដាម៉ូម), there lived an old monk named Vanna who had spent forty years watching things disappear and return.

Every morning at dawn, he watched the mist rise from the forest floor and dissolve into the sky. Every evening, he watched the dew form on the leaves from the same invisible air. He burned incense and watched the smoke climb and vanish. He composted fallen leaves in a pit and watched them become soil, which fed new plants, which made new leaves, which fell and composted again.

He told the young novices: *"Nothing is lost. It only travels."*

The novices were polite but skeptical. One of them — a sharp-eyed boy named Kosal, who had come from a fishing village on the Tonle Sap — asked the monk to prove it.

Vanna pointed to the forest. "What is a tree made of?"

"Wood," Kosal said.

"And what is wood made of, at the deepest level?"

Kosal had studied chemistry at school before taking his robes. He thought hard. "Carbon, mostly. Glucose chains. The tree built them from carbon dioxide in the air, using sunlight."

"So the tree is made of air?"

Kosal smiled slowly. "Yes. Essentially. It is crystallized air."

"And when we burn the wood from the old fallen tree at the edge of the kitchen garden?"

"The carbon returns to the air as CO₂."

Vanna nodded. "The tree breathes in. The fire breathes out. Nothing is lost. The cycle turns."

For many years, Kosal thought of this teaching as simply beautiful. Then he left the monastery, studied environmental science in Phnom Penh, and came to understand it as urgent.

He learned that the cycle Vanna described had been running for hundreds of millions of years. Plants absorbing CO₂. Animals eating plants, breathing CO₂ back out. Dead matter rotting, releasing CO₂. The atmosphere held a steady concentration that kept the planet at just the right temperature — warm enough for water to be liquid, not so warm that it boiled away.

He learned that there was a slow part of the cycle too — the geological part. Some ancient organisms, dying in vast quantities at the bottom of ancient seas, had been buried under so much pressure and heat that they transformed into coal, oil, and natural gas. That carbon was removed from the cycle. Locked away. The planet cooled slightly from losing it.

And then, in just two centuries, humans had started burning that ancient carbon. All of it, at once, in geological terms. Kosal calculated it: what took 300 million years to bury, humans were releasing in about 300 years.

The monastery's forest was still doing its part. Old Vanna's trees were absorbing CO₂. The soil held centuries of accumulated carbon. The REDD+ program was paying the monastery's community forest fund for that stored carbon — small payments, but enough to employ three rangers and buy tools to remove illegal snares.

But Kosal had also measured the monastery's rice paddy downstream. The flooded paddy — always slightly waterlogged, even in dry season — was a methane source. The bacteria in the anaerobic mud were doing their ancient work, breaking down organic matter without oxygen, releasing a gas that trapped heat 28 times more powerfully than CO₂. He calculated that the paddy produced about 12 tonnes of CO₂-equivalent per hectare per year.

He brought this to Vanna in the old monk's last year of life. "The paddy is breathing out in the wrong direction," Kosal said.

Vanna was quiet for a long time. Then: "Is there a way to change the breath without killing the plant?"

Kosal told him about alternate wetting and drying — letting the paddy dry partially between waterings, allowing oxygen into the mud, reducing methane. The yield loss was small. The methane reduction could be 40 percent.

"Then that is the practice," Vanna said simply.

Kosal implemented it the following season. He enrolled the monastery's paddy in a carbon reduction program. The payment was modest. But when the project coordinator asked him why he had joined, Kosal said the same thing his teacher had told him forty years earlier:

"Nothing is lost. It only travels. We are responsible for where it travels to."

---

*This story illustrates the **carbon cycle** (វដ្តកាបូន) — the continuous movement of carbon between the atmosphere, living organisms, soil, ocean, and geological reservoirs, disrupted by the rapid release of fossil carbon.*
