# Consumer Surplus — Analogy Bridge
# អតិរេកអ្នកប្រើប្រាស់ — ស្ពានប្រៀបធៀប

*Author: ichamrong | Date: 2026-06-01*

---

## Analogy 1: Walking Into a Bargain You Were Ready to Overpay For / ការដើរចូលរកការចចារ

**The Story:**
You go to Phsar Olympic for a phone charger, having decided in advance you'd pay up to 20,000 riel. The first stall says 12,000. You buy instantly, feeling clever. That feeling — the 8,000 you were willing to part with but didn't have to — is consumer surplus made visible. You didn't receive 8,000 in cash, but you walk out richer in satisfaction by exactly that much.

**The Mapping:**

| Analogy Element | Consumer Surplus Element | ខ្មែរ |
|---|---|---|
| The 20,000 you'd have paid | Willingness to pay | ឆន្ទៈបង់ប្រាក់ |
| The 12,000 sticker price | Market price | តម្លៃទីផ្សារ |
| The 8,000 "good deal" feeling | Consumer surplus | អតិរេកអ្នកប្រើ |
| Price jumping to 21,000 | Price above WTP → no sale | តម្លៃលើសឆន្ទៈ → គ្មានទិញ |

**Where the Analogy Breaks Down:**
One shopper feels one bargain. Market consumer surplus is the *sum* of thousands of such private bargains, each different — a quantity no single shopper ever experiences.

## Analogy 2: The Concert Where Everyone Paid the Same Ticket / ការប្រគុំតន្ត្រីសំបុត្រតែមួយ

**The Story:**
A famous singer plays Koh Pich. Tickets are 30,000 riel, flat. A superfan would have paid 200,000 and is overjoyed. A curious neighbor would have paid 35,000 and is mildly pleased. Someone who'd pay only 25,000 stays home. Same ticket price, wildly different hidden joy. Stack everyone's private "extra worth" on top of the ticket price and you have the audience's total consumer surplus.

**The Mapping:**

| Analogy Element | Consumer Surplus Element | ខ្មែរ |
|---|---|---|
| Superfan's huge private value | High WTP, large surplus | ឆន្ទៈខ្ពស់ អតិរេកធំ |
| Neighbor's small private value | Low WTP, thin surplus | ឆន្ទៈទាប អតិរេកស្ដើង |
| Person who stays home | Below price → no trade | ក្រោមតម្លៃ → គ្មានទិញ |
| Raising ticket to 40,000 | Marginal buyers lost | អ្នកទិញរឹមបាត់ |

**Where the Analogy Breaks Down:**
A concert has fixed seats. In most markets, lowering the price both deepens existing surplus *and* pulls in entirely new buyers — the audience can grow.

## Analogy 3: The Restaurant Buffet vs. the À La Carte / ប៊ូហ្វេ ទល់នឹង ការកម្ម៉ង់ម្ដងមួយ

**The Story:**
At an all-you-can-eat buffet for 25,000 riel, the very hungry eater feels he got 60,000 worth of food — huge surplus. The light eater feels he barely got his money's worth — tiny surplus. The same flat price hands a fat bargain to one and a thin one to the other, exactly as a single market price does. Consumer surplus is simply the meal's worth-to-you minus the price on the door.

**The Mapping:**

| Analogy Element | Consumer Surplus Element | ខ្មែរ |
|---|---|---|
| Flat buffet price | Single market price | តម្លៃទីផ្សារតែមួយ |
| Hungry eater's value | High willingness to pay | ឆន្ទៈបង់ប្រាក់ខ្ពស់ |
| Worth-to-you minus price | Surplus per buyer | អតិរេកក្នុងម្នាក់ |
| Closing the buffet | Trade prevented → lost surplus | រារាំងទិញ → អតិរេកបាត់ |

**Where the Analogy Breaks Down:**
A buffet's value depends on appetite that hour. Market valuations are more stable and can be ranked into a smooth demand curve.

---

## Core Insight Across All Three Analogies / ការយល់ដឹងស្នូល

All three reveal the same truth: **consumer surplus is the invisible bargain — value you capture above the price you pay.** It is biggest for those who value the good most, it shrinks as price rises, and it dies entirely when a price climbs past what a buyer was ever willing to give. Measuring it is how economists judge whether a tax, subsidy, or policy left buyers genuinely better or worse off — including in sustainable-resource debates.
