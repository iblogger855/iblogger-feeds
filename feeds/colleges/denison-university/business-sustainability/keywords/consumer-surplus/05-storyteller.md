## The Water Seller of the Dry Season / អ្នកលក់ទឹកនៅរដូវប្រាំង

Vuthy pushed a cart of cold bottled water through Phnom Penh in April, the cruelest month, when the city air sits on your chest like a warm hand. He had learned something in three years of pushing that cart, something no one had taught him and that the economics students who bought from him did not seem to know they were demonstrating.

He sold water at 1,000 riel a bottle. But he watched faces. He could tell, almost to the riel, what each person *would* have paid.

The construction worker who came down from the scaffolding at noon, shirt dark with sweat — that man would have paid 3,000, maybe 4,000, and never blinked. When Vuthy said "one thousand," the man's whole face loosened with relief. He bought two. He walked away feeling he had robbed Vuthy, and he had not; he had simply kept, invisibly, the two or three thousand riel of worth that the low price let him keep.

This is the first thing the story teaches: the price a person *pays* and the price a thing is *worth to them* are two different numbers, and the gap between them is a real, felt gain — even though no extra money ever changes hands.

The office girl in the pressed blouse, walking unhurried in the shade, was different. She would have paid perhaps 1,200. When Vuthy said "one thousand" she bought, but without relief — a small, ordinary transaction. Her hidden bargain was thin, just two hundred riel of worth kept back. Same bottle. Same price. A fraction of the construction worker's quiet windfall.

This is the second thing the story teaches: one price, but a thousand different bargains, each sized by how much the buyer needed the thing. The desperate keep the most; the indifferent keep the least.

Then came the festival week, and the bottled-water wholesaler raised Vuthy's cost, so Vuthy raised his price to 2,500. The construction worker still bought — he valued water far above 2,500 — but his face no longer loosened. His bargain had shrunk from three thousand to a few hundred. And the office girl? She walked right past. At 2,500 the water was not worth it to her. She bought nothing, and Vuthy earned nothing from her, and the small pleasant trade that used to happen between them simply... did not.

Vuthy noticed the empty space where her sale had been. Nobody had gained from its absence. He was not richer; she was not cooler. The little exchange had evaporated, and its modest good went nowhere at all.

Years later Vuthy's daughter, studying at university, drew him a triangle on a napkin — the area under a sloping line, above a flat price — and told him it was called consumer surplus, and the missing sliver was called deadweight loss.

Vuthy looked at the triangle a long time. "So this," he said, tapping the wide part, "is the construction worker's face when I said one thousand."

"Yes," she said.

"And this little piece that goes away when I raise the price — this is the girl who walked past."

"Yes, Pa. Most people learn it from a book. You learned it from the cart."
