# Consumer Surplus — Feynman Explanation
# អតិរេកអ្នកប្រើប្រាស់ — ការពន្យល់បែប Feynman

*Author: ichamrong | Date: 2026-06-01*

---

You walk into the market on a hot afternoon, thirsty, and you see iced sugarcane juice. In your head, before you look at the price, you think: "I would honestly pay 3,000 riel for that right now." Then you see the sign: 1,500 riel.

You buy it. And here is the quiet, lovely thing — you walk away feeling like you *won*. You were ready to give 3,000 and only gave 1,500. That extra 1,500 in value you got to keep is **consumer surplus** (អតិរេកអ្នកប្រើប្រាស់).

---

That is the whole idea. Consumer surplus is the gap between what something is *worth to you* and what you actually *pay*. It is the bargain in your pocket that nobody handed you in cash but that you feel as a little glow of "that was a good deal."

Let me say it plainly: **value to you − price you paid = your surplus.**

---

Now the clever part. The vendor charges everyone the same 1,500. But not everyone is as thirsty as you.

- You, parched, would have paid 3,000 → your surplus is 1,500.
- A man strolling by, not very thirsty, would have paid only 1,800 → his surplus is just 300.
- Someone who values it at exactly 1,500 buys it but keeps nothing extra.
- Someone who only values it at 1,000? He walks past. No deal, no surplus.

Same price for all. Different hidden bargains for each. If you add up everyone's little bargains, you get the **total consumer surplus** of the whole market.

---

Here is why it matters. Suppose the vendor raises the price to 2,500.

You still buy (you valued it at 3,000), but now your bargain shrank from 1,500 to just 500. And the not-very-thirsty man? He valued it at 1,800, so at 2,500 he walks away. He buys nothing. His little bargain vanishes completely — and the vendor doesn't get his money either. *That* lost bargain, the deal that simply never happens, is what economists call **deadweight loss** (បាត់បង់ស្ងួត). It is value that disappears for everyone.

---

**The simple version:**

Consumer surplus is the "I'd-have-paid-more" feeling, measured in money. Lower the price and the feeling grows — old buyers win more and shy buyers join in. Raise it and the feeling shrinks, and some good deals die unmade. When you hear an economist worry that a tax or a monopoly "destroys surplus," they just mean: bargains that everyone would have enjoyed are quietly not happening anymore.
