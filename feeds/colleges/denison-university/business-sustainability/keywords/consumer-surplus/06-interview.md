# Consumer Surplus — Journalist Interview
# អតិរេកអ្នកប្រើប្រាស់ — បទសម្ភាសន៍សារព័ត៌មាន

*Author: ichamrong | Date: 2026-06-01*

---

*Interview between Ratana Chan of the Phnom Penh Economic Tribune and Dr. Sokunthea Pich, welfare economist at the Royal University of Agriculture.*

---

**Ratana Chan:** Dr. Sokunthea, "consumer surplus" sounds like jargon. Why should an ordinary reader care about it?

**Dr. Sokunthea Pich:** Because it is the closest thing economics has to a measure of how much a market *benefits the people who buy from it*. Every time you pay less for something than it was worth to you, you pocket consumer surplus. It is the everyday experience of a good deal, turned into a number we can add up and compare. Without it, we can argue about prices forever but never say cleanly whether buyers, as a group, came out ahead.

**Ratana:** How do you actually measure something so personal?

**Dr. Sokunthea Pich:** We use the demand curve. Imagine lining up every buyer from the one who'd pay the most to the one who'd pay the least. The market charges them all one price. Everyone above that price keeps a little — or a lot — of extra value. Add all those slivers together and you get a triangle on the graph: the area below the demand curve and above the price. That triangle is consumer surplus. It is an estimate, not a perfect reading of anyone's mind, but it is a remarkably useful one.

**Ratana:** Give me a Cambodian example where this matters for policy.

**Dr. Sokunthea Pich:** Electricity tariffs. When the government keeps household power cheap, consumers enjoy enormous surplus — bills feel light, people run fans and fridges freely. That visible benefit is exactly the surplus we're describing. But if the cheap tariff comes from subsidizing diesel generation, the price hides the pollution and the fiscal cost. So a policymaker must weigh the consumer surplus citizens enjoy against the hidden harm. You cannot have that debate honestly until you can put a number on the surplus.

**Ratana:** You mentioned hidden harm. Is that where deadweight loss comes in?

**Dr. Sokunthea Pich:** Deadweight loss is the flip side. It is the surplus that *disappears* when a market is squeezed — by a monopoly charging too much, by a tax, by a price control. Some trades that would have made both buyer and seller happier simply never happen. Nobody captures that lost value; it evaporates. When we say a policy is "inefficient," we usually mean it is generating deadweight loss — burning bargains that everyone would have enjoyed.

**Ratana:** So is reducing consumer surplus always a bad thing?

**Dr. Sokunthea Pich:** No, and this is the heart of sustainable economics. A carbon tax deliberately reduces the consumer surplus on fossil fuels — petrol and coal power become dearer, and buyers feel the pinch. That looks like a pure loss if you only stare at the surplus triangle. But the fuel's old price never included the climate and health damage it caused. The surplus consumers were enjoying was, in part, borrowed from everyone downwind. A good carbon tax trades a slice of private surplus for a larger gain in public well-being.

**Ratana:** How should a young entrepreneur think about consumer surplus?

**Dr. Sokunthea Pich:** Two ways. First, every happy customer is one who got more value than they paid for — surplus is the foundation of loyalty and word of mouth. Second, understand that when you raise prices, you don't just earn more per sale; you also quietly kill the trades of customers near the margin. Pricing is the art of capturing value without destroying too many of those bargains. The best businesses leave their customers feeling they got the better end of the deal.

**Ratana:** One sentence for a reader to remember?

**Dr. Sokunthea Pich:** Consumer surplus is the worth you keep above the price you pay — and a healthy, sustainable economy is one that grows that surplus honestly, without hiding its costs in the air, the rivers, or the future.

**Ratana:** Thank you, Dr. Sokunthea.
