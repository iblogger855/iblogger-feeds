# Political Risk: A Cambodian Journalist Interviews a Risk Expert
# ហានិភ័យនយោបាយ៖ អ្នកសារព័ត៌មានខ្មែរសំភាសន៍អ្នកជំនាញ

*Journalist: Sreymom Pich, economic reporter, The Cambodia Daily relaunch*
*Expert: Dr. Andreas Weil, Political Risk Analyst, formerly Control Risks Group, Southeast Asia desk*

---

**Sreymom:** Dr. Weil, Cambodia has attracted billions in foreign investment over the past two decades. But critics say investors often underestimate the political risks here. What are they missing?

**Dr. Weil:** What they miss is the gap between formal institutions and real power. Cambodia has a land registry, a commercial court, a central bank — all the formal architecture of a normal investment environment. But in practice, decisions about who gets what are made through networks of personal relationship and political loyalty. Investors who read the law and think they understand the rules are only reading half the map.

---

**Sreymom:** Can you give a concrete example from Cambodia?

**Dr. Weil:** The Phnom Penh lakeside community displacement is a textbook case. Boeng Kak Lake — thousands of families had lived there for decades. Some had formal titles, some had informal claims. A politically connected developer, backed by ruling-party relationships, was awarded a 99-year lease. The lake was filled. Families were evicted. The courts sided with the developer. This is what political risk looks like from the ground up — not a coup, not a war, but the quiet use of state instruments in favor of politically connected actors.

---

**Sreymom:** What about large foreign investors — Chinese companies investing through BRI? Do they face different political risks than, say, a European manufacturer?

**Dr. Weil:** Completely different risk profiles. A Chinese company investing in a port or a special economic zone in Cambodia faces relatively low *host-country* political risk — because Beijing-Phnom Penh relations have been very stable, and Cambodian leadership has strong incentives to protect that investment. But the Chinese company faces *elevated third-country* political risk: US and EU sanctions, supply chain transparency laws, investor pressure in home markets. They may be safe from Cambodia's government but exposed to Washington's.

---

**Sreymom:** The US suspended Cambodia's preferential trade access under EBA — the Everything But Arms program — the EU did something similar. Is that political risk?

**Dr. Weil:** Absolutely. And it's a fascinating illustration of how political risk can come from your customer's government, not just your host government. Cambodia's garment sector — which employs hundreds of thousands of workers, mostly women — was effectively punished for the political behavior of the Cambodian state: the 2017 opposition crackdown, the 2018 election. Factories did nothing wrong. But their risk materialized through a political decision made in Brussels.

---

**Sreymom:** How should Cambodian businesses — not just foreign investors — think about political risk domestically?

**Dr. Weil:** Cambodian entrepreneurs face a specific form of political risk I call "proximity risk" — the danger of being successful enough to attract the attention of more powerful political-economic actors. Your business is too small to matter until it isn't. Once you reach a certain scale, you become interesting to powerful people who may want a share. Managing this requires deliberate choices: how visible to be, when to partner with politically connected figures, and how to structure ownership so that you're not easily displaced.

---

**Sreymom:** The Hun Sen era is transitioning to Hun Manet's leadership. Does generational leadership change alter the political risk landscape?

**Dr. Weil:** It can, in two directions. On one hand, a transition within the same political family suggests continuity — the same networks, the same basic bargain. On the other hand, a new leader has to establish his own authority, build his own coalition, potentially sideline old power centers. That transition period — even within a stable authoritarian system — is actually a period of elevated political risk, because the rules of the game are being renegotiated.

---

**Sreymom:** What tools do you recommend for a company assessing political risk in Cambodia?

**Dr. Weil:** Four things. First, go beyond the indices — the ICRG scores and World Bank governance indicators are useful baselines but they're backward-looking. Second, do primary research: talk to local businesses, NGOs, journalists, lawyers — the people who live inside the system every day. Third, map the political-business network around your specific sector, not just the country generally. Fourth, scenario-plan: what does your investment look like if the US imposes sectoral sanctions? If ASEAN solidarity fractures? If Chinese investment cools? Test your resilience before the event.

---

**Sreymom:** Final question. Is Cambodia a high political risk country, overall?

**Dr. Weil:** It's a *specific* political risk country. The risk of a sudden war or coup is actually quite low. The risk of a regulatory reversal, a selective enforcement action, or an informal expropriation targeting a firm that competes with a connected actor — that risk is elevated. If you understand that specific risk profile and design your investment accordingly, Cambodia can be very profitable. If you assume it operates like Singapore because it has similar legal architecture on paper — you will be surprised.
