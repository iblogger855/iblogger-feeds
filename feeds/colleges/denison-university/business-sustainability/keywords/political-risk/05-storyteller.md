# The Man Who Built the Best Hotel in Kep
# បុរសដែលសង់សណ្ឋាគារដ៏ល្អបំផុតនៅកែប

*A story from the Gulf of Thailand coast*

---

Sovann had been saving for eleven years.

He worked three jobs after his father's fishing boat was damaged in a storm — cashier at a convenience store, motorbike taxi driver on weekends, and nighttime security guard at a garment factory in Phnom Penh. Every riel he didn't spend, he put in a small notebook under his mattress, then eventually in a savings account at Acleda Bank.

By 2019, he had enough.

He took the bus down to Kep — that quiet, ghostly town on the Gulf of Thailand, where crumbling French colonial villas stood like old teeth among the casuarina trees. He had grown up nearby, in a small village outside Kampot. He knew the coastline. He knew that tourists were beginning to come.

He bought a plot of land near the crab market, right at the edge of the beach. He got the title registered, paid a local architect, and hired a team of builders from his home village. Two stories, eight rooms, a rooftop terrace facing the sea, a restaurant downstairs serving the fresh crab the town was famous for. It took fourteen months.

He called it *Chha Trey* — Saltwater.

The first two years were better than he had dreamed. European backpackers. Chinese tour groups. A travel blogger came and wrote about the rooftop sunset. He hired four staff. He sent money home to his mother. He started saving again, now for a second floor extension.

Then, in the spring of 2022, a man came to see him.

The man wore a clean white shirt and drove a black Lexus with no license plate. He sat down at the restaurant table, looked at the sea, and ordered nothing.

"This land," the man said pleasantly, "is of interest to us."

Us, Sovann discovered, meant a development company with connections that ran from a provincial governor's office to a senator's family to an address in Sihanoukville that was registered to an entity none of the public databases could resolve. They wanted the whole beachfront. A resort. International investors. The kind of project that appeared in government press releases about "economic development and foreign partnership."

Sovann showed them his title. He had done everything correctly.

The man smiled. "Titles can be reviewed," he said. "Especially when the original survey data is found to contain errors."

Over the following eight months, Sovann received a tax audit. Then a health inspection that found "violations" in his kitchen. Then a letter from the land authority informing him that his survey map was "under review." None of his neighbors received these notices. Only him.

He hired a lawyer. The lawyer was good. But the lawyer also explained something to him that no business school textbook had prepared Sovann for: in systems where power and law overlap imperfectly, the stronger argument is often not the legal one.

He sold. For less than half of what the land and the building were worth.

On the bus back to Phnom Penh, staring at the Gulf of Thailand growing smaller in the window, Sovann understood something that took most people a lifetime to learn.

He had not failed because of the market. He had not failed because of bad management, or bad luck, or bad timing. He had failed because he had built something valuable in a place where the rules about who gets to keep valuable things had not yet been settled — where political relationships determined property rights more reliably than land titles did.

He had not assessed the political risk.

---

*What Sovann encountered had a name: micro-level political risk — regulatory reversal, selective enforcement, and the use of state instruments to benefit politically connected actors at the expense of ordinary investors. Unlike macro-level political risk (wars, coups), this variety is invisible until it arrives at your door in a white shirt and a black Lexus.*
