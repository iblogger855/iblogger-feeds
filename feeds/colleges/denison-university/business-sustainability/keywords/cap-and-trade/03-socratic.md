# Cap and Trade — Socratic Dialogue
# ប្រព័ន្ធកំណត់កម្រិត និងការជួញដូរ — ការសន្ទនាបែប Socratic

*Author: ichamrong | Date: 2026-06-01*

---

**Professor:** Lina, suppose we want to limit a country's carbon emissions to exactly ten million tonnes a year. How might we be sure we hit that number?

**Lina:** We could set a hard limit and issue exactly ten million permits, one per tonne. No permit, no emitting.

**Professor:** Good. If a firm has more permits than it needs, what might it do with the spare ones?

**Lina:** Sell them to a firm that needs more.

**Professor:** Why would the second firm buy rather than just cut its own emissions?

**Lina:** Because for that firm, cutting might be very expensive. Buying a permit could be cheaper than cutting.

**Professor:** And the firm selling — why does it have spares?

**Lina:** Because cutting was cheap for it. It cut more than required and freed up permits to sell.

**Professor:** So who ends up doing the actual emission cuts?

**Lina:** The firms that can cut cheaply. They cut and sell; the expensive ones buy and keep emitting.

**Professor:** Notice what just happened. We hit the total of ten million exactly, but the cuts landed where they were cheapest. Did the regulator decide which firm cuts?

**Lina:** No. The trading sorted it out. The regulator only set the total.

**Professor:** Now, where does the price of a permit come from?

**Lina:** From buyers and sellers, like any market price. Lots of demand for permits, high price; little demand, low price.

**Professor:** So if cutting carbon turns out to be cheap everywhere, what happens to the permit price?

**Lina:** It falls — nobody needs to buy many permits, so they are not worth much.

**Professor:** And if the price falls near zero, is that a failure?

**Lina:** Not really. It means the cap was easy to meet. Though if it's too easy, maybe the cap was set too loose.

**Professor:** Sharp. Now compare this to a carbon tax. The tax sets the price per tonne. What does cap-and-trade set instead?

**Lina:** The quantity — the total emissions. The price is whatever the market produces.

**Professor:** So which tool gives you certainty about the environmental outcome?

**Lina:** Cap-and-trade. You know the emissions because you set the cap. With a tax you know the price but not the final quantity.

**Professor:** Last question. A factory buys permits instead of cutting and keeps polluting its neighbourhood. The national cap is met, but this one community still breathes the smoke. What does that reveal?

**Lina:** That cap-and-trade controls the *total* but not *where* the pollution lands. Cuts cluster where it's cheap, so some local areas — often poorer ones — can stay polluted. That's an environmental-justice gap.

**Professor:** Exactly. Efficiency in total can still be unjust in place. Hold onto that — it's where market design meets fairness.
