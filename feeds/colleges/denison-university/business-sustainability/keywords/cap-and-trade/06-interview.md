# Cap and Trade — Journalist Interview
# ប្រព័ន្ធកំណត់កម្រិត និងការជួញដូរ — បទសម្ភាសន៍សារព័ត៌មាន

*Author: ichamrong | Date: 2026-06-01*

---

*Interview between Ratana Chan of the Phnom Penh Economic Tribune and Dr. Sokunthea Pich, environmental economist at the Royal University of Agriculture.*

---

**Ratana Chan:** Dr. Sokunthea, cap-and-trade sounds complicated. In plain terms, what is it?

**Dr. Sokunthea Pich:** It's two ideas bolted together. First, the cap: a government decides the maximum carbon that may be emitted in a year and issues exactly that many permits, one per tonne. To emit, you need a permit. Second, the trade: those permits can be bought and sold. That's it. The cap guarantees the environmental outcome; the trade guarantees it's achieved cheaply.

**Ratana:** Why not just tell each company how much to cut?

**Dr. Sokunthea Pich:** Because companies differ enormously in how cheaply they can cut, and the regulator can't know each one's costs. If you order everyone to cut the same percentage, you force some firms into ruinously expensive cuts while a neighbour could have cut twice as much for half the price. Trading fixes that. The cheap cutters cut deeply and sell their spare permits; the expensive cutters buy permits instead of cutting. The total is met, but the cuts happen wherever they're cheapest. The market finds the least-cost path that no planner could calculate.

**Ratana:** The European Union runs the biggest such system, the EU ETS. What did it teach us?

**Dr. Sokunthea Pich:** Two big lessons. Early on, the EU handed out too many free permits, the price crashed, and the cap was too loose to bite — a warning that the cap must be tight and that auctioning permits beats giving them away. They fixed it later with a tighter, declining cap and mechanisms to support the price. The second lesson is that a well-designed cap-and-trade system genuinely works: EU power-sector emissions have fallen substantially, and the permit price now sends a real signal across the economy.

**Ratana:** How does this differ from a carbon tax?

**Dr. Sokunthea Pich:** They're mirror images. A carbon tax sets the *price* and lets emissions land wherever they land. Cap-and-trade sets the *quantity* — the cap — and lets the price float. If your priority is hitting a precise emissions target, you cap. If your priority is giving businesses a predictable cost to plan around, you tax. Cap-and-trade gives environmental certainty but a volatile price; a tax gives a stable price but uncertain emissions. Many real systems now add a price floor and ceiling to a cap, borrowing the best of both.

**Ratana:** Is there a fairness concern, as with the carbon tax?

**Dr. Sokunthea Pich:** Yes, and a particular one. Because the market cuts emissions where it's cheapest, the cuts can cluster in some places and skip others. A factory in a poor neighbourhood may simply buy permits and keep polluting locally, even while the national total falls. The accounting succeeds; the local air doesn't improve. That's an environmental-justice problem, and good schemes monitor where emissions actually land, not just the total.

**Ratana:** How is this relevant to Cambodia, which doesn't run its own cap-and-trade system?

**Dr. Sokunthea Pich:** Through carbon credits. Cambodian forest-protection and clean-cookstove projects already sell verified credits into international markets — that's permit trading in spirit, channelling foreign money into keeping our forests standing. The danger is credit quality: if a project claims to protect a forest that wasn't truly at risk, the credit is hollow and the buyer's emissions aren't really offset. Cambodia's opportunity is to host credits that are rigorously real, so the world pays us properly for genuine conservation.

**Ratana:** Final question. One thing for a young policymaker to remember about cap-and-trade?

**Dr. Sokunthea Pich:** Set the cap honestly and let the market do the sorting. The cap is the promise to the planet; the trade is the gift to the economy. Get the cap right — tight, declining, and well-monitored — and the rest is just arithmetic.

**Ratana:** Thank you, Dr. Sokunthea.
