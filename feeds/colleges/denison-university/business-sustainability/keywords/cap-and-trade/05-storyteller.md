## The Two Mills on the Same River / រោងម៉ាស៊ីនពីរនៅលើស្ទឹងតែមួយ

There were two rice mills on the same stretch of the Stung Sen, and for years they competed at everything — who paid farmers a riel more, who dried paddy faster, who ran later into the night. Their owners, Veasna upstream and Phally downstream, barely spoke.

Both mills ran on old diesel boilers that coughed smoke over the town. When the province joined a pilot scheme, the rule was new and strange: each mill received a fixed bundle of permits, and together the two mills — plus a dozen others in the district — were allowed only so much carbon in total. That total was the cap. It would shrink a little each year. And, oddly, the permits could be bought and sold.

Veasna, the upstream miller, was the tinkerer. He had always wanted to replace his diesel boiler with one fired by rice husk — the very waste his mill produced in heaps. The permit scheme changed his arithmetic. With husk firing he would emit far less than his permits allowed, leaving a stack of permits unused. And those permits were now worth money.

So Veasna rebuilt. His emissions dropped by more than half. He held a pile of spare permits.

Phally, downstream, was in a different position. Her mill sat on rented land, her boiler was newer, and ripping it out made no financial sense. To meet the cap on her own she would have to cut production and lay off workers — an expensive, painful cut.

This is the first thing the story teaches: **the same rule meets two firms in two different situations, and the cheap cut and the expensive cut are not at the same place.**

One evening Veasna did something the old rivalry would never have allowed: he offered to sell Phally his spare permits. Phally bought them. She kept her workers, kept her boiler, and paid Veasna for the right to emit. Veasna pocketed money for cuts he had been happy to make anyway. The town's total smoke fell to exactly the cap — no more — because the cap was fixed and the permits were finite.

This is the second thing the story teaches: **trading let the cheap cutter cut deeply and the costly cutter pay instead, so the whole district hit its limit at the lowest total cost, with no official deciding who should cut.**

But there was a third thing, and Phally felt it before she could name it. Her mill kept emitting, and it was her downstream neighbourhood — the poorer side of town — that kept breathing it. The district total was met. The air over the poor quarter was not much better. The cap had controlled the total; it had not controlled the *where*.

She raised this at the next district meeting, and an economist visiting from Phnom Penh nodded slowly. "That," he said, "is the hardest part. The market finds the cheapest tonnes to cut, not the most harmful places to cut. A good scheme sets the cap *and* watches where the pollution lands."

Veasna, sitting across from his old rival for the first time in years, surprised them both. "Then sell me fewer permits next year," he said to no one in particular. "Tighten the cap. I'll cut more — it's cheap for me anyway." Phally almost smiled. The rivalry, it turned out, made better neighbours than enemies once the permits gave them a reason to talk.
