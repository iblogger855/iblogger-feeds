# Onboarding and Ramp-Up Phrases

**Tags:** #english #idioms #onboarding #learning #communication  
**Read Time:** ~4 min

> Your first weeks on a team are full of phrases that assume you already know the culture. Here's what people actually mean when they say these things to you.

---

## "Hit the ground running"

Start working productively immediately — no slow warm-up period.

```
"We need someone who can hit the ground running — the sprint starts Monday."
"I was able to hit the ground running because the documentation was excellent."
```

---

## "Getting up to speed" / "Up to speed"

Reaching the level of knowledge needed to contribute effectively.

```
"Give yourself two weeks to get up to speed on the codebase."
"Can you bring me up to speed on what happened during the outage?"
"Once you're up to speed, you'll be the one leading this feature."
```

---

## "Drink from a firehose"

Receiving so much information so fast that it's overwhelming. A firehose delivers water at a pressure you can't drink from — it just hits you.

```
"The first week felt like drinking from a firehose — so much to absorb."
"Don't worry if you feel overwhelmed. Everyone drinks from a firehose at first."
```

What to say when it happens to you:
```
"I'm still drinking from a firehose a bit — could you point me to the best 
 place to start with the auth flow?"
```

---

## "Shadow"

Watch someone else do their work to learn how it's done — without doing it yourself yet.

```
"Shadow Sophea for the first on-call shift so you understand the runbooks."
"Can I shadow you during the client call to see how you handle requirements?"
```

---

## "Pair on it"

Work together on the same task at the same time — one person drives (types), the other navigates (thinks and advises).

```
"I'll pair on it with you — two sets of eyes will find it faster."
"Want to pair on this? I've done this integration before."
```

---

## "Onboard"

The process of introducing a new person (or system) to how things work.

```
"We're onboarding two new engineers this sprint — please be patient with questions."
"Who's onboarding the new payment provider?"
```

Variants: `offboard` = the process of removing access and wrapping up when someone leaves.

---

## "Deep end"

Being put in a challenging situation before you feel fully ready. From "thrown in the deep end" — a swimming pool analogy.

```
"Sorry to throw you in the deep end, but this is the fastest way to learn."
"I was thrown in the deep end on my second day — P1 incident during lunch."
```

---

## "Getting your bearings"

Figuring out how things work, where things are, who to talk to. From navigation — finding your direction.

```
"Take the first week just to get your bearings — no pressure to ship yet."
"I'm still getting my bearings — who owns the payments service?"
```

---

## "Tribal knowledge"

Information that exists only in people's heads — not written down anywhere. You only learn it by asking the right person.

```
"That's tribal knowledge — it's not documented anywhere. Ask Makara."
"We have too much tribal knowledge on this team. That's why we write ADRs."
```

---

## "Bus factor"

How many people could be lost before the project is in serious trouble. A bus factor of 1 means one person knows everything. (See also: [Engineering Slang](./03-engineering-slang.md))

```
"The bus factor on this service is 1 — only Dara knows how it works.
 We need to document it and cross-train."
```

---

## "Ramp up" / "Ramp-up period"

The time it takes to become fully productive in a new role or on a new project.

```
"Budget 4–6 weeks for ramp-up before you're fully productive."
"The ramp-up was faster because the docs were thorough."
```
