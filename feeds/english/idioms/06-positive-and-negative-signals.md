# Reading Between the Lines — Positive and Negative Signals

**Tags:** #english #idioms #communication #subtext  
**Read Time:** ~5 min

> In English-speaking work culture, people often don't say exactly what they mean. They soften bad news, imply disagreement, and express approval indirectly. This guide helps you decode what's really being said.

---

## When "Interesting" Means Something Else

In everyday English, "interesting" is positive. In work settings, it often signals polite disapproval.

| They say | They often mean |
|:---------|:----------------|
| `"That's interesting."` | "I'm not sure about this." |
| `"Interesting approach."` | "I wouldn't have done it this way." |
| `"That's certainly one way to do it."` | "I don't think that's the right way." |
| `"I see what you're going for."` | "I don't think it's working." |
| `"I'll take that under advisement."` | "I'm probably not going to do that." |

---

## Positive Signals

These phrases mean genuine approval:

| Phrase | Meaning |
|:-------|:--------|
| `"LGTM"` | Approved, ready to merge |
| `"Nice work"` / `"Great job"` | Genuine appreciation |
| `"This is exactly what we needed."` | Strong approval |
| `"I wish I'd thought of this."` | Impressed |
| `"Shipping this."` | Approved and moving forward |
| `"No comments — looks good."` | Genuinely clean PR |

---

## Negative Signals (Politely Expressed)

These sound neutral or even positive but carry negative meaning:

| They say | They mean |
|:---------|:---------|
| `"It might be worth revisiting this."` | I think this is wrong. |
| `"I have a few thoughts on this."` | I have significant concerns. |
| `"I'm not sure this is quite right."` | I'm pretty sure this is wrong. |
| `"This is a brave choice."` | This is a risky decision I would not have made. |
| `"Can you walk me through your thinking?"` | I don't understand why you did this. |
| `"I wonder if there might be a better way."` | There is definitely a better way. |
| `"This might need a bit more work."` | This is not ready. |
| `"We should probably discuss this."` | There's a problem here that we need to address. |

---

## Approval-with-Doubt Signals

These signal conditional or reluctant agreement:

| They say | They mean |
|:---------|:---------|
| `"I can live with this."` | I don't love it, but I won't block it. |
| `"That works for now."` | I expect we'll revisit this. |
| `"I'll defer to your judgment on this."` | I disagree but I'm not going to fight it. |
| `"If that's what the team wants."` | I wouldn't choose this. |
| `"Fair enough."` | I accept this point, but I'm not fully convinced. |

---

## Urgency Signals

Sometimes urgency is implied rather than stated:

| They say | They mean |
|:---------|:---------|
| `"Just checking in on this."` | This is overdue and I'm concerned. |
| `"Any updates on this?"` | This should have been done already. |
| `"Wanted to make sure this didn't get lost."` | I sent this before and got no response. |
| `"When do you think you'll have a chance to look at this?"` | Please look at this now. |
| `"No rush, whenever you have a moment."` | Actually, this is somewhat urgent. |

---

## Feedback Signals

In code review and feedback:

| They say | They mean |
|:---------|:---------|
| `"nit"` | Minor — optional to fix |
| `"just a thought"` | Not blocking, but worth considering |
| `"might want to"` | I think you should |
| `"could potentially"` | Will almost certainly |
| `"this might cause issues"` | This will cause issues |
| `"I'd be curious to see tests for this"` | There should be tests for this |
| `"happy to discuss"` | I disagree and want to talk about it |

---

## How to Respond to Implied Criticism

When someone is clearly unhappy but not saying so directly, bring it into the open:

```
"It sounds like you have some concerns about this approach — 
 could you share them? I'd rather address them now."

"I notice some hesitation — is there something about this 
 that isn't working for you?"
```

This is more effective than waiting for polite non-answers to become a real problem.
